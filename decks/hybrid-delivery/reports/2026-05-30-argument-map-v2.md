## 1. Core Claim (revised)

**As restated for v1.1.0:**
Human+AI hybrid teams produce meaningfully superior outcomes for the generative, iterative knowledge work of delivery teams when the collaboration is structured — but the advantage is not automatic; iteration managers who learn to architect centaur collaboration, select and govern human-in-the-loop patterns for their team's workflows, and own risk at the human-AI boundary will define what delivery leadership looks like for the next five years, while those who do not will find the role bypassed around them rather than dissolved.

**Falsifiable assertion:**
Structured human+AI collaboration produces measurably superior outcomes for delivery-type knowledge work, and the IM/DM who develops the specific capabilities of workflow architecture, HITL pattern governance, and human-AI risk ownership will remain the irreplaceable human at the boundary — while those who develop only AI-generation skills without governance and accountability skills will find the structural value of the role absorbed by adjacent roles.

The compound structure (performance claim + role claim) is unchanged from v1. The first half remains partially supported. The second half is materially stronger in v1.1.0 because the bypass framing is a structural claim about skill differentiation rather than a speculative claim about role elimination.

## 3. Toulmin Decomposition of the Five Load-Bearing Claims (v1.1.0)

### Claim 1: Human+AI outperforms for delivery-type work under structured conditions

| Toulmin element | Content |
|---|---|
| Claim | For generative, iterative knowledge work, human+AI under structured conditions produces meaningfully better outcomes than either solo condition |
| Data | BCG RCT (758 consultants, Organization Science 2026): 12.2% more tasks, 25.1% faster, 40% quality uplift. P&G RCT (776 professionals, NBER 2025): 3x more likely to produce top-10% solutions. Kasparov 2010/2023: "weak human + machine + better process" outperforms strong computer and strong human + inferior process. arXiv centaur programmer paper: human-AI collaboration in software development outperforms AI working independently. |
| Warrant | Consulting, product development, and software work share the generative, iterative cognitive structure of delivery management work — therefore findings generalize conditionally |
| Backing | BCG and P&G studies used complex professional populations in iterative knowledge-creation tasks; centaur programmer paper explicitly bridges to software development teams |
| Qualifier | Only for generative, iterative knowledge work; on decision/classification tasks, Malone et al. shows average human+AI does NOT outperform best solo performer — the claim is conditional |
| Rebuttal | Malone et al. (Nature Human Behaviour 2024, 106 studies): on average, human+AI does not beat best solo performer — surfaced and resolved by the deck's scope to generative task type |

**Gap assessment (v1.1.0):**
G1 from v1 (overstatement via "consistently outperform") is resolved by the conditional framing. The centaur Kasparov citation strengthens the backing with a primary-source named concept. The warrant (consulting/product development generalizes to delivery management) remains unstated as an explicit argument — it is now better supported by the centaur programmer paper's software bridge, but the deck still needs to state the generalization, not merely imply it.

**Severity: MINOR** — the fatal gap is closed; a residual warrant gap remains but is significantly smaller than in v1. The conditional qualifier must appear on the slide or in speaker notes.

**Severity: MINOR** — materially stronger in v1.1.0. The confidence grade transition from Arc 2 to Arc 3 should still be acknowledged in speaker notes.

### Claim 3: The IM/DM is the natural locus for hybrid team architecture (HITL selector)

| Toulmin element | Content |
|---|---|
| Claim | The IM/DM role is the right human to own HITL pattern selection, centaur composition, approval gate design, and governance at the human-AI boundary |
| Data | DEV Community HITL article: "Relying on the model to correctly judge severity is itself a high-severity mistake" — the workflow designer makes the classification, not the agent. LangGraph/Anthropic documentation: HITL oversight mode selection is a design decision external to the agent. Anthropic Trustworthy Agents: agents already better calibrated than humans at knowing when to pause — the IM/DM's job is designing where those pauses land. McKinsey MGI: manager role shifts to orchestrating people, agents, and robots. |
| Warrant | (1) HITL pattern selection requires knowledge of workflow consequence, reversibility, and organizational risk tolerance — all of which the IM/DM owns by existing accountabilities. (2) The three HITL patterns each require IM/DM-specific governance decisions (DoD gates, backlog stewardship, escalation thresholds) that no adjacent role has standing to make. |
| Backing | The DEV Community article's 2x2 matrix (consequence × reversibility) maps directly onto delivery risk assessment frameworks the IM/DM already applies; Anthropic's plan-mode framing matches the IM/DM's sprint planning function |
| Qualifier | No controlled study has directly measured IM/DM outcomes before and after HITL pattern implementation; the skill-mapping argument is structural, not experimentally validated |
| Rebuttal | Role fragmentation (tech lead + PM + AI governance specialist) is explicitly named as the fork-in-the-road alternative; the deck argues evolution is better for the organization — not that fragmentation is impossible |

**Gap assessment (v1.1.0):**

The residual gap: the warrant still rests on structural alignment between IM/DM skills and HITL requirements — this is a logical mapping exercise, not empirical evidence. The claim that the IM/DM is the natural locus rather than (say) a tech lead or a dedicated AI governance role is supported by functional argument but not by comparative evidence. For an audience that includes both IMs and sceptics, this is where pushback will concentrate.

**Severity: SIGNIFICANT** — the mechanism is now argued, which is materially better than v1. But the claim "the IM/DM specifically, not the tech lead" requires explicit comparative argumentation that is not yet in the deck. The fork-in-the-road slide names the fragmentation alternative but the argument for why evolution beats fragmentation for the organization is still light.

### Claim 4: Governance failure is real, grounded in documented incidents, and the IM/DM owns the prevention design

| Toulmin element | Content |
|---|---|
| Claim | Ungoverned agentic workflows produce documented operational failures; the IM/DM who designs and enforces approval gates, HITL patterns, and context constraints owns the prevention architecture |
| Data | Replit July 2025: agent deletes production database during code freeze — missing approval gate at infrastructure level (High confidence — Fortune, The Register, AI Incident Database). Meta December 2024: agent posts sensitive data publicly without authorization — context leak, missing authorization check (High confidence — multi-source press attribution). Gartner May 2026: 40% of agents will be decommissioned due to governance gaps (Medium-High — press release accessible). Gartner: binary governance failure modes — over-restriction creates shadow AI; under-restriction creates incidents. |
| Warrant | If documented failures result from absent approval gates and missing authorization checks, and if the HITL pattern taxonomy prescribes that the workflow designer — not the agent — must encode these controls, then the role that owns workflow governance also owns failure prevention |
| Backing | Anthropic Trustworthy Agents: agents cannot question whether they have been given the right direction; they cannot design workflows reflecting actual organizational needs. DEV Community: "You encode this classification in your agent's action manifest, not in the agent's prompt." |
| Qualifier | The two fully documented incidents (Replit, Meta) occurred in engineering/technical team contexts — not specifically in delivery team governance contexts. The inference to delivery teams is structural, not directly evidenced. |
| Rebuttal | Counter-claim: governance is an IT or security function, not a delivery function. The deck's response: the failure modes are workflow design failures, not IT security failures — the agent had legitimate access; the governance failure was the absence of designed approval gates in the workflow, not a breach of perimeter security. |

**Gap assessment (v1.1.0):**
This is a substantial upgrade from v1's speculative cost slide. The documented incidents provide high-confidence evidence that governance failure modes are real. The Gartner institutional framing provides a credible prediction about scale. The qualifier is important and must not be suppressed: both documented incidents are in engineering/technical team contexts. The argument that these failure modes apply equally to delivery team governance is logical but not yet evidenced by a delivery-specific incident. The deck should acknowledge this scope in speaker notes.

The old Claim 4 (AI governance as durable career moat) is now split: the skill-development framing is what remains; the "career moat" language is removed per Change 5. This resolves G6 from v1 (overstatement / missing qualifier on "durable"). The governance claim is now appropriately scoped to "this is a real and growing skill surface" rather than "this is your personal career moat."

**Severity: SIGNIFICANT** — high-confidence incident evidence is a genuine upgrade. The qualifier (incidents occurred in engineering contexts; the delivery team application is inferential) must not be omitted in speaker notes. The counter-claim (governance is IT's job) also needs explicit handling in the deck or appendix.

The squad capability gap is real and well-evidenced directionally.

The new logical gap introduced here: the argument connects well from "gap exists" to "someone must close it" but does not explicitly argue why the IM/DM is the agent of change rather than engineering leadership or a central CoE. This is G-NEW-1 in the gap register — not fatal because the HITL pattern selector argument in Claim 3 provides an implicit mechanism, but it needs to be connected explicitly in the deck.

The credential framing (skill development, not career moat) appropriately resolves G6 from v1 and avoids the overstatement. G7 from v1 (non-sequitur between governance argument and certifications) is substantially resolved by the framing of credentials as a named part of daily practice development rather than a career moat add-on.

**Severity: SIGNIFICANT (G-NEW-1)** — the bottom-up transformation argument is well-supported but the specific claim "IM/DM as the transformation vector" needs the connection to the HITL selector argument made explicit. Without it, an audience member can correctly ask: "why not the engineering manager?"

## 5. Summary Assessment of the Five Structural Changes

### Change 1: Drop cost slide; replace with governance failure modes

**Logical effect:** Net positive. The cost slide's evidence was directionally credible but sourced to a single practitioner analysis. The two documented incidents (Replit, Meta) are high-confidence primary sources. The replacement strengthens the evidentiary foundation of the governance arc substantially.

**Residual issue:** The failure modes occurred in engineering deployment contexts. The delivery team governance application must be explicitly argued (or the speaker notes must acknowledge the scope). G-NEW-3.

### Change 2: Add centaur teams + HITL patterns

**Logical effect:** Major positive. This is the single most impactful structural change. It provides:
- A named concept (centaur) with primary-source backing (Kasparov 2010/2023, arXiv programmer paper)
- An explicit mechanism for the "natural locus" claim (HITL pattern selector — the IM/DM makes the pattern assignment, not the agent)
- A taxonomy with engineering-grade documentation (LangGraph, Anthropic, DEV Community)
- Three specific IM/DM accountability decisions (DoD gate design, task backlog stewardship, escalation threshold setting) that directly answer "what does the IM/DM actually do differently?"

**Residual issue:** G-NEW-2 — the centaur framing implies IM/DM will develop sufficient AI literacy to evaluate prompts, tools, and models. The expanded Arc 4 must argue this is learnable, not simply list the capabilities.

### Change 3: Reframe dissolution to bypass; expand Arc 4 mastery specifics

**Logical effect:** Net positive. The bypass framing is logically more precise than dissolution — it makes a structural claim (those who develop mastery become the conduit; those who don't are bypassed) rather than an empirical claim (roles are being eliminated) for which there was no IM/DM-specific evidence. G2 is substantially reduced from Fatal to Significant (residual).

**Residual issue:** The expanded Arc 4 mastery specifics (evaluating prompts, tool usage governance, model evaluation, agent enablement) need to be connected explicitly to the centaur steering function — otherwise they read as a capability catalogue, which was the original G7 problem.

### Change 4: Expand leveling-up arc from 1 to 3-4 slides; bottom-up emphasis

**Logical effect:** Net positive for coverage; introduces G-NEW-1. The squad capability gap is now well-evidenced (Stack Overflow, DORA, arXiv longitudinal study). The bottom-up transformation claim is well-supported by the Salesforce case and champion model evidence. The introduction of the IM/DM specifically as the transformation vector, however, is a new claim that requires explicit logical connection to the HITL selector argument. The expanded section must not become a catalogue of practices without the governing warrant.

**Residual issue:** G-NEW-1 — the argument must connect "IM/DM develops mastery through daily practice" to "IM/DM is the right agent of team transformation" explicitly, or the audience can correctly ask "why not the engineering manager?"

### Change 5: Remove salary/career moat language

**Logical effect:** Net positive. G6 (overstatement on "durable career moat") is fully closed. The governance skill surface is now appropriately framed as genuinely valuable without the overstatement. No new logical gaps introduced.
