# Adversarial Scorecard — Hybrid Delivery Deck v1.1.0

**Reviewer:** Vera, Adversarial Critic
**Date:** 2026-05-30
**Deck:** The Iteration Manager in the Age of Agents
**Version:** v1.1.0
**Prior version score:** 5.0 / 10

---

## Adversarial Score: 6.5 / 10

**Score rationale:** v1.1.0 is a materially stronger deck than v1.0.0. The two most damaging changes are both improvements: the $110K token cost figure has been replaced with named, multi-source-verified governance incidents (Replit, Meta), closing the single biggest credibility hole in the prior version; and the Centaur / HITL slides (14–15) provide the first substantive structural defense of the IM-as-natural-locus claim, moving it from bare assertion toward argued position. The bypass framing (Slides 10–11) is an improvement over "dissolution" but it creates a new attack surface: the bypass mechanism is still structural inference with no empirical measurement behind it, and a well-prepared opponent who has read the Malone study will now note that the deck's own evidence base (decision task degradation) applies directly to the governance decisions the IM is being asked to own. The governance failure incidents work as urgency evidence but are both engineering-context incidents; the deck acknowledges this in speaker notes but the main slide body does not, leaving a gap the audience will notice. Critical 2 (natural locus) is partially resolved but not closed: the structural case for IM over tech lead still rests on a role-boundary argument that is asserted, not demonstrated by evidence that IMs are actually winning this contest. The score reflects a deck that has done genuine analytical work, removed its worst vulnerability, and added real structural content — but still leaves the presenter exposed to two critical attacks and two significant attacks that a hostile analyst will open with.

## Steelmanned PoV

The deck's strongest version of its argument is this: the historical record from Wave 1 (2020–2024) demonstrates that delivery roles defined by ceremony facilitation collapsed under ROI accountability pressure — the enrollment collapse from 49% to under 5% in four years is not contested. The structural dynamic in Wave 2 is related but distinct: agentic tools can now perform the transactional delivery work (story drafting, sprint tracking, dependency mapping, status reporting) at adequate quality, creating a substitution pressure on exactly the roles that failed to own delivery accountability in Wave 1. The roles that survived Wave 1 did so by owning delivery outcomes. The deck's argument is that the same move is available and time-bounded in Wave 2: an IM or DM who claims the human-AI governance boundary — who designs the HITL patterns, owns the approval gates, and takes accountability for what agents can generate but cannot decide — occupies a position that is organizationally legible, value-attributable, and not currently contested by a single obvious competitor. The Centaur model (Kasparov, NYRB 2010; arXiv 2304.11172) provides a primary-sourced structural model for why process design quality — not raw capability on either side — is the performance variable. The HITL taxonomy (LangGraph, Anthropic Trustworthy Agents, DEV Community) provides the engineering-grade taxonomy that maps governance decisions to IM/DM accountability. The Replit and Meta incidents provide documented evidence that governance gaps at the human-agent boundary produce consequential failures. The deck does not over-claim permanence: Slide 13 states explicitly that the boundary is not permanent and the IM's job is to own it now. For an audience of IMs and Delivery Managers who have watched a prior role category contract around them, this is a structurally coherent, evidence-grounded argument for a specific career move.

## Motivated Opponent Profile

**Primary opponent:** A tech lead or engineering manager at the same organization as the audience IM/DM, who has read the same McKinsey and DORA data, is already running agentic workflows, and believes they are — and functionally are — the person currently making the governance decisions the deck says the IM should own. This person does not need to be hostile to the IM; they simply need to accurately describe their current operating reality to neutralize the deck's "nobody's governing this" claim. They will note: (a) they already own the HITL pattern selection because they configure the agent systems; (b) the three HITL patterns on Slide 15 are engineering configuration decisions, not delivery management decisions; (c) the DEV Community source the deck's speaker notes cite explicitly frames HITL classification as something you "encode in the agent's action manifest" — which is an engineering artifact, not a delivery process artifact; and (d) the Malone meta-analysis that the deck correctly surfaces (Slide 6) documents performance degradation on decision tasks, which is exactly what the deck is now asking IMs to own.

**Secondary opponent:** A governance or compliance specialist at a regulated-industry organization who will note that in their domain, approval gates and risk thresholds at the human-agent boundary are owned by risk management and compliance functions, not delivery managers — and that the incidents cited (Replit, Meta) were both resolved by engineering and security teams, not by IMs or DMs.

## Severity-Ranked Attack Log

---

### ATTACK 1 — The HITL pattern governance claim collapses against the deck's own Malone counter-evidence

**Severity: Critical**

**The attack in full:**

The deck's core role evolution argument — assembled across Slides 13, 14, 15, 17, and 22 — positions the IM as the decision-maker for which HITL pattern governs which work, for risk threshold design, and for approval gate ownership. Slide 17 names these as the IM's three survival capabilities: workflow architecture, agentic governance, and "risk at the boundary." Slide 15 maps three specific HITL patterns to concrete IM accountability decisions.

The structural problem is that every one of these accountability items — selecting HITL patterns per work type, designing escalation thresholds, setting approval gates, making production calls — is a *decision task*, not a generative task. The deck's own Slide 6, citing Malone et al. (Nature Human Behaviour 2024), establishes the core conditional: human+AI underperforms the best solo performer on decision and classification tasks. The Malone reconciliation presented on Slide 27 (appendix) confirms: 90% of studies in the meta-analysis tested decision and classification tasks — exactly the conditions where AI degrades human performance.

The deck uses the Malone finding to scope down the performance evidence for AI augmentation to generative work only. But it does not apply the same conditional to the IM's new governance accountabilities. An IM using AI to help them make approval gate decisions, select HITL patterns, or evaluate agent risk thresholds is doing precisely what Malone says humans get worse at when AI is in the loop. The deck cannot simultaneously present Malone as the honest counter-evidence for AI augmentation *and* ask the audience to use AI-assisted governance as the career survival move without addressing this conflict.

A well-prepared opponent who has read the Malone study will raise this in Q&A. The deck provides no mechanism for distinguishing which parts of the IM's evolved role are decision tasks (where Malone applies) versus generative tasks (where BCG/P&G applies). The speaker notes acknowledge the conditional framing but the main deck body does not carry this distinction into the evolved role slides.

**Required resolution:** The deck must either (a) demonstrate that the IM's governance accountabilities are generative-iterative in character — not discrete decision tasks — and thus fall outside the Malone conditional, or (b) explicitly address how the IM makes governance decisions *without* relying on AI augmentation for the decision layer, which would also need to be reconciled with the centaur framing.

### ATTACK 2 — The natural locus claim survives partial defense but the tech lead's competing claim remains unaddressed

**Severity: Critical**

**The attack in full:**

v1.1.0's most substantive improvement is the addition of Slides 14 and 15. The Centaur model (Slide 14) provides a named, primary-sourced structural concept for human+AI collaboration. The HITL patterns (Slide 15) map three specific governance decisions to IM accountability: define review gates in the Definition of Done, maintain the task backlog agents pull from, set escalation thresholds.

However, the structural defense has a foundational gap that v1.1.0 does not close. The speaker notes on Slide 15 make the argument explicitly: "The tech lead designs the agent. The IM/DM decides which human-in-the-loop pattern governs how that agent participates in the team's delivery system. That distinction is the structural argument for why the IM/DM is the natural locus — they make the governance decisions the tech lead does not have standing to make by role."

This argument depends entirely on an organizational boundary — the tech lead does not have standing to make governance decisions by role — that is asserted, not established. In most organizations in 2026, the tech lead who configured the agentic workflow *de facto* made all three of the HITL governance decisions on Slide 15 when they configured the system:

- They encoded the review gates in the CI/CD pipeline or the agent's action manifest (not in the DoD)
- They maintained the task queue agents pull from (it lives in their Jira board or their agent orchestrator, not in an IM-owned backlog)
- They set the escalation thresholds as alert rules in their monitoring system

The DEV Community source the deck's own speaker notes cite makes the point that undercuts the argument: "You encode this classification in your agent's action manifest, not in the agent's prompt." The action manifest is an engineering artifact. The "natural standing" argument requires the IM to have standing to make decisions that are currently implemented in engineering artifacts owned by the tech lead.

Slide 22 (maturity locator) acknowledges that engineers are using "multiple HITL patterns in use (informally)" — but this means the HITL governance decisions have already been made informally by the engineering team. The IM's task, if they enter the space, is not to occupy a vacant governance role but to formalize and take ownership of decisions already embedded in technical systems. That is a much harder organizational claim than "nobody's governing this." It requires an argument for why the IM should take back something already occupied, not simply fill a vacuum.

**Required resolution:** The deck must address the competitive landscape directly. The argument for IM as natural locus must either (a) demonstrate why formalized delivery-layer governance is superior to the technically-implemented informal governance already in place, and why that argument gives the IM standing to claim it over the tech lead's de facto ownership; or (b) acknowledge that the IM is not filling a vacuum but challenging an existing informal arrangement, and provide evidence that this challenge is succeeding in real organizations.

### ATTACK 3 — The governance failure incidents do not demonstrate delivery management failure

**Severity: Significant**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 19 replaces the old $110K token cost slide (v1.0.0 Critical Attack 1) with three documented governance failure incidents: Replit July 2025, Meta December 2024, and a general "context leak pattern." The sourcing is materially stronger than the prior version: Replit is logged in the AI Incident Database (#1152), covered by Fortune and The Register, and acknowledged by the company. Meta is reported by multiple technology press sources and carries a Sev 1 severity classification. This is a genuine improvement.

The residual attack is this: both incidents occurred in engineering and technical deployment contexts, not in delivery team governance contexts. The Replit incident was an engineering agent (a "vibe coding" agent) acting without infrastructure-level permission controls — a DevOps and security failure. The Meta incident was an internal technical agent without an authorization check — a security engineering failure. In both cases, the failures were identified, contained, and remediated by engineering and security teams. No delivery manager or iteration manager is mentioned in any of the source coverage.

The speaker notes on Slide 19 acknowledge this directly: "Both documented incidents occurred in engineering and technical deployment contexts, not specifically in delivery team governance contexts. The inference to delivery teams is structural." This is an honest disclosure — but it is in the speaker notes, not on the slide. The main slide body presents the incidents under the heading "No owner at the boundary. Here is what that looks like." A skeptical audience member will ask: who was the missing "owner at the boundary" in the Replit and Meta incidents? The answer the evidence supports is: the engineering team lacked infrastructure-level permission controls and authorization checks. The answer the deck implies is: an IM/DM should have been governing these workflows. These are not the same claim.

The third item — "context leak pattern" — has no incident citation; its "source" is a design implication. It is not a documented case, it is advice. Placed alongside two cited incidents, it acquires the visual weight of a third confirmed failure without the evidential basis.

**Required resolution:** The deck must either (a) surface the "inference to delivery teams is structural" qualifier on the main slide body (not only in speaker notes), and make the mechanism of that inference explicit — how specifically would IM/DM governance have prevented either incident; or (b) find and use a documented governance failure that occurred specifically in a delivery management context, where the missing governance owner was a delivery role rather than a security or engineering role.

### ATTACK 4 — The bypass framing is more defensible than dissolution but the causal mechanism still rests on a single analogy

**Severity: Significant**

**The attack in full:**

Slides 10 and 11 replace the v1.0.0 dissolution framing with bypass language: "The roles that didn't, got routed around" and "The automation is here. The governance gap is where you get bypassed." The speaker notes on Slide 11 make the mechanism explicit: "if nobody governs the right column, the tech lead fills it de facto because they understand the tooling. That is not elimination of the IM/DM role — it is quiet erosion of standing."

This is a meaningful improvement over v1.0.0. Bypass is empirically more accurate than dissolution: Wave 1 saw SM roles become irrelevant through organizational routing-around, not formal elimination. The mechanism is plausible and recognizable.

The residual attack: the bypass claim for Wave 2 is still argued entirely by analogy from Wave 1, with no independent evidence that the bypassing mechanism is currently occurring in organizations running agentic workflows. Slide 11's right column ("Nobody's governing this") is a claim about the current state. But the maturity locator on Slide 22 acknowledges that engineers are already running HITL patterns informally. If informal governance is already in place — even without IM/DM involvement — then the bypass has already begun, and the deck's framing of the governance gap as open and unoccupied is already out of date. The argument would need to shift from "occupy an empty space" to "formalize what's currently informal and claim it" — which is a harder and different case.

The Slides 10–11 bypass framing also inherits the v1.0.0 Attack 4 residual: Wave 1 contraction began before generative AI had meaningful enterprise adoption (the research brief acknowledges this — "structural role contraction, not AI-caused contraction"). The deck presents bypass as the same mechanism in both waves, but Wave 1 was driven by ROI pressure under QT; Wave 2's proposed bypass mechanism is agent substitutability of IM/DM work. These are not the same structural force. An informed audience member will notice and press.

**Required resolution:** The deck must close the analogy gap. It needs either (a) empirical evidence — even a documented example or practitioner case study — of an IM/DM role being bypassed in an AI-enabled team in the current wave (not Wave 1); or (b) an explicit acknowledgment that the bypass claim for Wave 2 is a structural prediction from the Wave 1 analogy, not a documented current pattern, which would require the confidence qualifier to be on the main slide rather than buried in notes.

### ATTACK 5 — The squad gap slide attributes IM-vs-engineer differential to AI4Agile data it does not contain

**Severity: Significant**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 21 presents "AI4Agile 2026 (n=289): IM/DM cohort at chatbot-level · engineering teams agentic" as a secondary stat citation. The v2 research brief (Finding 3) is explicit that the AI4Agile survey does not separately segment IM/DM respondents from the broader agile practitioner population: "no single study directly measures 'engineer vs IM/DM AI adoption' in the same survey." The IM/DM-specific claim is assembled from contrasting two surveys with different sampling frames — Stack Overflow (developers) and AI4Agile (agile practitioners broadly) — not from within-survey segmentation.

The slide's citation "AI4Agile 2026 (n=289): IM/DM cohort at chatbot-level" implies that AI4Agile specifically measured the IM/DM cohort's adoption level. It did not. The research brief's own caveat states: "The directional claim — engineers are doing more, going deeper, and using more agentic tooling than their delivery managers — is well-supported. The precise magnitude is not." The main slide body does not carry this caveat. For a technically literate audience that includes people who run surveys, the claim "IM/DM cohort at chatbot-level" sourced to AI4Agile is a misattribution — AI4Agile did not measure the IM/DM cohort separately.

This is worth noting separately from the broader squad gap framing (which is directionally sound) because the misattribution could be called out specifically and will damage credibility on the whole slide if it is.

**Required resolution:** The AI4Agile citation on Slide 21 must accurately describe what the survey measured. The slide should note that the IM/DM-vs-engineer gap is assembled across two surveys with different sampling frames, not derived from a single within-survey comparison. The research brief's own language — "directional claim well-supported; precise magnitude not" — is the honest qualifier.

### ATTACK 6 — The Centaur model's chess-to-delivery-management transfer is not demonstrated

**Severity: Low**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 14 uses the Kasparov Centaur model (NYRB 2010; 2005 Freestyle Chess Championship) as the structural foundation for the human+AI collaboration argument. The citation is primary-sourced and credible. The arXiv centaur programmer paper (2304.11172) bridges the chess analogy to software development — coding tasks, not delivery management.

The gap: the deck uses "Weak human + machine + better process beat the grandmaster" as the foundational claim for the IM's role in process design, but the evidence for this claim applies to chess play and software coding. The transfer to delivery management — sprint planning, governance decisions, stakeholder communication, approval gate design — is not argued; it is assumed. A skeptical opponent will note that the centaur programmer paper (the closest evidence) covers developer tasks (code generation, code review) managed by a developer, not delivery management decisions managed by an IM. The deck needs the transfer to hold for the human/AI split table on Slide 14 ("Judgment / Accountability / Context / Stakeholder navigation / Final sign-off" as the human column) to be more than an assertion dressed in Kasparov's credibility.

This is Low severity because the model is plausible and the audience is unlikely to challenge the chess analogy directly. However, it is a gap a hostile analyst preparing a written counter-argument will identify.

**Required resolution:** Either (a) cite evidence that the process-design variable in centaur performance applies to delivery management contexts specifically — ideally a study of team-level coordination performance in human+AI delivery settings; or (b) explicitly frame the centaur analogy as a model drawn from adjacent domains (chess, software development) rather than a demonstrated finding in delivery management.

### ATTACK 7 — Slide 2's "jobs question is settled" retains its ambiguity from v1.0.0

**Severity: Low**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 2 — "The jobs question is settled. The structure question isn't." — is a carry-forward from v1.0.0 with no modification. The v1.0.0 Attack 6 (Significant) identified this as unsupported: the deck never demonstrates that the jobs question is settled; it asserts it as a rhetorical device to redirect the audience toward structure. The v2 research brief did not address this gap. The severity is downgraded from Significant to Low in this pass because the rest of the deck is now sufficiently substantive that the rhetorical opening is less exposed — but the logical tension remains.

The specific tension identified in v1.0.0 still holds: for an audience of IMs and Delivery Managers whose role category has been actively contracting (Wave 1 data, Slides 9–10), "the jobs question is settled" may read as dismissive of an ongoing and personal concern. The deck then immediately shows them Wave 1 elimination data. An audience member who interprets "settled" as "AI won't eliminate jobs" will experience cognitive dissonance when Slide 9 follows.

**Required resolution:** Clarify what "settled" means on Slide 2. The intended meaning — "the debate about whether AI changes your work is no longer productive; the productive question is structural" — is reasonable and defensible. The current wording implies a broader and unearned claim. A single qualifying phrase ("settled enough that we should be discussing structure, not debating displacement") would neutralize the objection without disrupting the rhetorical intent.

## Single Strongest Attack

The deck's evolved role argument — HITL pattern governance, approval gate design, risk threshold setting — asks the IM to own decision tasks; these are precisely the task type where the deck's own Malone counter-evidence (Slide 6) establishes that human+AI combinations underperform the best solo performer, a contradiction the deck does not address anywhere in its 32 slides.
