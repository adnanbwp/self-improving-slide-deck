## 3. Assessment of the Five Key Changes

### 3.1 Centaur Teams Slide (Slide 14) — Does it close G4 (natural locus gap)?

**Finding: SUBSTANTIALLY CLOSES G4. One structural residual remains.**

Slide 14 delivers the Kasparov "weak human + machine + better process" quotation with correct attribution (NYRB 2010 / 2005 Freestyle Championship). The human/AI column split is directly consistent with the Kasparov primary source and the arXiv centaur programmer paper. The key sentence — "Process design is the variable. That is your job." — makes an explicit ownership assignment rather than an implicit one.

The column split (Human: Judgment / Accountability / Context / Stakeholder navigation / Final sign-off; AI: Speed / Throughput / Pattern matching / Draft generation / Spec execution) is a structural claim about the division of cognitive labor. It is sourced to Kasparov at high confidence.

What the slide does NOT do: it does not argue why the IM/DM specifically is the human in the centaur pair, as distinct from the tech lead, engineering manager, or product owner. "Process design is your job" assumes the audience is an IM/DM audience — in a conference setting this is reasonable, but the logical gap is visible to anyone reasoning about whether this prescription applies to them.

**Verdict on G4:** G4 is substantially closed. The mechanism now exists. The comparative case (IM/DM vs. adjacent roles) is still implicit. G4 is reduced from Significant to Minor — but only because Slide 15 then provides the explicit IM/DM accountability mapping that completes the argument.

### 3.2 HITL Patterns Slide (Slide 15) — Structural mechanism or assertion?

**Finding: THIS IS A STRUCTURAL MECHANISM. It is the best new content in the deck.**

Slide 15 delivers three named patterns, each with:
- A use-case scope
- An explicit IM role stated in the slide body (not only in speaker notes)

The three IM role entries are:
- "IM role: define review gates in Definition of Done" (Pattern 01)
- "IM role: maintain task backlog agents pull from" (Pattern 02)
- "IM role: set escalation thresholds" (Pattern 03)

These are concrete, role-specific accountability decisions. They are not shared with the tech lead, PM, or product owner by existing role definitions. The slide heading — "You decide which pattern. The agent can't." — is a logically sound structural claim: the HITL pattern selection is a governance decision that requires organizational context the agent cannot evaluate.

The speaker notes correctly cite the DEV Community "Relying on the model to correctly judge severity is itself a high-severity mistake" framing, and the Anthropic plan-mode backing.

**Verdict on G-NEW-1 (partial):** Slide 15 provides the explicit mechanism connecting IM/DM accountability to the HITL decision. This is the strongest argument for why the IM/DM is the right role. However, the connection from Slide 15's mechanism to Slide 22's "You close the squad gap" claim is not drawn explicitly in slide body text. The argument that "IM/DM closes the squad gap because they are the HITL pattern selector" lives in Slide 22's speaker notes, not in its slide copy.

### 3.3 Bypass Framing (Slides 10, 11) — Structural claim or rhetorical softening?

**Finding: STRUCTURAL CLAIM. Language change is load-bearing and correct. One residual gap.**

Slide 10: "The roles that survived 2022 owned delivery. The roles that didn't, got routed around."
Slide 11 annotation: "The automation is here. The governance gap is where you get bypassed."

The shift from "facilitated it" (v1.0.0) to "got routed around" is the correct framing. Bypass is mechanistically different from dissolution: dissolution requires active elimination (headcount cut); bypass is progressive loss of standing as adjacent roles fill an accountability vacuum. The bypass mechanism is structurally grounded in Slides 11 and 12 — the governance gap between 84% AI adoption and 49% governance guardrails (Digital.ai / Slide 12) is exactly the vacuum described.

**Residual gap:** The bypass claim is the second half of G2-residual. The structural argument is well-made. But the claim that IM/DMs specifically (not product owners, engineering managers, or tech leads) are the ones who get bypassed if they do not govern the right column is a structural inference that the deck does not yet evidence with IM/DM-specific data. The Wolpers data (Slide 28) covers Scrum Masters. No data covers IM/DM headcount or standing changes in AI-era teams. This is a known gap; the bypass framing is better than the dissolution framing precisely because it does not require empirical evidence of role elimination — but it is still an analogical claim.

**Verdict on bypass framing:** Not rhetorical softening — it is a more precise structural claim. G2 is materially reduced. The residual is a Significant gap that cannot be closed without IM/DM-specific Wave 2 evidence.

### 3.4 Governance Failure Modes (Slide 19) — Is the Replit/Meta evidence used correctly?

**Finding: EVIDENCE IS USED CORRECTLY. One scope qualification gap that must not be suppressed.**

Slide 19 presents three failure modes:
1. Replit July 2025 — "Agent deleted production database during a code freeze. Missing infrastructure-level approval gate."
2. Meta December 2024 — "Agent posted sensitive user data to a public forum without authorization. 2-hour exposure. Sev 1."
3. Context leak pattern — design implication stated directly.

The failure modes are correctly characterized. The Replit incident root cause — "Missing infrastructure-level approval gate" — is exactly right per the source material. The Meta incident root cause — missing authorization check, context leak — is correctly stated. The Gartner 40% decommission prediction is used as an institutional-grade forward-looking statement, not a present fact, which is the correct use of predictive evidence.

The inference in the slide heading — "No owner at the boundary. Here is what that looks like." — is a structural claim: these incidents occurred because no one designed the approval gate. That is accurate. The conclusion implied — that an IM/DM who designs approval gates would prevent this class of failure — is structurally correct.

**Scope qualification:** Both named incidents occurred in engineering/technical deployment contexts (a vibe-coding user on Replit; a Meta internal engineering agent). Neither incident is a delivery management failure specifically. The inference that delivery teams face the same failure modes is logical — delivery agents operating without approval gates would produce the same pattern — but it is inferential, not evidenced by a delivery-specific incident. The speaker notes on Slide 19 correctly acknowledge this scope: "Both incidents occurred in engineering and technical deployment contexts, not specifically in delivery team governance contexts. The inference to delivery teams is structural." This is the correct handling. The gap is Minor because the scope is acknowledged in the deck's own notes.

However, the main slide body does not acknowledge the scope — it presents the incidents as straightforward evidence of what happens when "nobody owns the boundary," implying a universal claim. A technically sophisticated audience member may correctly note that both incidents are engineering team failures, not delivery management failures. The deck does not preempt this objection in the visible slide content.

**Verdict on G-NEW-3:** Correctly classified as Minor. The evidence is valid and used properly. The scope qualification should ideally appear on the face of the slide or at minimum in the annotation, not only in speaker notes.

### 3.5 Squad Capability Gap + Maturity Locator (Slides 21, 22) — Resource catalogue or earned argument?

**Finding: MIXED. Slide 21 earns its place. Slide 22 partially earns its place. The transition from gap to IM/DM-as-solution is not fully drawn.**

**Slide 21** is strong. The 441% DORA hero stat is concrete and sourced (DORA 2025). The secondary stats are from named primary sources with sample sizes. The arXiv longitudinal study finding — "individual AI adoption does not improve team coordination" — is the most important single line in the leveling-up arc because it provides peer-reviewed evidence that the squad gap problem is real and not self-correcting. The slide heading — "Your engineers are already running agents. The coordination isn't keeping up." — makes a specific, falsifiable claim. This is not a resource catalogue.

**Slide 22** (Maturity Locator) identifies a real and well-documented gap in slide copy. The compare layout — "Where your squad is" vs. "Where you are" — is logically valid given the evidence on Slide 21. The annotation — "The gap is the accountability. You close it from inside the team, not from a mandate." — is the correct logical conclusion if the IM/DM is the right transformation vector.

The gap: the annotation treats the IM/DM's role as established at this point in the deck. But the explicit comparative argument — "you close it, not the tech lead, because HITL pattern selection is your governance accountability" — appears only in the speaker notes. An audience member who did not track the Slide 15 mechanism through to Slide 22 will accept the "you close it" assertion without the warrant that makes it a logical claim rather than an appeal to the audience's professional self-interest.

**G-NEW-1 assessment:** G-NEW-1 is alive in the delivered HTML. The warrant connecting the squad capability gap (Slide 21) to the IM/DM-as-transformation-vector claim (Slide 22) is in speaker notes only. The logical bridge — "you are the HITL pattern selector, which is why the gap is yours to close, not the tech lead's" — needs to appear in slide-visible text for the argument to be fully self-contained.

## 4. Assessment of G-NEW-1 and G-NEW-2

### G-NEW-1: IM/DM vs. tech lead as transformation vector

**Status: OPEN. Severity: SIGNIFICANT.**

The argument that the IM/DM is the correct agent of team-level AI transformation — not the tech lead or engineering manager — depends on the chain: IM/DM owns HITL pattern selection (Slide 15) → HITL pattern selection is the governance mechanism for closing the squad gap (implied) → therefore IM/DM closes the squad gap (Slide 22). This chain exists in speaker notes but not in slide body text or annotation copy. A reader of the deck's visible content receives the premise (squad gap is real) and the conclusion (you close it) without the intermediate step that makes the conclusion follow from IM/DM role accountability specifically.

The Slide 20 speaker notes make the connection explicitly: "the IM/DM is the natural actor to move a team from L2 to L3 because the move requires governance decisions about HITL patterns, approval gate design, and escalation thresholds — all IM/DM accountabilities from Slide 15." This is exactly the warrant needed. But it lives in notes.

The Slide 22 speaker notes make it again: "the reason the IM/DM, not the tech lead or the engineering manager, closes this gap is the same structural reason they own the HITL pattern selection from Slide 15." Again, correct — and again, notes-only.

This is a meaningful gap in the deck's visible argument structure. The answer exists; it is not being surfaced where the audience can engage with it.

### G-NEW-2: Centaur framing implies learnable AI literacy — acquirability argued or listed?

**Status: PARTIALLY ADDRESSED. Severity: SIGNIFICANT (reduced).**

Slide 17 expands the capability list to include: "Prompt evaluation, tool usage governance, model evaluation on new releases, permission scoping, oversight gate design." The Slide 17 speaker notes note: "these skills develop in practice, not through a one-time training event." Slide 24 frames the development through daily practice over three time horizons (this week / this month / this year).

The acquirability argument is present but thin. The claim that prompt evaluation and model evaluation on new releases are learnable by a non-technical IM/DM through daily delivery practice is plausible but rests on practitioner prescription rather than evidence of practitioners having done so. No case study, cohort data, or reference to practitioner trajectories is provided.

The credential section (Slide 23) provides an organised track but states only that credentials "map to" capabilities — not that practitioners who completed them developed measurable capability. The v1.0.0 salary correlation (N-A gap from the prior scorecard) is correctly absent, but the credential-to-capability chain still lacks backing evidence.

The partial resolution: the framing is correct (daily practice, not one-time workshop; credentials as proof of practice, not source of capability). The gap that remains: the deck does not show that the centaur capabilities listed on Slide 17 are achievable within the leveling-up timeframe for a working IM/DM without deep technical background. This is not fatal because the claim is made tentatively (practitioner prescription, not empirical claim), but it is significant because it is the final logical requirement for the deck's conclusion to hold.

## 8. Score Rationale

**Score: 6.5 / 10**

The v1.1.0 delivery is a genuine structural improvement over v1.0.0. The two v1.0.0 fatal gaps are closed or substantially reduced. The centaur + HITL addition is the most significant improvement to the argument in any version of this deck — it converts the "natural locus" claim from an asserted position to an argued mechanism. The governance failure modes replacement is a meaningful upgrade in evidence quality. The bypass framing is more precise and more defensible than the dissolution framing.

**The score is 6.5 rather than 7 for four reasons:**

First, G-NEW-1 is an unresolved Significant gap in slide-visible text. The IM/DM-as-transformation-vector claim at Slide 22 is not supported by the warrant that justifies it (the HITL selector mechanism from Slide 15). The warrant exists in speaker notes — which an analytical audience member will not see. The deck's public argument has a missing step at precisely the moment the audience needs to decide whether to apply the argument to themselves.

Second, G-NEW-2 is a Significant gap that is not materially smaller than when predicted. The centaur capability list on Slide 17 is more specific than v1.0.0 but still rests on practitioner prescription rather than evidence that delivery managers can acquire these capabilities in the timeframe implied by the action path.

Third, the bypass claim (G2-residual) remains a Significant gap. The structural argument is better than the dissolution argument, but it still lacks IM/DM-specific evidence. The Wolpers Wave 1 data covers Scrum Masters, not Iteration Managers. An analytically demanding audience member — an IM/DM who is sceptical of the deck's premise — is entitled to ask for evidence specific to their role and will not find it.

Fourth, the argument map v2 predicted score of 7/10 assumed that G-NEW-1 and G-NEW-2 would be "sufficiently addressed" in the delivered HTML. They are addressed in speaker notes but not in slide-visible content. A logic audit must assess the argument the audience receives, not the argument the presenter delivers verbally using notes they may or may not read.

**The score matches v1.0.0 at 6.5 despite genuine structural improvements because the new slides introduce new load-bearing claims (IM/DM as transformation vector; centaur capabilities as learnable) that are not yet fully argued in slide-visible text. The deck has more to argue in v1.1.0 than in v1.0.0, and the increment in ambition is not yet matched by an increment in the visible argument structure.**
