# Narrative Scorecard — hybrid-delivery v2.0.0 (canonical)

## Mode: Critique

## Objective
Convince Iteration/Delivery Managers that they should OWN what agents generate but can't decide — the governance of the human-AI boundary — and run a squad audit this week.

## Inferred arc & purpose
**Man in a Hole** (comfort → knocked down by the agent/bypass gap → treasure found = the generate/decide boundary → climb = own governance → better place = the role evolves) — told as an expository **Convince + Motivate** argument rather than as a narrative. The arc is present but buried under stacked frameworks.

## Checks

### Hook — FAIL
- Evidence: The deck opens on a title (Slide 1) then a definitional bridge — Slide 2 "'Agent' stopped meaning a chatbot. Now it means something that acts" — followed by three teaching slides (ladder, chat-vs-agent, the loop) before the IM is given a reason to care. The audience's own stakes don't land until Slide 6 (441% PR-review spike) and the pivot question ("What happens when a team runs *with* AI?") is stranded at Slide 9. The strongest hook material arrives 6th–9th.
- Fix: Open on the stake, not the definition. Lead with the 441% coordination spike or the bypass threat ("the tech lead quietly absorbs the workflow while you run the same ceremonies as last year") as Slide 1–2, then teach what an agent is once they need to know. Move the Slide 9 pivot question to the top.

### One arc — FAIL
- Evidence: A Man-in-a-Hole spine exists, and the fork (Slide 18: fragmentation vs evolution) is its natural crisis→recovery. But the arc is interrupted twice: the upside/counter-evidence block (Slides 10–12, BCG/P&G vs Malone) pauses the IM's journey to litigate "does human+AI even work," and the back third stacks three separate frameworks — five collisions (19), VERDICT seven pillars (21), maturity ladder (23) — after the climax, so the deck reads as several mini-arguments in sequence rather than one climbing line.
- Fix: Commit to Man in a Hole explicitly. Make the fork (18) the crisis and the 12-month path (25) the "better place." Demote the framework stack: keep one governance framework on the main line, appendix the other two, and fold the human+AI-works reconciliation into a single evidence beat.

### Movie moment — PASS
- Evidence: Multiple concrete, visualisable moments: the 2005 Freestyle Chess Championship where amateurs + computers beat grandmasters (Slide 16), Replit's agent deleting a production database *during a declared code freeze* (Slide 22), Meta's agent posting user data to a public forum (Slide 22).
- Fix: n/a — protect these in any recut; the chess and code-freeze moments are the deck's most memorable beats.

### Rule of three — PASS
- Evidence: The load-bearing points are deliberately boiled to three or to a binary: the boundary is a clean generate-vs-decide split (Slide 15), the survival argument is three capabilities (Slide 20), the mechanism is three HITL patterns (Slide 17).
- Fix: n/a for the key points — but see Leave it out: the 5-rung ladder (3), 5 collisions (19), and 7 VERDICT pillars (21) pull against the three-discipline and should not compete with the three-capability payoff.

### Leave it out — FAIL
- Evidence: 32 slides, and the back third over-explains. Three frameworks each restate "govern the boundary" (collisions 19, VERDICT 21, maturity 23); the full BCG/P&G-vs-Malone reconciliation (10–12) plus Appendix A is defensive completeness that stalls the IM's arc; chat-vs-agent (4) and the agentic loop (5) are taught in full for an audience that mostly needs the stakes, not the mechanism.
- Fix: Cut the main line to ~18–20 body slides. Move the five collisions and one of {VERDICT detail, maturity ladder} to the appendix. Trust the audience to accept "human+AI works on generative work" from one RCT stat, and let a questioner pull the Malone reconciliation from the appendix.

### Concreteness — PASS
- Evidence: The 441% PR-review-time spike (Slide 6) is a vivid single detail an IM can check in their own sprint data — the speaker note even says "if PR review time increased in the last two sprints, that's an agent-in-the-loop signal." It stands for the whole coordination problem.
- Fix: n/a — this is the deck's best concrete anchor; consider promoting it to the opening.

### Payoff — PASS
- Evidence: The CTA (Slide 26) earns a real payoff line: "The capability gap closes on its own. The governance gap is yours to close." It ties the two ladders together and hands the audience the lever — not a flat summary.
- Fix: Protect it, but shorten the denouement before it (19–25) so the line lands with force instead of after fatigue.

### Trust — FAIL
- Evidence: The objective is a "back my judgment" ask, but authority rests almost entirely on citations (DORA, RCTs, named incidents) — Data-Detective trust, which is strong — while the *teller's* character and judgment are barely shown. There is no first-person moment of the presenter having governed a boundary himself. The team slide (27) models human-AI collaboration, but that is a light, indirect signal.
- Fix: Add one first-person "here's a boundary I owned" beat early — a workflow the presenter governed, the gate they designed, what it cost or caught. Show the judgment in action, don't only cite others' evidence for it.

## Ranked issues
1. **Leave it out** — 32 slides with three redundant governance frameworks and a full evidence-reconciliation on the main line bury the arc and fatigue the audience before the payoff. Highest-leverage cut.
2. **One arc** — the Man-in-a-Hole spine is real but interrupted by the human+AI-works detour and buried under the post-climax framework stack; the fork should be the crisis, not one slide among many.
3. **Hook** — the IM's stakes (441%, bypass threat) sit behind five slides of AI definition; the deck teaches before it grips.
4. **Trust** — for a back-my-judgment objective, the presenter's own boundary-governance is never shown in action; trust is borrowed from citations, not demonstrated.

## Verdict
Holds as one arc only loosely — **needs work**. The bones are strong: excellent concreteness, memorable movie moments, and a genuinely earned payoff line. But economy and sequencing are working against the story — the arc climbs, then stalls in a three-framework plateau before the CTA, and the hook grips too late. Priority for the next cycle: cut to ~18–20 body slides, lead with the stake, and add one first-person judgment beat. None of these require dropping a warrant Rex or Vera relies on — the cut material moves to the appendix, it does not disappear.
