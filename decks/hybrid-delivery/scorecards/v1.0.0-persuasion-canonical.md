## 1. Audience model

### Cognitive priors

IMs and DMs are delivery pattern-matchers. They navigate ambiguity by mapping it onto known delivery structures — sprints, ceremonies, risks, owners, gates. When a new stimulus arrives (this deck), they immediately ask: "where does this plug in?" and "who is accountable?" Their BS detector is calibrated to flag vague assertions. Claims without citations are dismissed as vendor positioning. Claims with citations are accepted provisionally until they can check the source.

### Values (real distribution — not a flat archetype)

The segment is not monolithic. Three sub-profiles coexist:

- **Pragmatic operators** (~50%): value stability, proven method, team protection. They adopted agile to reduce chaos, not to chase novelty. They are mildly threatened by the AI governance framing if it implies their current practice is deficient.
- **Capability builders** (~35%): value professional growth, credentialing, scope expansion. They survived 2022 by learning fast. They actively look for the next surface area to claim. This deck speaks most directly to them.
- **Structural sceptics** (~15%): believe AI hype is cyclical, have seen BPR in the 90s and RPA in the 2010s. They will push back on any claim that reads as "this time is different."

### Anxieties

The 2022 consolidation left a residue of scepticism about their own role stability — but not panic. They are watching the wave from a position of relative safety (they survived once), which creates a specific psychological posture: controlled wariness, not alarm. The main anxiety is not job loss — it is being outmanoeuvred, watching a tech lead or a new AI specialist quietly absorb their accountability while they are still nominally "delivery manager." Role erosion by drift, not by layoff.

### Jobs-to-be-Done

- Protect and expand scope of accountability in an environment where AI is redistributing task ownership
- Demonstrate value to senior stakeholders who are asking "what does my IM/DM do now that we have agents?"
- Acquire credentialed language to name what they already do intuitively, so they can defend it
- Find one concrete change they can make before the next sprint planning cycle

### Current belief state on the deck's PoV

Most of the audience is already past "will AI matter for delivery?" — that question is settled in their heads. The gap is not belief in AI's relevance but uncertainty about their specific role in it. They have not yet articulated the generate/decide boundary in their own terms. They sense it but lack the vocabulary. The deck's central claim — agents generate, humans decide — will feel like crystallisation, not revelation.

## 2. Activation gap assessment

The gap is **narrow**. This audience does not need converting. They need framing that crystallises what they already suspect and gives them a vocabulary to defend the governance claim internally. A single sharp move — the generate/decide boundary (Slide 13) — is sufficient to close the gap for capability builders and pragmatic operators. Structural sceptics will require the evidence chain (Slides 5–7) to stay engaged.

The deck makes that single move. The gap is closable in one session.

## 3. Audience fit score: 8 / 10

### What lands

The language register is correct throughout. The deck avoids both cheerleading and catastrophising — it speaks in the register of analytical practitioners reading a delivery risk brief. Slide 10 ("The roles that survived 2022 owned delivery. The roles that didn't, facilitated it.") is the highest-fit moment in the deck. It names the lived experience without dramatising it. The audience will register it as an accurate description, not a threat. That is the right emotional frequency.

The evidence sourcing is strong and specific. Named surveys with sample sizes (BCG n=758, P&G n=776, Digital.ai n=350) will satisfy the analytical subgroup. The Malone counter-evidence (Slide 6) is a genuine show of intellectual honesty — presenting the research that cuts against the rosy view earns credibility disproportionate to the word count it occupies.

The five collision points (Slide 8) are real delivery problems this audience has encountered. Pull vs. push lifecycle, handoff ambiguity, shared context gaps — these map onto failure modes they have already written up in retrospectives. They will nod.

The three-capability frame (Slide 15) uses the vocabulary of the role accurately: workflow architecture, governance, risk at the boundary. These are words IMs and DMs already use for adjacent topics. The deck extends their existing conceptual toolkit rather than asking them to adopt a foreign one.

### What misses

The cover tagline "Own what agents can generate but can't decide" is the PoV thesis, not an audience-resonant entry point. For an IM/DM reading blind — without the framing from the presenter — this sentence requires knowing what the generate/decide split means before it lands. The deck earns that meaning by Slide 13, but the cover does not set up the arrival; it states the conclusion. Low friction for a warmed-up room; potential friction for a cold read.

Slide 8 presents five collision points as labels only ("Pull vs. push lifecycle," "State transition mismatch") with no brief explanation. For pragmatic operators, who need to map claims onto their delivery context, the labels are too compressed. The capability-builder subgroup will fill in the meaning; the pragmatic operator may disengage before Slide 9.

The fragmentation path (Slide 14) is named but not viscerally real. "Tech lead takes workflow, PM takes prioritisation, new AI specialist takes risk" is the right diagnosis — but the structural sceptic subgroup needs one line about why this fragmentation is already happening in their sector, not hypothetical.

## 4. Rhetorical balance score: 8 / 10

The deck is correctly weighted for this audience: **logos-first, ethos-reinforcing, pathos-minimal**. The evidence chain (Slides 3–7) runs before the prescription (Slides 15–18). Counter-evidence is presented before the argument is built (Slide 6 before Slide 13). This is the right sequencing for an analytically oriented audience — showing the limits of the claim before stating the claim is a high-ethos move.

Pathos is present but restrained. Slide 10 and Slide 20 carry the emotional load. That is appropriate. This audience does not need emotional manipulation; they need clarity. The deck provides it.

One imbalance: the $110K figure (Slide 17) arrives as a shock stat without the context that makes it defensible. The methodology appears in Appendix C (Slide 24), but in a live presentation, the main-deck audience will have moved on before the appendix is visible. The figure will generate scepticism in structural sceptics — "that seems inflated" — without the immediate rebuttal available. The annotation on Slide 17 ("based on public API pricing") is not sufficient to neutralise this; it will read as a hedge.

## 5. CTA strength score: 6 / 10

### Assessment of "Own the decisions agents can't make" (Slide 20)

The CTA headline is philosophically correct but operationally incomplete for this audience. IMs and DMs respond to CTAs that have a clear first action — they are trained to break epics into stories. "Own the decisions" is a framing statement, not an action. The support text ("The roles that survived 2022 owned delivery. You already know how this works.") is rhetorically strong — it closes the loop elegantly on Slide 10 — but it reinforces the framing rather than specifying the action.

The actual actionable content sits one slide earlier, on Slide 19 ("Start this week. Not next quarter."), with the three-step action path. The deck buries the action on Slide 19 and closes on a framing statement on Slide 20. For this audience, these should be integrated or the CTA slide should carry the first step explicitly.

The CTA footnote "Next step — speak to Adnan after this session" is a live-event-specific instruction. It is not a durable CTA for anyone watching a recording or reading the deck cold. It also deflects rather than activates — it makes the next action contingent on Adnan's availability rather than something the audience can initiate.

### Recommended 48-hour CTA for IM/DM audience

A specific, self-executable CTA that does not depend on presenter availability:

**"In your next sprint planning, name one task that agents will generate and one decision only a human can make. Write them separately on the board. That is your governance boundary. Audit it at the retrospective."**

This is: (1) executable within 48 hours for anyone currently in a sprint, (2) requires no tooling, budget, or approval, (3) physically enacts the generate/decide split the deck has been building toward, (4) creates a retrospective artefact that makes the governance claim visible to stakeholders.

## 6. Friction audit

These are internal audience reactions, not external objections.

**Friction point 1 — The $110K figure will not be trusted by structural sceptics.** Internal reaction: "Whose estimate is this? What's the actual assumption set?" The "LeanOps practitioner analysis" citation reads as self-referential. This will not derail the argument, but it will create a cognitive split — the sceptic subgroup will spend mental energy doubting this number rather than processing the governance argument that follows. Framing adjustment: add one line on Slide 17 naming the specific assumption (e.g., "assumes 20 developers, each running one long-context agent loop per day at current tier-1 API pricing"). Specificity neutralises doubt faster than source attribution.

**Friction point 2 — Slide 8's collision points are too abstract for pragmatic operators.** Internal reaction: "I know what handoff ambiguity is, but I don't know what it means for an agent specifically." These practitioners will want one concrete example. Framing adjustment: consider a parenthetical after each label in the speaker notes — not in the slide text, since word economy is a constraint — so the presenter can drop one sentence of context per point.

**Friction point 3 — The fork slide (Slide 14) may read as a false binary.** Internal reaction: "In practice, some orgs are doing both — the DM governs while also having a specialist." The framing "evolution path vs. fragmentation path" is slightly adversarial. Structural sceptics will mentally counter-argue rather than absorb the point. Framing adjustment: Appendix E (Slide 26) handles this well with the annotation "Fragmentation is a real outcome. The argument for evolution is probabilistic, not guaranteed." If that honesty lived on Slide 14 itself — even as a three-word note — it would reduce the false-binary friction in the main flow.

**Friction point 4 — The cover premise is accessible only to the warmed-up room.** Internal reaction (cold reader): "What does 'generate but can't decide' mean?" This is not a blocking problem for a live session where the presenter controls the room, but it is a friction point for anyone encountering the deck without context. No framing adjustment needed for this version — the deck is clearly a presenter-led artefact — but flag for any self-running version.

## 7. Emotional resonance score: 7 / 10

The deck earns its emotional landing through the Wave 1 reference, not through emotive language. Slide 9 (timeline) and Slide 10 (the survived/facilitated contrast) are the emotional core. For an audience that lived through 2022, this is exactly right — it activates recognition, not alarm. The recognition triggers a small hit of status validation: "I'm the one who survived because I owned delivery." The CTA then redirects that validation into forward motion.

The emotional deficit is at the start. The cover through Slide 4 is analytically correct but emotionally flat. The question slide (Slide 4: "What actually happens when a delivery team runs with AI — not beside it?") is a good hook but not quite resonant for this audience, because they already know the answer is "it depends on the workflow." The question slightly undersells them. A question that assumes their competence — e.g., something that implies they are the person who would design the answer — would land with higher emotional register. This is a small gap.

Overall, the emotional architecture is right: calm entry, lived-experience recognition in the middle, quiet confidence at the close. No fear-mongering. No cheerleading. That is the correct register for this audience.

## 8. Narrative clarity score: 8 / 10

The argument is followable without the research brief. The deck makes a clean move: wide adoption but shallow integration (Slide 3) → performance upside is real but conditional (Slides 5–6) → most orgs don't redesign (Slide 7) → the collision points (Slide 8) → Wave 1 lesson (Slides 9–10) → the governance gap (Slides 11–12) → the generate/decide boundary (Slide 13) → the fork (Slide 14) → the evolved role (Slides 15–16) → the urgency signal (Slide 17) → the ladder (Slide 18) → the action path (Slide 19) → the CTA (Slide 20).

This is a well-structured argument. Each slide builds on the prior. The conceptual pivot — from "here is the problem" to "here is what you specifically do about it" — happens cleanly at Slide 13. The appendix is correctly segregated.

One clarity gap: Slide 2 ("The jobs question is settled. The structure question isn't.") is a bold opening statement that the deck does not fully cash out until Slide 13. For a practitioner who hasn't read the brief, the referent of "structure question" is not obvious until the generate/decide boundary is introduced eleven slides later. This is not a fatal gap — the deck earns the meaning — but a one-clause bridge on Slide 2 ("The structure question: who decides what agents can't?") would pay off the statement earlier for cold readers.

## 9. Respect signal score: 9 / 10

This is the deck's strongest dimension. The evidence is presented without interpretation scaffolding — the audience is trusted to draw the implication from the numbers. The counter-evidence (Malone) is presented unhedged. The fork slide does not pretend fragmentation is impossible. The appendix exists precisely for the audience member who wants to go deeper. The $141K–170K salary range in the credential slide is stated as a data point, not a promise.

The one condescension risk is the governance maturity ladder (Slide 18). "Most teams are ungoverned. That's your opening." reads slightly as explaining-the-obvious to an audience that will have already thought this. The ladder is valuable; the framing of the insight as a discovery — rather than as a confirmation — slightly undersells their analytical capability. This is a minor point.

## 10. Word economy score: 8 / 10

The slide text is genuinely minimal. Most slides carry a heading and tightly compressed supporting items. The compare and stats layouts enforce economy by design. The presenter is trusted with the explanatory layer. The one exception is Slide 19 (action path), where the "This year" step ("PSM-AI (Scrum.org) · PMI AI in Agile Delivery · AIGP (IAPP — $141K–170K salary range)") reads as a shopping list packed into a single line. The information is valuable but the compression makes it feel like a disclaimer rather than an action. This would be better as three clean items in the appendix, with the main slide carrying only "Credential the capability" and one recommended path.

## 11. Overall persuasion score: 7.6 / 10

### Rationale

This is a well-constructed deck for its audience. It matches the analytical register, deploys evidence correctly, earns its emotional moments through lived-experience reference rather than manipulation, and treats the audience as capable practitioners. The argument is complete and the logic is sound.

The score is held below 8 by three specific gaps:

1. The CTA (Slide 20) does not carry a self-executable first action. The action lives on Slide 19, not the closing frame. An analytically oriented audience needs the imperative and the first step in the same breath.

2. The $110K figure will create active scepticism in roughly 15% of the room (structural sceptics), and the methodology to address that scepticism is buried in Appendix C. This is not a fatal problem — the appendix exists — but it is a missed opportunity to pre-empt the doubt in the main flow with one specific assumption line.

3. The cover tagline states the conclusion before the argument is made. For a live presenter-led session this is acceptable. For any self-running version of this deck, it is a friction point.

These are version-1 calibration gaps, not structural problems. The deck is ready to present. A 48-hour CTA injection and a single assumption line on Slide 17 would move this to a high 8.

## 12. Single most critical framing adjustment

**Integrate the first concrete action into the CTA slide (Slide 20).**

The closing frame currently ends on a philosophical imperative and a presenter-dependent instruction. For IMs and DMs — who are trained to close every planning session with an owner and a next action — a CTA without a first step feels incomplete. The action path on Slide 19 has already done the work. The CTA slide should echo the first step explicitly: "This week — redesign one ceremony. Start there." This requires no new text, only the relocation of one phrase from Slide 19 into Slide 20.

## 13. Recommended 48-hour action for IM/DM audience

This is the CTA the deck should activate, whether stated explicitly or used by the presenter as a closing verbal:

"In your next sprint planning, write two columns: what agents will generate this sprint, and one decision that only a human can make. Put it on the board. That is your governance boundary. Review it at the retrospective."

This CTA is:
- Executable within one sprint cycle (within 48 hours for most teams)
- Tool-free and approval-free
- A physical enactment of the generate/decide boundary — it makes the abstract argument tangible
- Retro-reviewable, creating a feedback loop that builds the governance habit
- Not contingent on Adnan's availability after the session
