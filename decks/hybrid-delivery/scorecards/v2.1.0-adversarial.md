## Steelman of the PoV (recut form)

Agents already generate the routine delivery outputs inside the squad — and the coordination around that generation is visibly straining (PR review time up, review capacity flat). The one accountability agents cannot hold is the *decision* at the human-AI boundary: which actions need a gate, who can halt a run, what risk is tolerable, which HITL pattern governs which work. That boundary work is workflow *design* set before the agent runs — architecture, not the in-task augmented judgment where the evidence says human+AI degrades. The Iteration/Delivery Manager is the natural, already-in-place owner of that design, and can start owning it this week with a five-question squad audit, from inside the team, without waiting for a mandate. The recut's bet is that leading with the felt coordination pain (441%) and a first-person ownership scar makes an IM *back this judgment* faster than leading with a definition of "agent."

## Motivated opponent

Unchanged from prior passes: a senior engineering leader / staff tech lead who already owns the agent tooling, reads the RCTs, and will argue the governance decisions are engineering decisions they are better placed to make. The recut hands this opponent a louder opening (see A2).

## Answers to the four commissioned questions

### Q1 — Does leading with 441% expose a new attack surface? (A5, promoted to hook — Significant, exposure WORSENED)

Partly yes, and the exposure is worse because it now sits at maximum salience.

Two distinct vulnerabilities, and it matters not to conflate them:

1. **The composite-citation blur.** The 441% *number itself* is single-source (DORA 2025) — so a naive "that's not one study" jab is answerable. But the slide body prints 441% above a src line that bundles **four sources with different sampling frames** (DORA 2025, Stack Overflow 2025 n=65k, AI4Agile 2026 n=289, arXiv 2023–25). A prepared opponent reads that as "a scary composite assembled from whatever surveys pointed the right way," and the qualifier that disarms it — *"magnitude cannot be stated as a single number from a single study"* — lives **only in the speaker notes, not on the slide.** As the opening hook, this is now the first thing a skeptic scrutinizes, with no on-slide caveat. That is strictly worse exposure than v2.0.0, where the same slide sat sixth, after the audience had been walked through the mechanics.

2. **Causation and domain.** "PR review time up 441% *as* agentic tooling accelerated throughput" is a correlation asserted as a mechanism ("the bottleneck shifted right"). And PR review time is an **engineering throughput metric** — leading with it foregrounds the engineering domain (feeds A2 below).

**Required resolution:** put the multi-frame caveat and the DORA-specific attribution of the 441% *on the slide body*, not in notes, if this claim is to carry the hook; and frame the number as a signal-to-check-in-your-own-data (which the note already does) rather than a proven causal magnitude.

### Q2 — Do the appendix moves (five collisions, governance maturity ladder) weaken a main-line defense? (No — net neutral-to-positive)

Main-line + appendix remains adequate, and one move actively *helps*:

- **Five collisions → appendix (28):** this was a Medium-confidence Webframp practitioner framework. Removing it from the flow removes a soft-sourced slide from the main line. Adversarially this is neutral-to-positive — one fewer wobble target.
- **Governance maturity ladder → appendix (29):** this **mitigates A8.** In v2.0.0, VERDICT (single-source practitioner framework, Srivastav & Saxena) underpinned *two* main-line slides — the ceremonies map (21) and the maturity ladder (23). If VERDICT fell, both wobbled together. v2.1.0 leaves VERDICT on only *one* main-line slide (19); the maturity ladder is now appendix-only. The "two slides wobble together" concern Vera raised at v1.5.0 is reduced on the main line.
- **Residual (Low):** the squad-audit slide (21) question 4 still references the maturity ladder ("Unseen / Observed / Controlled / Autonomous") and points to the appendix. The main line now cites a ladder it doesn't display. This is a coherence nit, not an adversarial opening.

**No defense the deck previously mounted on the main line is meaningfully weakened by these moves.**

### Q3 — Does the new first-person beat (slide 07) create an adversarial opening? (A9 — NEW Significant)

Yes, but not the one the framing anticipates. The generic "n=1 / anecdote-as-evidence" attack lands **weakly**, because the deck does not use the story as evidence — it is positioned as an ethos beat *before* the RCT slides (09/10), and the rationale explicitly calls it "judgment shown, not cited." An opponent who says "one release note isn't data" is attacking a claim the deck isn't making.

The sharper opening is **trivialization of the flagship example.** The presenter's chosen illustration of "the first boundary I owned" is *catching a customer's name in an auto-drafted release note.* A motivated opponent reframes this precisely: "Your own headline example of governing the human-AI boundary is a content-proofreading miss — a PII catch in a release note. That's QA, or an engineer's checklist item. It demonstrates the boundary work is mundane review, not a distinct IM accountability that justifies elevating the role." This is a live opening because it uses the deck's own most-emphasized personal moment against the thesis. It is *answerable* (the failure-modes slide scales the same "nobody owned the gate" pattern up to Replit deleting a production database), which keeps it at lower-end Significant rather than Critical — but the presenter has to make that recovery in the room, and a weak recovery undercuts the whole "own the boundary" argument that immediately follows.

Secondary (Low): **authenticity/integrity risk from the swap-in instruction.** The slide is scripted in a specific first person ("A team I ran let an agent draft every release note..."), and the presenter note says "swap in your own real boundary-ownership moment." A presenter without such a story who delivers the scripted version verbatim is presenting a fabricated personal anecdote as lived experience — a credibility risk if ever surfaced, and a "manufactured vulnerability" tonal risk regardless. This is a presenter-discipline hazard the deck *invites* by scripting a concrete-but-generic scar story.

**Required resolution:** either (a) escalate the anecdote to a boundary miss with a non-trivial decision stake (something with a genuine risk/production/stakeholder consequence, not a content typo), so it can't be dismissed as QA; or (b) keep the release-note story but wire the on-slide/near-slide beat explicitly to the scaling argument ("small gate, same absence of ownership that deleted a prod database three slides from now") so the trivialization jab is pre-empted. And mark the swap-in as mandatory, not optional, for any non-Adnan presenter.
