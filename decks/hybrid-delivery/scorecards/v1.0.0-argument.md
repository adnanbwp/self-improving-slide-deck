## 1. Six-Question Evaluation

### Q1 — Does the deck honour the conditional framing on the performance hierarchy claim (G1 fix)?

**Status: RESOLVED.**

Slide 5 heading reads "For complex, generative work — the upside is real." The word "consistently" from the prior PoV framing does not appear in the deck. Slide 6 presents Malone directly and uses a dedicated stat-card labeled "cond." (in accent colour) with the text "The advantage is real — and conditional on task type / Generative + iterative work only." The conditional qualifier is in the deck's face — not in a footnote or appendix.

The G1 fix is fully implemented and well-executed.

---

### Q2 — Does the deck handle the Malone counter-evidence credibly (slide 6)?

**Status: SUBSTANTIALLY ADDRESSED, with a residual warrant gap.**

Slide 6 presents Malone directly (106 studies, 90% decision/classification tasks). Appendix slide 22 extends this with the 10%/90% breakdown and explicit reconciliation language: "10% generative/creative tasks — where BCG and P&G results live."

The reconciliation logic is sound in structure: the bulk of Malone's corpus covers task types that delivery teams do not primarily do. However, the mapping of BCG/P&G work into the 10% generative bucket is asserted rather than demonstrated. The deck states the task-type classification as established fact; an analytically inclined audience member could legitimately ask whether the BCG consulting work truly falls in the same task-type category as IM/DM work. This is the same G3 generalisation warrant, now also visible inside the Malone reconciliation itself.

The handling is credible for a practitioner audience. It is not airtight for a research-literate one.

---

### Q3 — Does the deck use the Wave 1 historical precedent correctly — as pre-AI structural pressure, not AI causation (G2 fix)?

**Status: CORRECTLY IMPLEMENTED.**

Slide 9 (timeline) frames Wave 1 entirely in terms of financial-cycle pressure: "QE Era / QT Era / Rates rise. ROI pressure returns. SM enrollment collapses to under 5%." There is no mention of AI as a causal agent in the 2020–2024 contraction. Slide 10 draws the lesson: "The roles that survived 2022 owned delivery." Appendix slide 23 labels this "Wave 1 dissolution — primary sources" with enrollment and demand data.

The historical precedent is correctly deployed as an analogy for what economic pressure does to roles that cannot demonstrate delivery ownership — not as evidence that AI caused the cuts. This is logically defensible and the causation confusion from the research brief has not propagated into the slide text.

The residual gap is that Wave 1 is analogical precedent for Wave 2 dissolution, not direct evidence. This was already the G2 finding and remains partially open (see Q2 evaluation below and Fatal gap G2).

---

### Q4 — Does the deck confront the fragmentation counter-claim (G4 — slide 14)?

**Status: SUBSTANTIALLY ADDRESSED. Rebuttal remains assertion-only.**

Slide 14 ("The Fork") is a dedicated compare slide that names fragmentation explicitly: "Tech lead takes workflow / PM takes prioritisation / New AI specialist takes risk / The IM/DM role dissolves into its parts." The annotation is: "This fork is real. Here's the argument for evolution." This is the correct logical posture — acknowledging the counter-claim before making the case.

Appendix slide 26 extends the analysis with a right-panel case for evolution: "Single accountability — delivery and governance in one role / Faster decisions at the boundary / Cheaper than three specialists." The annotation explicitly qualifies: "Fragmentation is a real outcome. The argument for evolution is probabilistic, not guaranteed."

This is a meaningful improvement. The counter-claim is no longer suppressed. However, the rebuttal in slide 26 is three bare assertions. "Cheaper than three specialists" is the most exposed — it is presented without cost modelling or precedent. The deck earns credit for naming the fork and qualifying the argument as probabilistic. It does not earn credit for evidencing the rebuttal.

### Q5 — Does the deck earn the Arc 5 → Arc 6 transition (G7 — governance to actionable steps)?

**Status: PARTIALLY ADDRESSED. Bridge remains implicit.**

Slide 19 sequences three time-horizons that roughly map onto the three capabilities from slide 15:
- "Redesign one ceremony" maps to workflow architecture
- "Locate your org on the ladder" maps to agentic governance

The sequencing implies the bridge but does not state it. The slide's heading ("Start this week. Not next quarter.") is motivational framing, not a logical connection. An audience member who tracked the three capabilities from slide 15 will see the correspondence; one who did not will see a resource list.

The G7 gap is partially closed by structural sequencing. It is not closed by explicit warrant.

The deck asks the audience to believe that what financial-cycle pressure did to Scrum Masters in 2022 is what AI pressure will do to Iteration Managers — without a single study, survey, or headcount data point covering IM/DM roles specifically in the AI era.

## 5. Score Rationale

**Score: 6.5 / 10**

The v1.0.0 delivery is meaningfully stronger than the v0 argument map scored at 5/10. The improvements are genuine:

- G1 (fatal): Resolved cleanly. The conditional framing is in the slide body, not a footnote. Slide 6 surfaces Malone with appropriate emphasis.
- G4 (significant): Substantially addressed. Slide 14 dedicates a full compare layout to the fragmentation counter-claim. Appendix slide 26 qualifies the evolution argument as probabilistic.
- G6/G8: Resolved by removal of overstatements. The five-year framing and "consistently" are absent.
- Appendix apparatus: Slides 22 and 26 add genuine logical depth for objection-handling; this earns credit because the content is present in the deck, accessible to an analytical reader.

The score is held at 6.5 rather than 7 for two reasons:

First, G2 remains the deck's single most exposed vulnerability. The dissolution claim is the primary call to action for the target audience. It still rests on a Wave 1 analogy with no IM/DM-specific Wave 2 evidence. The analogy is now correctly framed, which prevents the score from remaining at 5. But analogical precedent is a different evidentiary category from direct evidence, and the audience most entitled to push back — a working IM/DM who asks "where is the data showing my specific role is at risk?" — will not find a satisfying answer in the deck.

Second, G3 (the generalisation warrant from consulting work to delivery management work) is the hidden structural assumption on which Claim 1 rests, and it persists unaddressed. The deck's strongest claim depends on an unstated inference that a peer-reviewed reader would flag immediately.

The deck argues at a level appropriate for a practitioner audience. It would not survive a rigorous academic or executive-level challenge on its two core evidentiary weaknesses.
