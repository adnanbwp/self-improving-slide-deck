## 1. Audience model

The audience model from v1.0.0 carries forward without material change. No new information in v1.1.0 alters the segment profile. Key parameters restated for this version's context:

### Cognitive priors

IMs and DMs are delivery pattern-matchers who navigate ambiguity by mapping it onto known structures — sprints, ceremonies, risks, owners, gates. Their BS detector is calibrated to flag vague assertions. Two new slides (14 and 15) speak directly to this wiring: Slide 14 gives them a named structural model (centaur) with a primary citation chain, and Slide 15 gives them a concrete decision taxonomy with role assignments. Both activate the pattern-matching prior rather than resisting it.

### Values (real distribution — not a flat archetype)

Three sub-profiles coexist as established in v1.0.0:

- **Pragmatic operators** (~50%): value stability, proven method, team protection. The HITL patterns on Slide 15 are well-calibrated for this group — they receive structured, labeled options with IM-specific role assignments. The centaur framing may feel abstract to them until the pattern slide lands, but the sequence is fast enough (Slides 14 then 15 in immediate succession) that the bridge is short.
- **Capability builders** (~35%): value professional growth and scope expansion. They are the primary beneficiaries of v1.1.0. The centaur named concept gives them language to claim the structural model; the HITL patterns give them a vocabulary for governance conversations with tech leads; the squad capability gap (Slide 21) gives them a data argument for their own urgency.
- **Structural sceptics** (~15%): the centaur framing is historically grounded (Kasparov 2010, chess experiment, not a vendor whitepaper). This is the right citation profile for this subgroup — it is not a consultancy claim, it is a chess player describing a tournament result. The Replit and Meta incidents (Slide 19) replace the $110K figure that was this group's friction point in v1.0.0. The incidents are named, dated, and multi-source confirmed — significantly harder to dismiss as vendor invention.

### Anxieties

Unchanged from v1.0.0. The primary anxiety remains role erosion by drift — being bypassed quietly while nominally still in the DM seat. Slide 22 (maturity locator) addresses this anxiety most directly: it names the exact bypass mechanism ("where you are: chatbot-level adoption, governance absent, ceremonies unchanged") without dramatizing it. The recognition is productive rather than threatening because the annotation frames it as an accountability gap the audience can close from inside the team.

### Jobs-to-be-Done

Unchanged from v1.0.0:
- Protect and expand scope of accountability
- Demonstrate value to senior stakeholders
- Acquire credentialed language to name what they already do intuitively
- Find one concrete change before the next sprint planning cycle

The v1.1.0 additions speak to all four JTBDs. The centaur model names the structural claim (JTBD 2 and 3). The HITL patterns give the IM language to use in the next sprint ceremony redesign (JTBD 4). The squad capability gap quantifies the urgency for senior stakeholders (JTBD 2). The credential slide maps to JTBD 3 while removing the salary-figure distraction that v1.0.0 carried.

### Current belief state on the deck's PoV

Unchanged from v1.0.0. The audience is past "will AI matter for delivery?" The gap is the vocabulary to articulate the governance claim internally. v1.1.0 gives them more vocabulary than v1.0.0 did: centaur, HITL pattern selector, maturity locator, coordination risk gap. Each new term is a concept they can carry out of the room and use in the next standup or planning session.

## 3. Audience fit score: 8.5 / 10

Increase from v1.0.0 score of 8.0.

### What lands better in v1.1.0

**Centaur framing (Slide 14) — net positive for the segment, with one qualification.** The Kasparov citation is well-chosen for this audience. IMs and DMs are not academic audiences — they will not verify the 2010 NYRB citation — but they will register "Kasparov" as a name that carries weight and "chess experiment" as an empirical event, not an assertion. The split-column layout (Human / AI responsibilities) translates the abstract concept into delivery vocabulary the audience already uses: judgment, accountability, context, stakeholder navigation, final sign-off. These are words they write in job descriptions. The centaur framing does not feel foreign because the column content maps exactly to what they already believe their job is. The Kasparov detour question (does it feel like an academic detour?) is the right one to ask. The answer is: probably not, because the slide itself does not dwell on chess. The headline is the delivery claim ("Process design is the variable. That is your job.") and the chess citation is a single attribution line at the bottom. The framing is inverted correctly — the chess story is evidence for the delivery claim, not the focus.

The one qualification: pragmatic operators who have never heard of Advanced Chess and are unfamiliar with Kasparov may experience the attribution as opaque. "NYRB 2010 · 2005 Freestyle Chess Championship" means nothing to a practitioner who doesn't know the chess world. The attribution could be replaced with a brief parenthetical explaining the experiment outcome (amateur teams beat grandmasters) without losing any credibility. As written, it works for capability builders and structural sceptics but may feel like a prestige drop for pragmatic operators who don't recognize the names.

**HITL patterns (Slide 15) — empowering, not prescriptive.** The question posed was: do three named patterns with IM/DM role assignments feel empowering or prescriptive? For this audience, the answer is empowering, for a specific structural reason. The patterns are framed as a governance decision ("You decide which pattern. The agent can't."), not as a compliance checklist. The IM role entries are stated as design functions — "define review gates in DoD," "maintain task backlog agents pull from," "set escalation thresholds" — all of which are in the IM/DM's existing accountability space. The slide does not introduce new accountability; it maps existing accountability onto a new surface. That mapping is what empowers rather than prescribes. The risk of prescriptive read is mitigated by the kicker ("Governance decision"), which positions the IM as the decision-maker rather than the executor.

**Bypass framing (Slides 10–11) — "got routed around" lands harder.** The v1.0.0 language ("facilitated it") was accurate but passive. The v1.1.0 language ("got routed around") is accurate and activates the anxiety model more effectively. "Got routed around" names the bypass mechanism precisely — not elimination, not layoff, not direct competition, but architectural irrelevance. It describes how a governance gap becomes a standing-erosion mechanism. For IMs and DMs whose primary anxiety is role erosion by drift, not role elimination by layoff, this language is more resonant. Slide 11's annotation ("The automation is here. The governance gap is where you get bypassed.") reinforces this by connecting the bypass mechanism directly to the governance gap they can close. The language update is a meaningful improvement in emotional register alignment.

**Governance failure modes (Slide 19) — incident framing creates more urgency, more durable urgency.** The Replit and Meta incidents replace the $110K token cost figure, which was the deck's primary friction point for structural sceptics in v1.0.0. The incidents are materially stronger evidence for this audience on two dimensions. First, they are named, dated, and multi-source confirmed — they cannot be dismissed as vendor estimates or practitioner back-of-envelope calculations. Second, they are failure patterns, not cost patterns. For IMs and DMs, the governance failure narrative is more directly in-role than a cost argument. They are not budget owners for AI infrastructure in most cases; they are process and governance owners. The incident framing says "here is what no governance looks like in production" — that is directly in the IM/DM's accountability space. The cost framing said "here is what no governance will cost you" — which is a concern they pass to a budget owner, not one they act on themselves. The urgency is more personal and more actionable with the incident framing. The context leak pattern (third item, Slide 19) is the weakest of the three — it is a design principle rather than a named incident — but it bridges from the two named incidents to the "design implication" instruction, which is what the IM/DM needs.

**Squad capability gap (Slide 21) — the 441% figure makes urgency personal for a DM.** The question posed was whether this makes urgency personal for a delivery manager. It does, for a specific reason: 441% increase in PR review time is a metric a delivery manager can verify in their own sprint data. It is not a prediction, a survey result, or an abstract finding — it is a throughput metric that manifests as a delivery problem (slower cycle time, invisible review burden, rising change failure rate). Any DM whose sprint velocity looks healthy but whose team is working evenings will recognize this signal immediately. The "your engineers are already running agents" headline on Slide 21 activates the specific anxiety profile — role erosion by drift, not by layoff — because it names the asymmetry: engineers are ahead, the coordination layer is not keeping up. The three secondary stats (Stack Overflow, AI4Agile, arXiv longitudinal) are correctly positioned as supporting evidence. The primary delivery is the 441% figure in the hero-stat layout, which gives it the visual weight it deserves.

**Maturity locator (Slide 22) — productive self-awareness, not defensive discomfort.** The question posed was whether the two-column framing creates productive self-awareness or defensive discomfort. The balance here is good, for one structural reason: the annotation ("The gap is the accountability. You close it from inside the team, not from a mandate.") redirects from diagnosis to empowerment. The right column ("where you are") is potentially the most uncomfortable slide in the deck for the audience — it describes their current state as behind their own teams. However, it avoids two common failure modes in slides of this type. First, it does not attribute blame — the framing is descriptive, not evaluative. "Chatbot-level adoption" and "ceremonies unchanged" are stated as facts, not as failures. Second, it names the reason this is solvable without institutional authority ("from inside the team, not from a mandate"). For IMs and DMs who are not budget owners and cannot force a top-down rollout, "you close it from inside the team" is the correct register — it hands them a lever they already hold, rather than describing a problem they cannot fix without organizational permission. The risk of defensive discomfort is present but mitigated by the annotation. The slide will create productive tension in the room if the presenter holds it for a beat without rushing to the annotation.

**Credentials as skill development (Slide 23) — removing salary figures improves the slide.** The question posed was: does removing salary figures make the credential section feel more or less compelling? The answer is more compelling, for a reason that the v1.0.0 scorecard partially anticipated. In v1.0.0, the salary shopping-list compressed into a single line read as a disclaimer. The v1.1.0 version replaces that compression with a capability-mapping structure (each credential maps to a specific capability in this deck) that is more directly useful to this audience. The audience's JTBD is to acquire credentialed language for what they already do — not to be told what salary that language might earn them. The mapping structure ("Maps to: HITL pattern governance and ceremony redesign") treats credentials as capability development tools, not compensation signals. This is the correct frame for practitioners who are already employed and seeking to expand scope, not for job-seekers. The slide is now a cleaner structural fit for the audience's actual purchase decision.

### What still misses

The cover tagline assessment from v1.0.0 carries forward unchanged. For a warmed-up presenter-led room this is not a friction point. The cover does not need to change for this version.

The Slide 8 collision points are still too abstract for pragmatic operators. This was flagged in v1.0.0 and has not changed.

## 6. Friction audit

Audience-internal reactions, distinct from external adversarial objections.

**Friction point 1 — The Kasparov attribution may be opaque to pragmatic operators.** Internal reaction: "Who is this? Why chess?" For capability builders and structural sceptics, Kasparov needs no introduction. For pragmatic operators who are chess-agnostic, "Kasparov · NYRB 2010 · 2005 Freestyle Chess Championship" is a row of proper nouns that communicates prestige without meaning. The slide design mitigates this by placing the attribution in a small caption font below the main content — pragmatic operators who don't recognize the name are unlikely to let it interrupt the slide's main argument. This is a low-intensity friction point, not a disruptive one.

**Friction point 2 — Slide 22's right column may create brief defensive reflex before the annotation lands.** Internal reaction: "My ceremonies haven't changed, but that's because there's been no direction from leadership." The column items on the right ("chatbot-level adoption," "governance absent," "ceremonies unchanged") describe a failure of action that could be read as a personal failure. The annotation redirects this, but in a live session the audience reads both columns before the annotation animates in. A speaker who pauses on the annotation and says "this is your opening, not your failure" will neutralize the reflex effectively. This requires presenter intent — it is not handled by the slide alone.

**Friction point 3 — The three HITL patterns may feel incomplete to technically literate audience members.** Internal reaction: "I've read about four HITL modes, not three. What happened to fully autonomous?" The research brief documents that engineering literature has converged on four modes; the deck presents three. The three chosen are the ones with IM/DM role assignments — fully autonomous is omitted because it is not a governance decision the IM/DM makes. For the technically literate subgroup in the audience, this may feel like a truncation. The risk is low — the three-pattern list is sufficient for the governance claim — but a speaker who mentions "there is a fourth mode, fully autonomous, which is appropriate for low-stakes, high-reversibility tasks and does not require a governance decision" will disarm this potential friction immediately.

**Friction point 4 — "Your engineers are already running agents" (Slide 21) may not be true for all audience members.** Internal reaction: "Actually, my engineers are still mostly using Copilot chat. We haven't gone full agentic yet." The headline is calibrated to the median case (Stack Overflow 2025: 31% of developers already using agents), not the universal case. For audience members whose teams are behind the median, the framing may feel premature rather than urgent. The secondary stats row provides the population-level evidence, but a speaker who acknowledges variance ("your team may be at different points in this — the data describes where the leading edge is") would broaden the landing.

**Friction point 5 (v1.0.0 friction point 1 resolved) — The $110K figure friction is eliminated.** The structural sceptic's primary friction point from v1.0.0 has been removed. The replacement (named incidents with primary source citations) is harder to dismiss. This is a net improvement in the friction profile.

Increase from v1.0.0 score of 7.0.

The half-point increase comes from two specific improvements:

First, the bypass language on Slides 10–11 ("got routed around" vs. "facilitated it") is a more precisely calibrated emotional activation. The v1.0.0 language was accurate but passive — "facilitated" describes a supportive relationship that went unrecognized. The v1.1.0 language is accurate and activating — "routed around" names the mechanism of progressive architectural irrelevance. For an audience whose primary anxiety is role erosion by drift, this is the more resonant description. It does not increase alarm (it is not "you will be fired") but it does increase recognition sharpness (it is "you will become structurally optional"). That sharpness creates productive motivation without inducing defensive dissonance.

Second, the squad capability gap (Slide 21) creates a new emotional moment the deck did not have in v1.0.0. The 441% PR review time stat is not emotionally dramatic — it is a number, not a story — but for a delivery manager who has been watching their team's cycle time slow while velocity metrics look acceptable, it is a recognition hit. "Your engineers are already running agents. The coordination isn't keeping up." This is the professional fear in language that does not dramatize it. That restraint is what makes it land for this audience. If this line had been "your role is becoming obsolete," it would have activated defensive dissonance. Stated as a coordination signal, it activates ownership.

The emotional deficit identified in v1.0.0 — the cover through Slide 4 is analytically correct but emotionally flat — has not been addressed in v1.1.0 and carries forward.

Increase from v1.0.0 score of 8.0.

Wide adoption, shallow integration (Slide 3) → performance upside is real but conditional (Slides 5–6) → most orgs bolt AI on (Slide 7) → the collision points (Slide 8) → Wave 1 lesson (Slides 9–10) → the bypass mechanism (Slides 10–11) → the governance gap in numbers (Slide 12) → the generate/decide boundary (Slide 13) → the centaur structural model (Slide 14) → the HITL pattern mechanism (Slide 15) → the fork (Slide 16) → the evolved capabilities (Slide 17) → ceremony redesign (Slide 18) → governance failure modes (Slide 19) → maturity ladder (Slide 20) → squad capability gap (Slide 21) → maturity locator (Slide 22) → credential development (Slide 23) → action path (Slide 24) → CTA (Slide 25).

The narrative improvement in v1.1.0 is structural. In v1.0.0, the generate/decide boundary on Slide 13 was the argument's central claim, followed immediately by the fork and the evolved role. The mechanism — how does an IM/DM actually occupy the boundary? — was underdeveloped. v1.1.0 inserts Slides 14 and 15 between the boundary claim (Slide 13) and the fork (Slide 16). The centaur model tells the audience what the structural form of the relationship looks like (Slide 14); the HITL patterns tell them what the governance decision actually is (Slide 15). The fork (Slide 16) then becomes a consequence of having or not having that mechanism, which is a more logical progression than the v1.0.0 sequence where the fork followed directly from the boundary claim.

The squad capability gap (Slide 21) and maturity locator (Slide 22) are well-placed between the maturity ladder and the credential section. In v1.0.0, the evidence chain ended at Slide 18 (the ladder) and jumped directly to the action path. v1.1.0 bridges that jump with two slides that personalize the urgency (21) and create a self-location moment (22) before the audience is asked to act. This is the correct narrative order for an audience that needs to see themselves in the problem before they are asked to act on it.

The one clarity gap from v1.0.0 — Slide 2's "structure question" referent is unclear until Slide 13 — has not been addressed and carries forward. It is a minor gap for a presenter-led session.
