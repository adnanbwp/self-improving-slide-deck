# Adversarial Scorecard — Hybrid Delivery Deck v1.0.0

**Reviewer:** Vera, Adversarial Critic  
**Date:** 2026-05-30  
**Deck:** The Iteration Manager in the Age of Agents  
**Version:** v1.0.0  

---

## Adversarial Score: 5 / 10

**Score rationale:** The deck's PoV is intellectually honest in places — the conditional framing on the performance hierarchy and the explicit acknowledgment of the dissolution fork are genuine strengths for a v1.0.0. However, four attacks land with significant or critical force: (1) the $110K token cost figure is stated with far more confidence than the source warrants and will be immediately challenged by a numerate audience; (2) the Wave 1 → Wave 2 dissolution argument is asserted through timeline slide framing that conflates cause-and-effect the research explicitly warns against; (3) the "IM as natural locus" claim is the deck's core career argument and receives no structural defense — it is declared, not earned; and (4) the governance moat claim is stated without any timer, despite the research brief explicitly flagging that omission as a credibility risk. A sceptical analytically oriented IM/DM audience will not be stopped by the appendix slides — they will press during the Q&A, and the main deck does not hand the presenter the tools to respond. The score reflects a deck that has done real intellectual work but still leaves the presenter exposed on every question a sceptic would open with.

## Steelmanned PoV

The deck's strongest version of its own argument is this: delivery roles built on ceremony facilitation proved epistemically fragile the moment organizations demanded ROI accountability under rising capital costs — that is Wave 1, and it is documented. The identical structural logic now applies to the remaining transactional delivery work (user story drafting, sprint tracking, status reporting, dependency mapping), which agent tools can already perform at adequate quality for a fraction of the human cost. The roles that will survive this second compression are those whose holders have claimed accountability not just for delivery output but for the governance of the systems generating it — who designs the approval gates, who sets the risk thresholds, who is on the hook when an agentic workflow makes a consequential wrong call. The IM/DM who reorients from facilitation to workflow architecture and agentic governance occupies a position that is organizationally legible, value-attributable, and not currently contested by any other role. The training enrollment data, the practitioner survey data, and the institutional acknowledgment from Scaled Agile and Scrum.org all point the same direction. The deck is asking the audience to act before the second compression arrives, not after it.

---

## Motivated Opponent Profile

**Who attacks this most effectively:** A senior Delivery Manager or Agile Coach who has survived Wave 1, carries healthy skepticism about agile evangelism dressed in new clothes, and has a working knowledge of the actual state of AI tooling in enterprise delivery teams. This person is not hostile to AI; they are hostile to arguments that use AI as a rhetorical device to amplify urgency beyond what the evidence supports. They have read enough McKinsey decks to recognize when a framework is doing work that evidence cannot. They will not attack the performance hierarchy claim — they will attack the cost figure, the causal specificity of Wave 1, and above all the assertion that the IM role specifically is the "natural locus" for governance, rather than one of several plausible candidates. They will also note that the same argument for governance-as-career-moat applies to tech leads, risk managers, and the emerging AI Governance Specialist role — and that the IM is currently losing that competition at most organizations.

## Severity-Ranked Attack Log

### ATTACK 1 — The $110K token cost figure is a practitioner estimate presented as a data point

**Severity: Critical**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 17 presents "$110K / month — a 20-person team running agents without governance" as a headline stat with the visual weight of the BCG and P&G figures that precede it. The source note reads "LeanOps practitioner analysis 2026 · based on public API pricing." The research brief is explicit that the $110K figure is "illustrative based on API pricing math; real-world variation is large" and rates the entire token cost evidence base as Medium confidence. A numerate audience member — any IM or DM who has run API cost dashboards — will immediately ask: what usage assumptions underlie this? What models? What context window sizes? What call frequency? "A 20-person team running agents without governance" is not a well-defined scenario. The gap between the 90th-percentile figure ($1,650/developer/month from the same source) and the $110K headline ($5,500/developer/month) is a 3.3x discrepancy within the same practitioner analysis. That gap is larger than the cost savings the deck claims as the mitigation (88% prompt caching, 60–80% model tier routing). A sceptical audience member who runs the arithmetic in the room will notice that the upper-bound claim is being used to justify the urgency of a governance role while the same source's typical-case figure makes the exposure far less alarming. The appendix slide (Slide 24) discloses the methodology but the main slide (Slide 17) does not carry any confidence qualifier. The net effect is that the deck's most concrete urgency signal rests on a worst-case scenario from a single unreviewed practitioner source, presented without qualification, while the audience is analytically oriented and will check the math.

**Required resolution:** The main deck must either (a) carry explicit uncertainty language on Slide 17 ("illustrative upper-bound based on API pricing assumptions") alongside the source note, or (b) replace the $110K figure with the more defensible median exposure with the upper bound in a subtext note. The appendix methodology slide is not a substitute for confidence qualification on the primary slide — most audiences never open the appendix.

### ATTACK 2 — "IM as natural locus" is declared, not earned

**Severity: Critical**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 14 presents an "Evolution path" and a "Fragmentation path" as a fork, with the evolution path showing the IM/DM owning workflow architecture, agentic governance, and human-AI risk. The annotation reads "This fork is real. Here's the argument for evolution." Slide 15 names three capabilities the IM should build. Slide 26 (appendix) adds the counter-argument that fragmentation produces three separate owners with handoff friction.

The problem is structural: the deck asserts that the IM is the natural candidate for this governance role but offers no organizational theory, no precedent from analogous transitions, and no empirical evidence that IMs are actually winning this claim against the plausible competitors: tech leads (who already own workflow architecture), risk and compliance managers (who already own approval gates in regulated industries), and newly created AI Governance Specialist roles (which Digital.ai data shows 27% of organizations already experimenting with for exactly these functions). The appendix slide acknowledges that "the argument for evolution is probabilistic, not guaranteed" — but this honest admission in the appendix is not matched by honest acknowledgment in the main deck body. The main deck body reads as if the evolution path is available to any IM who builds the three capabilities. It does not address the question of who is *currently winning* this position at the organizations the audience works at.

A well-informed audience member will note that at most large enterprises in 2026, the organizational contest for agentic governance ownership is already underway and the IM is frequently not the frontrunner. The deck does not engage with this competitive landscape at all.

**Required resolution:** The deck must either (a) provide an organizational argument for why the IM's position — spanning delivery accountability, team workflow knowledge, and stakeholder interfaces — gives them a structural advantage in this contest that tech leads and risk managers do not have; or (b) explicitly acknowledge that fragmentation is the current default at most organizations, and that the evolution path requires the IM to actively claim the space before others occupy it, rather than framing it as an available opportunity.

### ATTACK 3 — The governance moat has no timer

**Severity: Significant**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 15 frames "agentic governance" as one of three survival capabilities, and the deck's overall arc positions governance skill as a durable career moat. Slide 18 introduces the governance maturity ladder with L4 defined as "Autonomous Governance — agents monitoring agents." The research brief explicitly flags the counter-claim: "As AI self-governance matures and Level 4 autonomous governance becomes achievable, the human governance layer shrinks. The career moat has a timer." Pax specifically recommended the deck frame governance as "a 5-year window of structural advantage, not a permanent redefinition." The deck does not do this. It treats governance as a stable, enduring claim. The annotation on Slide 13 ("The boundary is not permanent. Your job is to own it right now.") comes close but does not quantify the window or explain what happens after. A sceptical senior audience member will ask: "If L4 is agents monitoring agents, what does the IM do when we reach L4?" The deck has no answer. The research brief does.

**Required resolution:** The deck must acknowledge that the governance moat is time-bounded and give the audience a frame for how long the window is realistic. The "own it right now" language on Slide 13 needs to be extended: owning the boundary now creates organizational track record and institutional knowledge that compounds — not just a static skill that disappears when AI self-governance matures. Without this, the governance argument feels like a short-term arbitrage dressed as a career strategy.

### ATTACK 4 — Wave 1 → Wave 2 causal chain is stated but not established

**Severity: Significant**

**The attack in full:**

Slides 9 and 10 present the Wave 1 → Wave 2 argument as a historical pattern the audience lived. The timeline on Slide 9 is clean and persuasive in presentation. However, the research brief is explicit on a critical limitation: "The training enrollment decline began in 2021, before generative AI had any meaningful enterprise adoption. This is structural role contraction, not AI-caused contraction." And: "In all three cases [Capital One, Royal London, the UK bank], the stated rationale was organizational maturity and cost pressure, not AI adoption."

The deck's argument requires Wave 1 to be a *precedent* — "the same structural logic now applies at Wave 2, but AI is the new compression mechanism." This is a valid interpretive argument. The problem is that the deck's timeline slide presents Wave 1 as if the mechanism were self-evidently analogous to Wave 2, when the mechanisms are actually different: Wave 1 was driven by ROI accountability pressure under rising interest rates (QT); Wave 2 is driven by agent capability making substitution technically plausible. These are related but distinct pressures. A careful audience member will ask: "The SM role didn't decline because AI replaced facilitation — it declined because organizations decided facilitation wasn't worth paying for under ROI pressure. Why should I assume the IM role faces the same dynamic, given that IM work is more directly tied to delivery outcomes?" The deck does not have a tight answer to this because it has blurred the causal mechanism.

Slide 10 ("The roles that survived 2022 owned delivery. The roles that didn't, facilitated it.") is the clearest statement of the lesson — and it is a good one. But it needs to be accompanied by an explicit explanation of *why* this pattern repeats under AI compression, not just asserted as pattern recognition.

**Required resolution:** The deck must make the causal mechanism of Wave 2 explicit and distinct from Wave 1. The structural analogy works, but only if the specific mechanism at Wave 2 (agent substitutability of transactional delivery work, not just ROI pressure) is named. The current framing treats the two waves as the same kind of event when they are related but mechanistically different.

### ATTACK 5 — The performance hierarchy framing creates a scope mismatch

**Severity: Significant**

**The attack in full:**

Slides 5 and 6 present the BCG/P&G upside (+40% quality, 25% faster, 3x top-10%) alongside the Malone counter-evidence (human+AI worse on average, 90% of studies were decision tasks). The deck resolves the tension with "the advantage is real — and conditional on task type" (Slide 6, third stat card) and in the appendix (Slide 22, reconciliation slide). This is intellectually honest and the deck deserves credit for including the counter-evidence explicitly.

The residual attack is this: the BCG study was conducted with consulting tasks (business case development, creative problem-solving, document drafting). The P&G study covered product innovation tasks. Neither study covers delivery management work specifically — sprint planning, risk management, stakeholder communication, production decisions. The research brief acknowledges this directly: "Direct mapping to delivery management work (planning, risk management, ceremony facilitation) is inferential, not demonstrated." The deck presents the BCG/P&G evidence as if it validates the performance hierarchy for the audience's actual work, but the task domain is different. A delivery management audience doing sprint planning is not doing the same generative creative work BCG consultants were doing in an RCT.

The deck's conditional ("for complex, generative work — the upside is real") is partially protective. But a sharp audience member will note the slide headline says "the upside is real" without specifying that the generative work in the studies differs from their day-to-day work. The implication of universality is not quite retracted.

**Required resolution:** The deck's headline claim on Slide 5 should scope the evidence to the task domain studied. The "complex, generative work" qualifier is present but understated. The reconciliation needs to either (a) provide evidence that delivery planning and management work falls on the generative rather than decision side of the task type boundary, or (b) explicitly acknowledge the inferential gap between the research task domain and the audience's actual work.

### ATTACK 6 — "The jobs question is settled" is stated without establishment

**Severity: Significant**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 2 opens with "The jobs question is settled. The structure question isn't." This is a strong rhetorical framing device. It serves the deck's purpose: move past the displacement debate and into the structural question. The problem is that the deck never demonstrates that the jobs question is, in fact, settled. It asserts it. The research brief acknowledges that "the exact scale and timing of job transformation is still debated among economists." For a sophisticated audience of IMs and Delivery Managers who have been living through active headcount reduction in their role category, "the jobs question is settled" may read as dismissive of a real and ongoing concern — and it may backfire.

The specific risk for this audience: they are the people whose jobs are under structural pressure. Telling them "the jobs question is settled — AI won't eliminate jobs, it will transform them" and then immediately showing them evidence that their specific role category (SM training enrollment down 49% to <5%) was already being eliminated is a logical tension the deck does not resolve. If the jobs question is settled in the "transformation not elimination" direction, why does Wave 1 show elimination?

**Required resolution:** The deck must clarify what "settled" means. If the claim is "the debate about whether AI will eliminate most professional work is over — it won't," that requires evidence the deck does not provide. If the claim is "the question of whether AI changes your job is no longer open — it does," that is defensible and much narrower. The slide's rhetorical effect depends on ambiguity that an analytically oriented audience will not grant.

### ATTACK 7 — The "five collision points" slide has no evidence base

**Severity: Low**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 8 lists five workflow-agent collision points (pull vs push lifecycle, learning vs. execution orientation, state transition mismatch, handoff ambiguity, shared context gaps) and labels them "five failure modes." The research brief rates the underlying source (Webframp practitioner analysis, corroborated by Paul Stack) as Medium confidence — a single practitioner primary source. In the deck, these five items are presented as a structural claim without source attribution. For a sceptical audience, a list of five claimed failure modes without any evidence base looks like taxonomy-for-taxonomy's-sake — the kind of slide that consultants produce when they want to appear rigorous but don't have data.

This is a Low severity attack because the collision points are plausible and consistent with the broader research, and the audience is unlikely to push back on individual items. But a rigorous respondent could ask "how do you know these are the five? Where's the study?" and the presenter has no answer beyond "a practitioner wrote about it."

**Required resolution:** Either source the Webframp analysis explicitly in the slide note, or frame the five points explicitly as a practitioner-derived diagnostic framework rather than empirical finding.

### ATTACK 8 — The credential salary figure ($141K–$170K for AIGP holders) is presented as career evidence

**Severity: Low**

**The attack in full:**

Slide 19 (action path) and Slide 25 (appendix credential table) both cite a $141K–$170K salary range for AIGP holders. The research brief source is "AIGP credential salary data ($141K–$170K US median for AIGP holders)" — which is almost certainly self-reported salary data from IAPP's own marketing materials for the credential. This is not an independent salary survey. The AIGP was launched in 2023 and has a small holder population; median salary data from early adopters of a governance credential will be heavily biased by the fact that early adopters of any specialized governance credential tend to already be senior and highly paid. The credential may be worth pursuing, but the salary figure is doing inappropriate persuasive work as career evidence when it almost certainly reflects the seniority of early adopters rather than the market premium for the credential itself.

This is a Low severity attack because salary figures in presentation decks are a common rhetorical device and audiences are generally discounting. But for an analytically oriented audience that includes people who understand selection bias, the figure will raise an eyebrow.

**Required resolution:** The salary range should be accompanied by a source note that identifies it as IAPP-sourced data (or self-reported survey data) rather than an independent labor market finding, and the deck should not present it as evidence that the AIGP credential *causes* high salary — it is evidence of the population currently holding it.

## Single Strongest Attack

The $110K/month token cost figure — the deck's primary urgency signal — is a single-source practitioner estimate with large stated uncertainty, presented on the main slide without a confidence qualifier, while the same source's typical-case figures imply an exposure one-third the size and the deck's own mitigation numbers undercut the claimed scale.
