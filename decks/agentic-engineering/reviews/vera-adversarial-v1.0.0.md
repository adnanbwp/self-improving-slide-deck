## Steelman (the position I am attacking, at its strongest)

The "vibe → structured → agentic" build dial everyone talks about measures exactly one thing: is the output verified before it ships. That is real and valuable — the climb moves verification out of your head into artifacts (prompts → tests → evals → specs + CI). But it is *orthogonal* to a second axis the dial cannot see: runtime governance — while the agent runs, can you see it, stop it, and name who owns it? Production-ready *deployed* work is one cell of a 2×2: agentic build × Controlled (L3) governance. The trap almost nobody sees is agentic-build × Unseen — a perfectly-tested agent nobody knows is running. The build/govern orthogonality is independently reflected in every framework reviewed (NIST, SAIF, OWASP, EU AI Act); the VERDICT mnemonic is single-source *by name* but multi-source *in substance*; the L1-Unseen default is backed by IBM primary data; Knight Capital is an honestly-labelled pre-AI analogy for speed-without-runtime-control. And crucially, governance is framed as engineering (see/stop/own) throughout, with the deck pre-emptively flagging its own soft spots (single-source VERDICT, "our mapping" on SAIF, multi-agent frontier gap, task-dependence of L3). **This is a strong, well-researched, visually clean deck, and the central two-axis claim survives every attack below.** The attacks land on execution and on the deck's *own stated standards*, not on the thesis.

## Motivated opponent

A staff+ platform/security engineer who has actually shipped agents to production and is allergic to governance-consultant framing — and who knows OWASP, NIST, SAIF 2.0, and the EU AI Act at least as well as the deck does. They grant the two-axis frame instantly (it is obviously true) and then attack three things: (1) the packaging of a single Maven-session mnemonic as the organizing structure of the whole field; (2) every place the deck's own honesty/sourcing rule slips; (3) the practical objections a builder raises the second they try to *do* this — cost, overhead, alert-fatigue, and "why your mnemonic instead of the four real standards." Secondary opponent: a leader who has personally watched a "governance initiative" produce a registry nobody maintained.

## BLOCKERS (must be defended before ship)

### B1 — Slide 10 title reverses the provenance and inverts the deck's own honesty posture
**Attack.** The slide title reads **"Google independently arrived at a subset of VERDICT,"** and the body says **"SAIF reproduces three of VERDICT's seven pillars."** Both make VERDICT the prior, canonical framework that Google partially rediscovered. The chronology is backwards: SAIF 2.0 shipped **2025** (the slide's own note says so); VERDICT is a **2026-05-24** Maven session (slide 21). Google did not "arrive at a subset of VERDICT" — VERDICT did not exist yet; VERDICT's authors mapped their synthesis *onto* Google's earlier guidance. The title is the highest-visibility text on the slide and the one people remember. On the exact slide the deck uses to *prove* it is scrupulous about VERDICT's provenance, the headline dresses a one-session mnemonic as the framework a Google standard reinvented. A knowledgeable audience member catches this in two seconds, and it colours their trust in every other honesty claim in the deck. The fine print ("Our mapping, not 'Google's seven pillars'") mitigates but does not fix a title that needs fine print to not mislead.
**Severity rationale.** Blocker: a checkable factual/logical inversion, in a slide title, in a widely-shared deck whose entire credibility strategy is honesty about VERDICT. A reasonable reviewer could rank this high-Significant because the body disclaims it; I keep it Blocker because titles are load-bearing and this one turns the honesty flex into an over-claim.
**Required resolution.** The title and body must not imply VERDICT is temporally or logically prior to SAIF. The satisfactory version frames it as convergence with the correct direction of authorship (e.g. VERDICT's synthesis lands on Google's independently-published agent principles), never "Google arrived at a subset of VERDICT" / "SAIF reproduces VERDICT's pillars."

### B2 — Slide 15 attributes a non-IBM statistic to IBM's primary report
**Attack.** The slide-15 footer reads: *"IBM Cost of a Data Breach 2025: 63% of breached organisations had no AI governance policy, and about 43% cannot produce an AI inventory."* Per the research brief, the 63% and the +$670K are genuine IBM primary numbers (Target 3, Finding 3a) — but the **43% is not**. The brief lists "~43% cannot produce an AI inventory" under a *separate* finding (3b) sourced to CSA/Airia/Forbes aggregators, and its Limitations section states explicitly: *"Some shadow-AI percentages (43%, 24.4%, 79%) are vendor/aggregator-sourced, not read from primary survey PDFs … prefer IBM for on-slide stats."* The deck bundles the aggregator 43% under the IBM banner. That is precisely the sourcing-discipline failure the deck's whole credibility play ("primary/reputable only") is built to avoid — and a hostile fact-checker who pulls the IBM report and cannot find 43% will discount *every* statistic in the deck.
**Severity rationale.** Blocker: a checkable mis-attribution of a statistic to a primary source that (per the team's own research) did not produce it, on a deck about to be shared widely, in a deck that stakes its authority on primary sourcing. Lower visibility than B1 (it is a footnote), but the failure mode — one caught mis-cite poisons trust in all stats — is severe.
**Required resolution.** Verify the 43% figure appears in the IBM 2025 report. If it does not, either re-attribute it to its actual source (and accept its Medium confidence) or drop it and carry only the two genuine IBM primaries (63% no policy; +$670K shadow-AI). Same check on the slide-21 source list.

## SHOULD-FIX (Significant — attacks the deck survives but is weaker for)

### S1 — The "our mapping" honesty label is applied selectively; the six day→pillar overlays are equally synthetic and unflagged
**Attack.** The deck correctly flags its *most-defensible* mapping — SAIF↔VERDICT on slide 10 — as "our mapping." But every day-card carries an overlay tag ("Governs V·E·R," "Governs E·D·C," "Governs all 7") that is an equally invented author-construction mapping a curriculum day onto VERDICT pillars, and *none* of those carry the honesty label. Mapping "Sessions & Memory" to "E·D·C" (slide 9) or claiming Day 4 "Governs all 7" (slide 11) is a pure internal alignment exercise — arguably more of a stretch than the externally-corroborated SAIF mapping that *does* get flagged. A motivated opponent notices the honesty label lands on the one mapping with external backing and is silent on the six with none. Selective honesty reads as marketing, not rigour.
**Required resolution.** Either the day→pillar overlays are visibly the authors' pedagogical mapping (a single global note that the VERDICT tags are the deck's own alignment), or the honesty rule is applied consistently — you cannot flag the strong mapping and quietly assert the weak ones.

### S2 — The convergence mitigation proves the *substance*, not the *seven-fold cut* — and the deck relies structurally on the cut
**Attack.** The single-source mitigation (slides 10/14/21) demonstrates that the *ingredients* of VERDICT appear across four bodies. It does not demonstrate convergence *on VERDICT's specific 7-pillar decomposition* — and the deck's own evidence undercuts it: SAIF hits 3 of 7, NIST/OWASP/EU each map to *different* subsets, and no external body produces the same seven. That is evidence the seven-fold packaging is the authors' editorial choice, assembled by taking one or two pillars from each framework — not an emergent industry structure. A skeptic reasonably says: "You have shown the parts exist in the literature; you have not shown the field is converging on *this* seven-fold shape." And the deck leans on the cut *structurally* — the overlay tags on every day-card, the EXPOSE/BIND/ENFORCE ladder, the map rows are all organized by VERDICT's decomposition. If a reader rejects the seven-fold framing, that scaffolding becomes arbitrary. The verbal hedge ("single-source name for a multi-source consensus") is far lighter than the deck's actual structural dependence on the cut.
**Required resolution.** The deck must position the *seven-fold cut* explicitly as a pedagogical coverage/memorability device whose authority is the substance, not the shape — ideally showing that the union of the four frameworks spans all seven pillars (so the seven read as the minimal spanning set, not a cherry-pick). As drawn, slide 10 accidentally *strengthens* the attack by showcasing that the flagship external source covers only three.

### S3 — "VERDICT vs. just adopting the real standards" is never answered
**Attack.** The sharpest builder question: if SAIF 2.0 (Google), NIST AI RMF + CSA Agentic Profile, OWASP Agentic Top 10, and the EU AI Act already exist and are authoritative — and one is *law* from 2026-08-02 — why do I need your consultant's mnemonic at all? Implement SAIF's three agent principles, comply with the EU AI Act, done. The deck's convergence argument actively *undermines* VERDICT here: the more it proves four authoritative bodies cover the substance, the more VERDICT looks like redundant packaging. The deck asserts convergence as *validation* but never argues what the synthesis *buys* the reader that the four underlying standards don't.
**Required resolution.** State VERDICT's value-add over "just use the four real frameworks" — presumably a single memorable operating model for a builder who will never read four standards and a piece of law. Right now the deck needs the four standards to prove VERDICT is real, and that same move makes VERDICT look optional.

### S4 — No cost or proportionality model for governance; the deck's own logic is applied asymmetrically
**Attack.** The deck insists (slide 5) that the *build* axis is stakes-proportional — "more agentic is not more better; a weekend prototype stays vibe-coded." It never applies the same discipline to the *governance* axis. Yet the filled map (slide 17) literally labels the over-governed cell **"governed toy (overkill)"** — so the deck *knows* governance can be overkill, but gives the reader no rule for how much is enough, and no acknowledgement of what governance *costs*: observability infra, approval-gate latency, the human-in-the-loop bottleneck, registry maintenance burden. A leader's first question — "what does this cost me in eng-time and agent velocity?" — gets no answer. This is the strongest *argument-level* attack: the deck's own symmetry (proportion to stakes) demands a "when NOT to climb the governance ladder" beat, and there isn't one beyond a two-word cell label.
**Required resolution.** A proportionality rule for the governance axis mirroring the build-axis one (what governance each stakes-tier earns; what L3 costs; when L2 is genuinely enough), so "climb both axes" does not read as "maximum governance always."

### S5 — EXPOSE is sold as unarguable, but observability-nobody-watches is the exact bureaucracy the deck claims to avoid
**Attack.** Slide 18 calls EXPOSE "the one move nobody can argue against." The motivated opponent argues against it in one line: an unread audit log and an unmaintained registry are governance *theatre* — L1 wearing an L2 costume. "You can see it" silently assumes someone actually looks. Every leader in the audience has personally watched a registry go stale in a quarter. The deck's Monday-doable CTA is the single thing most likely to be implemented badly, and the deck gives zero guard against the maintained-by-nobody failure mode — which is ironic given the deck spends Act 0 pre-empting "governance = bureaucracy."
**Required resolution.** EXPOSE needs a "watched, not just logged" guard — the registry/log only counts if there is a named owner and a review cadence — or the "nobody can argue against it" claim should be dropped.

### S6 — Concreteness under-delivers against the spec's own constraint (the pre-mortem)
**Attack — this is why the deck falls flat with its stated audience.** The primary audience is builders asking *how* to do production-ready agentic engineering. Spec constraint #4 is explicit: *"Teach the frontier CONCRETELY — real artifacts (a test, an evalset, an AGENTS.md, a policy-server snippet), not abstract assertions."* The deck delivers **exactly one** concrete artifact (the slide-7 AGENTS.md) and then reverts to abstract three-lens cards for Days 2–5: "wrap the capability," "adopt MCP," "curate what persists," "shared eval gates," "write the spec." Those name the right nouns but show the reader nothing they can build. There is no eval example, no MCP config, no kill-switch snippet, no policy-server sketch — all four of which the spec named. A builder leaves with an excellent mental model and a vocabulary to sound sharp in a meeting, but the only thing they can actually *do* Monday is add a line to a markdown file. The deck is a brilliant reframe wearing the clothes of a how-to, and the how-to is one slide deep. (For a *leader* audience the frame + vocabulary + urgency is enough; for the stated *builder* audience it under-delivers on its own promise.)
**Severity rationale.** Significant, not Blocker: it is a partial violation of the spec's concreteness constraint and the primary-audience need, but one can argue a *base* deck's job is the frame, with concreteness deferred to follow-on decks. If Adnan intends this as builder-actionable on its own, it rises toward Blocker.
**Required resolution.** Either add ≥2 more real artifacts (an evalset row and an MCP/policy snippet are the highest-leverage), or the deck should scope itself explicitly as the frame with a named companion for the artifacts — so it is not read as failing the "teach concretely" promise it sets itself.

## NITS (Low)

- **N1 · Slide 6.** "Most agent failures are config failures, not model failures" — the quantifier "most" is unsourced on-slide and not corroborated in the research brief. Plausibly from the Google Day-1 whitepaper, but "most … not …" is an empirical claim begging "says who, what denominator?" Ground it or soften the quantifier.
- **N2 · Slide 14, C pillar.** "Cost / **Compliance** … spend caps and **policy limits**" is the one VERDICT pillar that reads as compliance rather than see/stop/own engineering, and "policy limits" is undefined-as-engineering. It sits on the marquee governance slide, giving the "this is bureaucracy" reflex its only foothold on the thesis slide. Consider splitting spend-control (engineering) from compliance (governance) or defining "policy limits" concretely.
- **N3 · Slide 9, Org lens.** "the memory store is data **you must govern**" is the single place the see/stop/own register slips into the "you must" compliance-imperative tone the deck otherwise avoids. Recast as an engineering action (what you do to the memory store), not an obligation.
- **N4 · Slides 6 & 18, Org lens.** "Fund the platform" / "Fund observability first" are budget asks a hostile builder skims as the bureaucracy creeping in. They are the Org lens so somewhat licensed, but they are the weakest-framed governance beats after N2/N3 — tie each tighter to an engineering trigger (slide 18 does this well with "no broader permissions until L2"; slide 6 does not).
- **N5 · Slide 11 lead.** "An eval that passes is **proof** a change is safe today" — "proof" over-claims for sampled/probabilistic evals, and contradicts the deck's own correct phrase two lines down ("your evalset is validation **evidence**"). Use "evidence" in both places.
- **N6 · Slide 4 / Knight Capital.** The analogy is fair and honestly labelled, but note a subtle own-goal: Knight was a *deployment/config* failure (dormant code reactivated) — which maps better to the deck's *build-axis* discipline (and to slide 6's "config failures") than to the *runtime-governance* case it is deployed to sell. A sharp reader notes Knight argues for better deployment testing as much as for a kill-switch. Not wrong to use it; worth pre-empting.
- **N7 · Slide 5 src.** "Task-dependency guard per research brief, Target 4" cites an internal artifact the external audience cannot see. Cite the external source or drop the internal reference.
- **N8 · Fearmongering bridge (slides 4/15/17).** "Silent time-bomb" borrows its catastrophic payload from a pre-AI analogy (Knight) while the AI-era data (IBM) only establishes *ungoverned/uninventoried* — the *precondition*, not a detonation count. The deck never conflates them falsely (Knight is labelled pre-AI), but the emotional claim rests on analogy + precondition, with no actual agent-caused runtime-harm datapoint. Honest as drawn; strengthen if a real (even smaller) agent incident becomes available.

## What is NOT wrong (so the pass is not padded)

- **The central two-axis thesis is bulletproof.** Every attack above lands on execution, sourcing, or missing beats — none contradicts the build-verify ⊥ runtime-govern orthogonality, which the research confirms is reflected in every framework reviewed.
- **Visual execution is clean.** All 21 slides render without overflow, broken grids, or illegible panels; the readiness map (frame→filled) and the code panel are legible; the progressive-reveal callback (4→17) works. No visual objections invented.
- **The honesty posture is genuinely above-average.** The deck self-flags single-source VERDICT, labels the SAIF mapping, names the multi-agent frontier gap (3×), and guards L3 as task-dependent. The blockers are failures *against this high standard*, not evidence of its absence.
- **Multi-agent gap is handled honestly**, though flagging it three times with an identical citation (OWASP 2026; EU AI Act Recitals 99–100) and zero concrete guidance edges from "honest caveat" toward "alibi" — better to flag than hide, so no penalty beyond this note.

## Adversarial score: 6 / 10 — FAIL

**Rationale.** The thesis is the strongest-standing part of the deck and withstands every attack; the research integration, visual execution, and self-flagging honesty are all genuinely strong. But this is a deck about to be **shared widely with expert builders and leaders**, and it carries **two checkable factual/sourcing defects that violate its own stated standards** in the exact places it stakes its credibility: a slide title that reverses VERDICT's provenance (B1) and a statistic mis-attributed to IBM's primary report (B2). Both are surgical wording fixes, but blockers block — a deck whose credibility strategy is "scrupulously honest, primary sources only" cannot ship to a hostile-expert audience with a backwards title and a mis-cited stat. Beneath the blockers sits a coherent cluster of Significant gaps (selective honesty label, the seven-fold cut vs. substance, VERDICT-vs-standards necessity, no governance cost model, EXPOSE-theatre, concreteness under-delivery against the spec) that keep the score off the 8+ the frame alone would earn. **Ceiling after the two blockers and S1–S4 are addressed is genuinely 8+**; the frame does not need rescuing, the execution does. Score reflects ship-readiness under adversarial fire, and it is not ship-ready as drawn. **FAIL — re-run after B1/B2 resolved.**
