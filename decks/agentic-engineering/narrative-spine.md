## Mode: Shape

## Objective
Convince a builder that agentic engineering is only half the journey — production-ready
deployment needs the governance axis too — and hand them a concrete first move by lens.

## Purpose mapping
**Convince** (primary) — recipe: `That's Funny → Data Detectives → Trust Me I'm an Expert →
Hero & Guide` — with a **Motivate** tail (`Innovation Curve → No Easy Way` thinking) for the
activation close. Rationale: the core job is to change a belief held by a non-expert-in-*this*
("a passing eval = shippable") using synthesized research, then move them to act. The recipe
maps almost 1:1 onto the spec: *That's Funny* = the ironic gap (tested ≠ safe); *Data
Detectives* = the readiness map + Knight Capital as the evidence board; *Trust Me I'm an Expert*
= two cited frameworks with an honest single-source flag; *Hero & Guide* = the builder is the
hero, the deck is the **Defender** guide ("protect your treasures," gives armour + padlock) and
the 3-lens first move is the gift.

## Arc: Man in a Hole
One-line why: the reader is doing fine on the build dial (comfort), gets knocked down by the
ironic gap they couldn't see (trigger), finds the treasure in the dark — a second axis exists
(crisis), climbs both axes (recovery), and arrives at deployed work that's harder to knock down
(better place). It's the recommended arc for *both* the Convince and Motivate families, and it
lets governance be discovered *by* the builder rather than *imposed on* them — which is exactly
the "engineering discipline, not compliance theatre" framing the audience needs.

## Hook
**Irony** (with a superlative edge) — the whole deck hinges on an ironic gap: you did everything
right, and that is precisely what makes this the most dangerous moment.
Illustrative hook direction (Coda writes final copy, runs humanizer): *"Your agent passed every
test you wrote. So why is nobody watching the one that's running right now?"* Plant the irony in
the title frame ("the axis the dial can't see"), land it hard on slide 3, detonate it on slide 4.

## Beats

### Beat 1 — Comfort zone (the build dial, fairly won)
- Purpose: Grant the build dial its full, honest win — the reader has genuinely climbed vibe →
  structured → agentic, and it earned them a great output. They must nod before they can turn.
- Deck prompts: What's genuinely good in the status quo (verification moving *head → artifacts*
  is real progress)? What potential is quietly going to waste (a great output is still not a
  deployment)? Whose comfort is this — IC / Team / Org?
- Style reminder: State the promise warmly, zero strawman. If the builder doesn't feel seen
  here, the turn reads as an attack instead of a reveal.

### Beat 2 — Trigger (the turn / the irony)
- Purpose: Knock the reader down with the ironic gap — passing every test is not the same as
  being safe to deploy. Introduce the unseen second question: *while it runs, can we see it,
  stop it, own it?*
- Deck prompts: What knocks the hero down — the question the dial literally cannot see? Bad luck
  or not paying attention (the dial only measures *is the output verified before it ships*, never
  *what happens while it runs*)? What's the single sentence that flips their confidence?
- Style reminder: One sharp turn, no hedging. Irony hook: "you did everything right — that's the
  problem." Don't soften it into a caveat.

### Beat 3 — Crisis (the silent time-bomb — treasure in the dark)
- Purpose: Take the reader to the bottom of the hole (agentic × Unseen = a perfectly-tested
  agent nobody knows is running) and, in the dark, reveal the treasure: a *second axis exists*.
- Deck prompts: What's the vivid disaster (Knight Capital: \$440M in 45 minutes; a
  broad-permission agent does equivalent damage in *seconds*)? What's found in the dark
  (governance = VERDICT + the maturity ladder — the axis the dial can't see)? Why can't normal
  review save you (agents act faster than review cycles, don't follow org charts, don't ask
  permission)?
- Style reminder: Rolls-Royce Moment — one vivid, exemplary, relatable stat carries the whole
  crisis. The map may appear here as a *frame*, but keep the treasure *earned*, not gifted:
  reveal the axes and mark the trap, withhold "governance is what saves you" until Act 2.

### Beat 4 — Recovery (climb both axes)
- Purpose: Put the lesson to use — walk the 5 days up *both* axes through all three lenses, then
  name the governance axis explicitly and its sequential climb.
- Deck prompts: What changes now (each day placed on the build axis *and* its governance overlay,
  through IC / Team / Org)? What's the order of the climb (EXPOSE → BIND → ENFORCE → SELF-GOVERN —
  you can't BIND what you haven't EXPOSEd)? How does each rung fill one more cell of the map?
- Style reminder: This is a **climb, not a checklist**. Every day must raise the stake and fill
  one more map cell, or the middle collapses into the day-by-day march the spec rejected (Approach
  B). Rule of three on every day: IC / Team / Org.

### Beat 5 — Better place (production-ready, both axes)
- Purpose: Arrive older and wiser — production-ready = agentic × L3, shown in one worked example
  — then hand the reader their concrete first move by lens.
- Deck prompts: What's the new normal (a task that is agentic-built AND L3-governed — harder to
  knock down next time)? What's the one first move for IC / Team / Org (EXPOSE first)? What's the
  six-word version they carry out the door?
- Style reminder: Payoff must mirror the crisis — the same time-bomb, now named and defused.
  Close on a verb, not a summary. The CTA is something the reader can do Monday.

## While writing (style tactics to apply throughout)
- **Movie Time** — every abstract (governance, VERDICT, "Unseen") gets a moment you can *see*.
  If a slide has no movie playing in the reader's head, it's facts, not story.
- **Three is the Magic Number** — IC/Team/Org; vibe/structured/agentic; EXPOSE/BIND/ENFORCE
  (SELF-GOVERN is the horizon, not a fourth item to memorise).
- **Leave it Out!** — the six-word deck: *"Passed every test. Nobody's watching it."*
- **Rolls-Royce Moment** — Knight Capital is the one detail that stands for the whole crisis.
- **Trust Me I'm an Expert / Data Sceptic** — flag VERDICT's single-source confidence honestly
  (slide 21). Acknowledging the gap makes the whole argument more solid, not less.

## The CTA (full text lives in `pov.md`)

Single driven action: **EXPOSE your agents before the next one ships.** Anchored on slide 18 as
the 3-lens synthesis (IC adds the agent to `AGENTS.md` with owner + logging; Team stands up a
registry; Org funds observability before autonomy), emotionally restated on slide 20. Chosen
because it's the *first rung* of the governance climb (EXPOSE → BIND → ENFORCE) and therefore the
one move that is valid at every altitude and impossible to argue against — you cannot govern what
you cannot see.

## Deviations & enhancements vs the spec's Act 0–3 order (flagged, not silent)

The spec's Approach A order maps cleanly onto Man in a Hole — **no hard slide reorder is
proposed.** Four framing/discipline deviations, each flagged with reasoning:

1. **Hook sharpening (enhancement, not reorder).** The spec title is descriptive. The arc wants
   the *irony* carried in the frame from slide 1 and detonated on slides 3–4. Recommend the title
   plant "the axis the dial can't see" and slide 3 carry the sharpest ironic turn. The spec's own
   open question (§10) already floats punchier titles ("Both Axes" / "The Silent Time-Bomb") —
   this endorses that instinct without mandating a rename.
2. **Progressive map reveal (discipline, not reorder).** Slide 4 presents the north-star map as a
   *frame* (axes named, target + trap marked) but must **withhold the full "governance saves you"
   treasure** until Act 2 (slides 14–17). Pure Man in a Hole needs the crisis to bite before the
   recovery; gifting the whole solution at slide 4 softens the hole. The spec's own success
   criterion ("map appears ≥3×, filled progressively") already supports this — treat it as a
   narrative requirement, not just a visual one.
3. **Slide 13 re-cast as "re-deepen," not new topic.** "Why runtime, not pre-deployment review"
   is dramaturgically **crisis material** (it's why the time-bomb is unstoppable by normal means),
   placed right before the governance reveal. Keep it at 13, but Coda should write it as the
   moment that *re-opens the hole* before the payoff — not as a fresh Act 2 lecture. This is the
   one beat most likely to read flat if written as exposition.
4. **The 5-day middle must read as ascent (the main risk).** Seven slides (6–12) of day-layers is
   exactly the shape that became the rejected Approach B "march." Each day must raise the stake
   and visibly fill one more cell of the readiness map, so the middle feels like *climbing*, not
   *enumerating*. If Coda can show the map gaining a filled cell per day, the arc holds.

## Scores (SHAPE-time projected DoD gates)

Provisional gates on the *design* + this spine. The binding **narrative CRITIQUE** re-runs at the

### Narrative — projected 8 / 10
Clean single arc (Man in a Hole) with a genuine hook (irony), a Rolls-Royce movie moment (Knight
Capital), a rule-of-three spine (IC/Team/Org; EXPOSE/BIND/ENFORCE), an honest trust flag (slide
21), concrete artifacts (slide 7, 19), and a payoff that mirrors the crisis (slide 17 callback to
slide 4). Held at 8, not 9, by one live execution risk: the seven-slide 5-day middle can flatten
into a march, and the early north-star map can pre-empt the crisis. **Fixes to hit ≥8 in the
built deck:** (a) fill the map *progressively*, one cell per day; (b) give slides 6–12 a rising
through-line so they read as climb; (c) write slide 13 as a re-deepening of the hole, not fresh
exposition; (d) plant the six-word line and the Knight Capital moment where the spine specifies.

### Persuasion — projected 8 / 10 · Audience fit 8 · CTA clarity 9 · Rhetorical balance: conclusion-early-then-evidence (right for a builder)
Builder-anchored framing throughout, cited (build axis = Google 5-whitepaper series; governance =
VERDICT/ladder, single-source flagged), and a concrete first-move CTA (EXPOSE) calibrated to a
*bridgeable* activation gap — the builder already believes the build axis; the ask is one new
belief (a second axis) plus one Monday action. Rhetorical balance: lead with the ironic
conclusion (slides 3–4), then evidence the climb — correct for an intuitive-but-technical builder
who won't sit through data-first buildup.

**Friction audit (audience-internal reactions — distinct from Vera's adversarial pass):**
- *"Governance = bureaucracy that slows my build."* — the highest-severity friction, from the
  skeptic-of-process reader. Pre-empt in Act 0 and hold all deck: every governance beat is an
  *engineering* question (see / stop / own), never a policy ask. **This is the single most
  critical framing adjustment.**
- *"A 2×2 this early? Just show me code."* — the hands-on builder can bounce off the slide-4
  abstraction. Fix: pair the map with the Knight Capital concreteness so the abstraction is
  *earned* by a visceral stake, not presented cold.
- *"Is VERDICT real, or one consultant's framework?"* — single-source skepticism. The honest flag
  (slide 21) plus Pax's corroboration pass mitigates; keep the flag visible, don't bury it.

**Fix to hit ≥8:** carry the governance-as-engineering framing consistently from slide 3 onward;
keep the CTA a verb (EXPOSE) rather than a posture ("take governance seriously").
