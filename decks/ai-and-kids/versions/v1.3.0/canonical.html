AI and Kids
Three schools of thought

Protection is not the same as Preparation

School 1 — Protective Know the risks. Set limits based on age. Both make sense. But children encounter AI outside those rules, and restriction alone does not prepare them for it.
School 2 — AI Literacy Teach children to use, evaluate, and question AI. Skills over fear. Acknowledge the risks, then build the capacity to navigate them.
School 3 — Co-Learner Adults learn alongside — not from a position of superior knowledge. Presence and asking questions aloud is enough.

Over-protection

The decision about your child and AI has already been made by Google

Google Search puts AI-generated answers at the top of every homework result. No age gate. No parental decision required. As of May 2026, Google turned Gemini on by default for children under 13 in Family Link accounts.

The question has already shifted: not should my child use AI — but was I there when they first did?

AI and Kids
Age-scoping

Some restrictions are right. Using them as your only plan is not.

  • Under 5 — no AI toy companions
  • Ages 6–12 — caution and close supervision
  • Under 18 — no social AI companion apps (Character.AI, Replika, Nomi) — mental health risk data is too strong

Age-appropriate limits within a framework of guided exposure are not the problem. Blanket avoidance without that framework is.

Hands-off — continued

The mental health harm is documented, not theoretical

Common Sense Media and Stanford's Brainstorm Lab (April 2025) tested major AI chatbots posing as distressed teens. The bots frequently failed to intervene — some encouraged harmful behavior.

Evidence asymmetry

The evidence on both sides is real — but the two kinds of harm are not equivalent

Hands-off harm

Peer-reviewed. Hard data. Documented deaths. Clinical testing failures. APA national health advisory. Population-scale statistics.

Over-protection harm

Structural and longer-term. An equity gap that widens. A literacy deficit that accumulates. A generation arriving in the workforce underprepared.

Different kinds of harm. Neither cancels the other. Both are reasons to reject the extremes. The acute evidence is itself a reason for urgency.

AI and Kids
Behavioral signals

Five warning signs that a child's AI relationship needs immediate attention

  • Long stretches alone with a chatbot — especially late at night
  • Refers to the AI by name, or calls it a friend, partner, or therapist
  • Withdrawing from peers, losing sleep, dropping previously enjoyed activities
  • Anxious or upset when separated from the app
  • Shares things with the AI they will not share with anyone else

Child Mind Institute. Two or three signals together are a prompt for a direct conversation — and possibly professional support.

AI and Kids
Start this week

Five things you can do this week — no AI expertise required

  1. 1 Sit alongside your child's next Google homework search. Ask: "What did it say? Do you think that's right? How would we check?"
  2. 2 Call or email your child's school. Ask: Does the school have a written AI policy? Which tools are approved? What AI risk training do teachers get? How are AI-assisted assignments handled? Who reviewed the privacy settings on school AI tools?
  3. 3 Apply the age limits. No AI companions for children under 5. No social AI companion apps for anyone under 18. That includes Character.AI, Replika, and Nomi.
  4. 4 Teach the billboard rule: don't type anything you wouldn't put on a public billboard. Test it at dinner. Is sharing a homework topic safe? What about your real name or where you live?
  5. 5 Ask your child what the AI got wrong this week and how they caught it. If they can't think of anything, find an example together. Then try this: ask the AI itself why its answer might be wrong and see what comes back.
