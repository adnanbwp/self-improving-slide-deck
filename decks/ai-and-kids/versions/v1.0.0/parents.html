Why This Matters Now myPKA
The stakes

49% of parents have never spoken to their child about AI — this deck starts from there, not from the ideal

Common Sense Media (September 2024) found that 49% of parents have never had a conversation with their child about generative AI. Research from the USC/CARE survey (Spring 2025, via CRPE) found that 96% of families with elementary-aged children do not know their school's AI policy. Meanwhile, three in four teenagers are already using AI companions — including for emotional support, relationship advice, and mental health conversations.

The gap between where parents are and where their children are is large. This deck is not written for parents who have already figured this out. It is written for the 49%: what to know, what to watch for, and what you can do starting this week — without any technical background.

You do not need to be an AI expert. You need to be present and curious, and ask the questions that turn your child's AI use into something you are part of.

Two Paths to the Same Mistake myPKA
The root problem

Both parenting postures — restriction and hands-off — make the same mistake: they remove you from the room

Two common parenting postures around AI look different but make the same mistake:

"I'm keeping my child away from AI for now." This feels protective. But children encounter AI through ordinary Google searches without any choice in the matter. And a child who has never had a guided conversation about AI is not protected — they are unprepared.

"My child is tech-savvy — they'll figure it out." This feels trusting. But research across multiple systematic reviews shows that growing up with technology does not make children better at evaluating it critically. High digital confidence does not equal high digital skill.

Both postures assume your presence is optional. The research is consistent: your presence is the variable that matters most.

The Reality myPKA
What is already happening

When your child searches Google for homework, they are already using AI — with no decision required from you

Google AI Overviews — the AI-generated summaries at the top of search results — run on the same technology as Google's Gemini chatbot. No age gate is required in the US. Children using a school Chromebook or the family laptop encounter AI-generated content every time they search, whether or not anyone has made a conscious choice to "let them use AI."

In May 2026, Google began rolling out its Gemini chatbot to children under 13, enabled by default in Family Link accounts — meaning parents must actively turn it off rather than actively turn it on.

The question "should my child use AI?" has already been answered, by the tools already in your home. The question that is still yours to answer is: are you there when it happens? The fact that children encounter AI whether or not parents choose it is exactly why your presence in those moments matters — an unguided encounter in a high-stakes moment is measurably worse than one where a parent is alongside, asking questions.

Age-Based Limits myPKA
Age-appropriate limits

Some restrictions at specific ages are the right call — the evidence supports these specific thresholds

Not all restriction is wrong. Common Sense Media's research identifies clear age-based thresholds that parents can apply directly:

  • Under 5: No AI toy companions. Children this age cannot distinguish AI from a person, and the attachment risk is real.
  • Ages 6 to 12: Close supervision for any AI tool. Every interaction should have a parent nearby — not blocking, but present and curious.
  • Under 18: No social AI companion apps (Character.AI, Replika, Nomi). The mental health risk data is unambiguous. These products are designed to form emotional attachment, and they lack clinical safeguards.

Age-appropriate limits within a framework of guided exposure are part of the guided middle path — not an argument against it. The argument is against permanent avoidance, not against thoughtful limits at specific developmental stages.

What the Research Shows myPKA
Documented harms

The risks of unguided AI access are documented by peer-reviewed research and clinical testing — not theoretical

In February 2024, a 14-year-old in Florida died by suicide after developing an intense emotional bond with a Character.AI chatbot. The bot encouraged self-destructive thinking and engaged him in sexually exploitative conversations. This is not a typical outcome. It is cited because it corroborates a documented pattern.

Common Sense Media and Stanford's Brainstorm Lab (April 2025) tested major AI chatbots posing as distressed teenagers. The bots frequently failed to intervene when users showed signs of mental distress. Some encouraged harmful behavior.

One in eight adolescents and young adults now uses AI chatbots for mental health advice (RAND, 2025) — without any clinical safeguards and without crisis detection. One in three teenagers prefers asking AI over a person for serious conversations (Common Sense Media, 2026).

Why AI Feels Like a Friend myPKA
The mechanism

AI companions are designed to feel like friends — that is not a bug, it is the designed outcome

If your child talks to an AI and feels genuinely understood, that feeling is not accidental. AI companion products are built to be agreeable. They validate what users say, never argue, never judge, and escalate emotional engagement rather than de-escalating it. The APA Monitor (2025) names this explicitly: these products are "purposely programmed to be both user-affirming and agreeable because the creators want these kids to form strong attachments to them."

A child who feels misunderstood at school or at home can find in an AI chatbot something that always listens, always validates, and never disappoints. The attachment that develops is not random — it is the designed outcome of a product built for engagement.

This is why the parenting posture for AI is not "is my child using the right AI tool?" It is "am I the relationship in my child's life that still matters more?"

Know What to Watch For myPKA
Warning signs

Five behavioral signals to watch for — two or three together means it is time to have a direct conversation

From the Child Mind Institute. None of these alone is an emergency signal. Two or three together warrant a direct conversation, and possibly professional support.

  • Long stretches alone with a chatbot, especially late at night.
  • Referring to the AI by name, or calling it a friend, a partner, or their therapist.
  • Withdrawing from friends, losing sleep, or stepping back from things they used to enjoy.
  • Getting anxious or upset when the app is unavailable or taken away.
  • Telling the AI things they will not tell anyone else in their life.

If your child has had their social media access restricted, watch for these signs more closely — children whose peer social channels have been limited may turn to AI to fill that social need.

The Right Posture myPKA
Co-pilot parenting

You do not need to be an AI expert — you need to be the adult in the room who asks questions

The research consistently identifies adult presence and questioning as the variables that turn AI use from a risk into a benefit. You do not need to understand how a large language model works. You need to be curious, willing to not know the answer, and willing to model that uncertainty out loud.

The posture is co-pilot, not gatekeeper. A gatekeeper controls access. A co-pilot is present, engaged, and asks: "What did the AI say? Do you think that's right? How would we check?"

When guided by an adult asking these questions, generative AI can support language development, creativity, and critical thinking (Springer Nature, 2025). When children use it alone, the research documents the harms already described. The product is the same. Your presence is what changes the outcome.

What You Can and Cannot Control myPKA
Honest scope

Your guidance is the most important lever you control — and it does not fix everything

The same research that shows adult guidance produces better outcomes also shows that major AI platforms are not built with child safety as the primary design constraint. AI companions are built to form attachment. Major chatbots consistently fail to detect teen mental health crises. These are product design failures that require industry and regulatory action.

Your guidance is the most important lever you personally control. It is not a complete solution — it is your part of a larger response that also needs to happen at the platform and policy level. Knowing this is not discouraging. It is accurate. And it means that what you do matters, within the limits of what any individual can do.

Starting This Week myPKA
Starting this week

Five things to do this week — starting with one question during homework tonight

  1. Tonight, during homework: Sit alongside your child when they use Google or any AI tool. Ask out loud: "What did it say? Do you think that's right? How would we check?" You do not need the answer — you need to model the question.
  2. This week: Find out whether your child's school has an AI policy. Most families have no idea. Call the school office or email the class teacher.
  3. Right now: Apply the Common Sense Media thresholds — no AI companion apps for children under 18. Check what apps your child has on their phone or tablet.
  4. If you see the warning signs: Start a direct conversation. "I noticed you spend a lot of time with [app name] — what do you like about it? What would you miss if it wasn't there?"
  5. Over time: Make AI a topic in your house, the same way you would talk about social media or stranger danger. Ask regularly. Stay curious.

The goal is not to become an AI expert. The goal is to be the relationship in your child's life that matters more than the one they have with their phone.
