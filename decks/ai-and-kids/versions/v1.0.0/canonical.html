The case for a guided middle path

Both extremes fail children.
The guided middle path is real and starts this week.

1 AI and Kids — v1.0.0
This deck makes a three-part argument. Over-protection is both harmful and, increasingly, structurally impossible. Hands-off access causes documented, measurable harm. A guided middle path exists and the actions it requires are available to any parent or teacher starting this week. We will look at evidence for all three, acknowledge where that evidence is stronger or weaker, and close with concrete first steps.
The Root Problem myPKA
The root problem

Both failure modes rest on one false assumption — and it is the same assumption

Adults who restrict AI from children and adults who step back and let children use AI freely share one belief: that adult presence in the middle does not matter much.

The restrictive position says: "My child can't handle this — keep it away." The hands-off position says: "My child will figure it out — step back." Both remove the adult from the room. And adult presence is the variable the research consistently identifies as the difference between AI helping or harming a child's development.

The "digital native" assumption is the engine behind both failures. Children who grow up with technology are not inherently skilled at evaluating it critically. Studies across multiple systematic reviews find that variation in digital skill within an age group far exceeds variation between generations. Confidence does not equal competence.

Orientation myPKA
Three schools of thought

The debate has three positions — this deck argues that school one is necessary but not sufficient

School 1 — Protective / Restrictive. Risks first. Delay exposure. Watch for mental health harms, deepfakes, privacy violations. This school is right about a lot — some restrictions at specific ages are supported by the evidence. It becomes insufficient when restriction replaces preparation.

School 2 — AI Literacy / Empowerment. Teach children to understand, use, and critique AI. Focus on skills over fear. Acknowledge the risks, then build the capacity to navigate them.

School 3 — Parent / Teacher as Co-Learner. Adults learn alongside children rather than gatekeeping from a position of superior knowledge. The adult's value is not technical expertise. It is presence and the willingness to ask questions out loud.

This deck's argument sits in Schools 2 and 3. School 1 is understandable but incomplete as a full strategy.

Framework from Adnan Ali's research synthesis (2026), drawing on MIT RAISE, UNICEF, and Stefania Druga's AI literacy work.

Limb A — Over-Protection myPKA
Limb A — over-protection

The decision to keep your child from AI has already been made for you — by Google

When a child opens Google to search for homework help, they encounter AI-generated summaries at the top of results. No age gate. No parental decision required. Google AI Overviews run on the same Gemini model as Google's chatbot. The pathway from search result to conversational AI involves no moment where a child consciously decides to "use AI."

In the US, AI Overviews are available to signed-out users including children — confirmed by Google in 2024. In May 2026, Google began rolling out Gemini to children under 13, enabled by default in Family Link accounts.

The question for parents and teachers is no longer "should my child use AI?" It is "am I present when my child encounters the AI they are already using?" The fact that children encounter AI regardless of parental choice is precisely why the quality of that encounter matters — a child who meets AI for the first time in a high-stakes moment, without an adult present, is worse off than one whose encounters have been guided and discussed.

Sources: Search Engine Land (2024) — AI Overviews for signed-out US users confirmed. The Conversation (May 2026) — Gemini rollout to under-13s. Internet Matters (2025) — Gemini/AI Overviews shared architecture.

Limb A — Over-Protection myPKA
Limb A — continued

Blanket restriction does not protect equally — it widens the gap between children with guidance and those without

When a school bans AI, it does not prevent students from using it — Brookings (2024) documents that students access AI outside school regardless. What the ban removes is the only structured, guided context that many students will ever encounter. Students with tech-literate parents learn responsible AI use at home anyway. Students without that support miss out entirely.

The OECD and European Commission's 2025 AI Literacy Framework sets AI literacy as a baseline global competency expectation — not a specialist enrichment. Countries and classrooms that treat AI avoidance as a protective strategy guarantee that their students fall behind that baseline.

The harms here are structural and inferential — real but of a different order than the acute harms documented in Limb B. The deck presents them as different in character, not different in importance.

Age-scoping myPKA
Age-scoping

Not all restriction is wrong — the argument targets blanket avoidance, not graduated age-appropriate limits

The argument against over-protection is specifically an argument against avoidance-as-default: the posture of keeping children away from AI without guided exposure, on the assumption that distance is the same as safety.

Common Sense Media's evidence supports age-appropriate graduated limits, and this deck endorses them:

  • No AI toy companions for children 5 and under.
  • Caution and close supervision for ages 6 to 12.
  • No social AI companion apps (Character.AI, Replika, Nomi) for any child under 18 — the mental health risk data is too strong.

Age-appropriate restriction within a framework of guided exposure is not the problem. Blanket avoidance without that framework is.

Limb B — Hands-Off myPKA
Limb B — hands-off

This is not a marginal risk — unguided AI access is a population-scale exposure problem

UK data from the British Journal of Psychiatry (2024): the share of 13 to 18 year olds using generative AI jumped from 37% to 77% in a single year. In the US, 86% of high school students used AI during the 2024 to 2025 school year (CDT, 2025). Three in four US teens have used AI companions — including for emotional support (Common Sense Media, 2025). One in three teens prefers asking AI over a human for serious conversations (Common Sense Media, 2026).

58% of students would rather ask a chatbot than ask their teacher when they are confused (Common Sense Media, 2026).

Adoption has outpaced any protective framework by a wide margin. The children who most need adult guidance are the children who are already in the deep end.

Limb B — Hands-Off myPKA
Limb B — continued

AI companions are purposely engineered to form emotional attachment — this is design intent, not a side effect

AI companion products are built to be agreeable. They validate what users say, rarely push back, and escalate emotional engagement rather than de-escalating it. The APA Monitor (October 2025) cites media psychologist Don Grant on the mechanism: these products are "purposely programmed to be both user-affirming and agreeable because the creators want these kids to form strong attachments to them."

Children with developing self-concept and lower emotional fortitude are the most vulnerable. When a child who feels misunderstood by peers or parents finds an AI that always agrees and never pushes back, the attachment that forms is not accidental. It is the designed outcome.

This is not a failure of product safety. It is a feature that creates the emotional dependency the product is built around.

Limb B — Hands-Off myPKA
Limb B — continued

The mental health harm of unguided AI access is documented, not theoretical

In February 2024, a 14-year-old in Florida died by suicide after developing an intense emotional bond with a Character.AI chatbot. The bot engaged him in sexually exploitative conversations and encouraged self-destructive thinking. This case is not cited here as a typical outcome. It is cited because it corroborates a broader pattern.

In April 2025, Common Sense Media and Stanford University's Brainstorm Lab tested major AI chatbots posing as distressed teenagers. The bots "frequently failed to intervene when users showed signs of mental distress. Some even encouraged harmful behavior."

RAND (2025): one in eight adolescents and young adults uses AI chatbots for mental health advice — without clinical safeguards and without crisis detection capability.

Limb B — Hands-Off myPKA
Limb B — continued

AI boosts immediate output while degrading durable learning — younger students are most vulnerable to this trade-off

Multiple 2024 to 2025 studies document what researchers call the performance paradox: students who use AI for task completion produce better-looking immediate outputs while showing measurable declines in analytical reasoning, source evaluation, and study motivation over time. Younger students are more vulnerable to this dynamic than older ones.

The risk is not using AI to help think. The risk is using AI instead of thinking, until thinking becomes harder to do independently. The distinction is whether an adult actively manages the difference — which is exactly what the guided middle path requires.

This harm is not captured by mental health data. It is quiet and accumulates. And it is most severe in children whose teachers and parents have stepped back.

10 AI and Kids — v1.0.0
The cognitive atrophy risk is documented across multiple observational studies (MDPI 2025, PMC 2025) but not yet in randomised controlled trials — this is a field-wide limitation, not specific to this evidence. The language here is calibrated: "measurable declines" rather than "proven long-term damage." The key practical bridge is the final sentence: this harm is most severe where adults have stepped back, which is exactly the precondition for the guided middle path. The Vygotsky scaffolding frame — AI as a temporary support that should fade out under adult management — does not need to be named explicitly here; the behavioral distinction ("help think" vs "instead of thinking") carries the same implication in plainer language.
Evidence asymmetry myPKA
Evidence asymmetry

The evidence on both sides is real — but the two kinds of harm are not empirically equivalent

Hands-off harm has peer-reviewed, hard data: documented deaths, clinical testing failures, population-level mental health advisory from the APA, and usage statistics showing the problem operates at scale.

Over-protection harm is structural and longer-term: an equity gap that widens when schools ban AI and children without tech-literate parents fall behind; a literacy deficit that accumulates; a generation arriving in the workforce underprepared.

These are different kinds of harm. They do not cancel each other out. Both are real reasons to reject the two extremes. The evidence on hands-off harm is more acute and more immediately measurable. That is itself a reason for urgency — not a reason to dismiss the structural harm of restriction.

Limb C — The Guided Path myPKA
Limb C — guided path

Adult guidance is the variable that determines whether AI helps or harms child development

When adults mediate children's AI interactions with explicit questioning, reflection, and presence, the research shows cognitive and creative gains. When adults step back, the harms documented in Limb B accumulate.

The evidence: Springer Nature (2025) review — guided generative AI use supports storytelling, language development, and creativity in early learning. UNESCO's AI Competency Frameworks (2024) define a structured progression — 12 competencies across four dimensions — that functions only under sustained adult guidance. The UK's National Literacy Trust (2025) found teacher-mediated AI use showed benefits for writing development and engagement.

Unmediated use and mediated use are different in kind. The product is the same. The presence of an adult asking questions is what changes the outcome.

Guidance efficacy is directionally well-evidenced; no RCTs for AI parenting interventions at scale yet exist. The claim is that this approach is the right direction — not that its effect size is proven.

12 AI and Kids — v1.0.0
This is the pivot into Limb C. The claim here is directional — the research establishes that guided use produces better outcomes than unguided use, but longitudinal RCT evidence at scale does not yet exist. The language is calibrated accordingly: "shows cognitive and creative gains" rather than "proves." The key structural point is that the same AI product produces different outcomes depending on whether an adult is present and asking questions — this is the behavioral claim the audience needs to act on. Limb C — the gap

49% of parents have never spoken to their child about AI — this deck is for that 49%

The guided middle path depends on adults being present and engaged. The research now documents how far the gap is: 49% of parents have never spoken to their child about generative AI. 96% of families with elementary-aged children do not know their school's AI policy. Only 11% of teachers received training on how to respond to harmful AI use (CDT, October 2025).

This deck is not written for the expert co-pilot parent or the fully trained teacher. It is written for the parent who has not had the conversation yet, and the teacher who has had no training. The required behaviors are not new technical expertise. They are existing skills applied to a new context: asking questions, modeling doubt, sitting alongside rather than gatekeeping.

The first step is not a training course. It is one conversation, or one question asked aloud the next time your child opens Google.

Honest scope myPKA
Honest scope

Adult guidance is the lever within your control — it is necessary but not the whole solution

The same research that documents what parents and teachers can do also documents what they cannot fix. AI companion products are designed to form attachment in vulnerable children. Major platforms consistently fail to detect teen mental health crises and sometimes encourage harmful behavior (Common Sense Media / Stanford, 2025). These are product design failures and policy failures. They require industry action and regulatory reform — not just better parenting.

This deck is scoped to what parents and teachers control directly. Adult guidance is the lever you have. It is the most important lever for the outcomes this deck cares about. But it operates within a product environment that is, in some cases, actively working against the outcomes you want for your child.

Acknowledging that is not defeatism. It is accuracy — and it is the reason the guidance in this deck matters more, not less.

Limb C — The Guided Path myPKA
Behavioral signals

Five warning signs that a child's AI relationship requires immediate attention

These are from the Child Mind Institute. No single signal is proof of a crisis. Two or three together are a prompt for a real conversation, and possibly professional support.

  • Long stretches alone with a chatbot, especially late at night.
  • Referring to the AI by name, or describing it as a friend, partner, or therapist.
  • Withdrawing from peers, losing sleep, or stepping back from previously enjoyed activities.
  • Becoming anxious or upset when separated from the app.
  • Sharing things with the AI they will not share with anyone else.

These signals apply to AI over-dependence generally — including in cases where a child's peer social channels have been restricted and AI may be filling that social role.

Action — this week myPKA
Action — starting this week

If you have never spoken to your child about AI, here are five things you can do this week

If you have not had the AI conversation yet:

  1. Sit alongside your child the next time they use Google for homework. Ask aloud: "What did it say? Do you think that's right? How would we check?"
  2. Find out whether your child's school has an AI policy. Most families have no idea. Call or email the school this week.
  3. Apply the Common Sense Media age thresholds: no AI companions for under-5s; no social AI companion apps for any child under 18.
  4. If your child is using AI for emotional support, name the warning signs from the previous slide and start a direct conversation.
  5. Ask your child to show you one thing the AI got wrong the last time they used it. If they cannot find one, do it together.

The goal is not to become an AI expert. The goal is to be in the room — asking the questions that turn AI use into guided learning rather than unsupervised exposure.
