The Classroom Reality myPKA
The classroom reality

86% of high school students used AI in 2024–25 — including in classrooms where it was banned

CDT (2025) found that 86% of high school students used AI during the 2024 to 2025 school year. 58% say they would rather ask a chatbot than ask their teacher when they are confused (Common Sense Media, 2026). Three in four US teenagers have used AI companions, including for emotional support (Common Sense Media, 2025).

Students are using AI regardless of school policy. What varies is whether they are doing it with a teacher present or alone. A classroom ban does not change what students do outside your class. It changes whether you are there when they do it.

Only 11% of teachers received training on how to respond to harmful AI use (CDT, October 2025). This is not a condemnation — it is a description of a systemic failure to prepare teachers for a technology that arrived in classrooms faster than any professional development system could respond.

Limb A — over-protection myPKA
Why avoidance fails

A school that bans AI removes the only guided context many students will ever have — and the ban does not stop usage

Brookings Institution (2024) documents the core problem with school AI bans: students access AI outside school regardless. What the ban removes is the structured, guided context in which responsible AI use could be practiced and evaluated by a professional.

Students with tech-literate parents learn responsible AI use at home anyway. Students without that support — in under-resourced schools, with parents who lack digital literacy, or in homes where no one has had this conversation — have no other venue. The school and the teacher are their entire infrastructure for guided AI exposure.

For those students, a school that bans AI is not protecting them. It is guaranteeing that they fall further behind the students who have access to adult guidance at home.

Limb A — continued myPKA
AI is already in your classroom

Every Google search on a school device returns AI-generated content — the question is whether a teacher is present to guide what happens next

Google AI Overviews — the AI-generated summaries at the top of search results — run on the same Gemini model as Google's chatbot. No age gate. No policy required. When a student on a school Chromebook searches for anything related to their homework, they encounter AI-generated content at the top of results.

Google added a "homework help" button to Chrome in 2025 that, when a student opens a homework page, offers to answer questions or show steps to solve problems. Teachers complained. Google paused the feature. It has not been removed.

The "no AI in my classroom" policy does not describe what is actually available on school devices. The meaningful question is: are students encountering AI-generated content with a teacher who can guide critical evaluation, or are they encountering it alone? The fact that students encounter AI regardless of school policy is exactly why guided classroom exposure matters — a student who first meets AI in an unstructured moment outside school, with no adult present, is worse placed than one whose encounters have been shaped by a teacher who asks questions.

Age-scoping myPKA
Age-appropriate limits

Not all restriction is wrong — the evidence supports these specific classroom limits

The argument against school AI bans is not an argument against all forms of age-appropriate restriction. Common Sense Media's evidence supports specific limits that inform classroom policy:

  • No AI companion apps or social AI tools for any student under 18 — the mental health risk data is clear, and these tools have no educational justification in a classroom context.
  • Structured, teacher-mediated use is appropriate for educational AI tools at all ages — with closer supervision and scaffolding for younger students.
  • Student exposure to AI-generated content (as in Google Search) is essentially universal and already happening — the classroom's job is guided critical evaluation, not prevention.

Age-appropriate graduated restriction within a guided framework is not the target. Blanket avoidance as a substitute for guidance is.

Limb B — hands-off myPKA
Documented harms

Unguided AI access causes measurable harm — mental health and cognitive — and students are already in the deep end

Common Sense Media and Stanford's Brainstorm Lab (April 2025) tested major AI chatbots posing as distressed teenagers. The bots frequently failed to intervene when users showed signs of mental distress. Some encouraged harmful behavior. One in eight adolescents uses AI chatbots for mental health advice without clinical safeguards (RAND, 2025).

Separately, multiple 2024 to 2025 studies document the performance paradox: students who use AI for task completion produce better immediate outputs while showing measurable declines in analytical reasoning and source evaluation over time. Younger students are more vulnerable. The decline is steepest when no adult is actively managing the difference between "AI helps me think" and "AI thinks instead of me."

These are the harms that a classroom ban does not prevent — and that teacher-guided AI use directly addresses.

Limb C — guided path myPKA
Limb C — guided path

Your role is not to out-deliver AI as a knowledge source — it is to be the human in the loop who teaches students to question it

Teaching and knowledge delivery are not the same skill — and AI has already pulled them apart.

AI is a better knowledge deliverer than any human teacher. It is available 24 hours a day, infinitely patient, and draws on more information than any classroom library. Teachers cannot compete with this and should not try.

The teacher's irreplaceable function is something AI cannot do: mediation. Critical questioning. Modeling intellectual humility. Recognising when a student's understanding is genuinely wrong versus superficially fluent. Building the relationship that makes a student willing to say "I don't understand this yet."

EdTech Hub (2025) frames this directly: the teacher's role is shifting from content deliverer to learning facilitator and AI guide — mentoring students through AI interactions, not competing with AI or banning it. That shift is already underway, whether or not teachers choose it consciously.

You Already Have the Skills myPKA
Limb C — continued

AI literacy pedagogy is existing pedagogy applied to a new context — you already have the skills it requires

You do not need a separate AI curriculum. The behaviors that define good AI guidance in the classroom are behaviors you already know how to do:

  • Socratic questioning: "What did the AI say? How did it reach that conclusion? What evidence did it cite?"
  • Source evaluation: "Where did this information come from? Can we verify it independently?"
  • Collaborative sense-making: "Let's look at this output together — what seems right about it? What seems off?"
  • Modeling intellectual uncertainty: "I don't know the answer to that either — let's see what the AI says and then check whether we trust it."

UNESCO's AI Competency Frameworks (2024) identify these behaviors as the core of effective AI guidance. They are already in your toolkit. The domain is new. The pedagogy is not.

What You Can and Cannot Change myPKA
Honest scope

Teacher guidance is necessary but not sufficient — asking for professional development is the evidence-backed response, not a weakness

The research is clear that teachers are being asked to guide students through a domain where most teachers currently lack baseline competency — not through individual failure, but because professional development has not kept pace with AI adoption. Asking for training, curriculum resources, and district-level AI principles is not a weakness. It is what Brookings (2024), UNESCO (2024), and APA (2025) all explicitly recommend.

Teachers also cannot fix product design failures. AI companions built to form emotional attachment in vulnerable students are a product problem and a policy problem. The teacher's lever is classroom mediation — the most important lever within a teacher's direct control, but not the only lever that needs pulling.

Watch for These in Your Students myPKA
Warning signs

Five behavioral signals in a student that warrant a conversation — or a safeguarding referral

From the Child Mind Institute. In a classroom context, you may observe these before parents do.

  • A student referencing an AI companion by name or describing it as a friend, partner, or emotional support.
  • Increasing withdrawal from classroom interaction or peer relationships.
  • Visible distress when device or internet access is restricted.
  • A student sharing that they prefer telling the AI about problems rather than talking to any person.
  • Assignments or conversations that suggest the student is processing personal distress through AI rather than human support systems.

If two or more of these are present, a conversation is warranted. In acute cases, follow your school's safeguarding protocol.

Starting This Week myPKA
Starting Monday

One classroom action this week — no new curriculum, no training course required

  1. This week: Assign a task where students must find one thing the AI got wrong about your subject area. Discuss it as a class. This is source evaluation applied to a new tool — you already know how to do it.
  2. Next class with AI use: Ask aloud before students start: "When we use AI today, I want everyone to notice something it says that surprises you or seems off. We'll compare at the end." You are modeling critical engagement, not expertise.
  3. This week: Find out whether your school or district has an AI policy. If none exists, raise it with leadership as a professional development and curriculum question — Brookings (2024) recommends teacher empowerment through district principles, not top-down bans.
  4. Ongoing: When students submit AI-assisted work, make the AI output the starting point for discussion rather than the submission. "What did AI give you? What did you add or change, and why?"
  5. If you observe warning signs: Follow safeguarding protocols. Teachers may observe AI over-dependence before parents do.

You do not need to be an AI expert. You need to be the adult in the room who asks the questions that turn AI use into critical thinking rather than passive consumption.
