AI and Kids myPKA
The case for a guided middle path

Both extremes fail children.

Over-protection is harmful and now structurally impossible. Hands-off is documented harm. The guided middle path starts this week.

1 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This deck makes a three-part argument. Over-protection is both harmful and, increasingly, structurally impossible. Hands-off access causes documented, measurable harm. A guided middle path exists and the actions it requires are available to any parent or teacher starting this week. We will look at evidence for all three, acknowledge where that evidence is stronger or weaker, and close with concrete first steps.
The Root Problem myPKA
The root problem

Both failure modes rest on the same false assumption

Remove the adult.
Remove the risk.
Wrong.

Restrict or step back — both positions eliminate adult presence. Adult presence is exactly what the research identifies as the deciding variable.

2 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide names the shared false premise before splitting into the two failure modes. The goal is to give the audience one thing to revise — one assumption — rather than two separate failure modes. The digital native myth is well-documented: Nature (2017) and multiple systematic reviews confirm that young people are not inherently better at evaluating technology than older adults. High digital confidence in self-report studies does not correlate with actual skills in detecting AI errors, bias, or misinformation.
Orientation myPKA
Three schools of thought

School one is necessary — but not enough

School 1
Protective

Risks first. Delay exposure. Necessary — some age limits are evidence-based. Fails when restriction replaces preparation.

School 2
AI Literacy

Teach children to use, evaluate, and question AI. Skills over fear. Acknowledge the risks, then build the capacity to navigate them.

School 3
Co-Learner

Adults learn alongside — not from a position of superior knowledge. Presence and asking questions aloud is enough.

This deck's argument sits in Schools 2 and 3. School 1 is understandable but incomplete as a full strategy.

3 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide is an orientation tool, not an argument. Its purpose is to help the audience locate themselves before the argument begins. Most audiences have a mix of School 1 and School 3 instincts. Naming School 1 as understandable but insufficient — rather than simply wrong — is important: Common Sense Media's own evidence supports age-based limits under School 1 logic, and the deck endorses those limits. The argument is against blanket avoidance as a comprehensive strategy, not against all protective instincts.

The decision to keep your child from AI has already been made for you — by Google

Google Search puts AI-generated answers at the top of every homework result. No age gate. No parental decision required. In May 2026, Google turned Gemini on by default for children under 13 in Family Link accounts.

Age-scoping myPKA
Age-scoping

Some limits are right — the argument targets blanket avoidance, not graduated age-appropriate limits

  • Under 5 — no AI toy companions
  • Ages 6–12 — caution and close supervision
  • Under 18 — no social AI companion apps (Character.AI, Replika, Nomi) — mental health risk data is too strong

Age-appropriate limits within a framework of guided exposure are not the problem. Blanket avoidance without that framework is.

The mental health harm is documented, not theoretical

Common Sense Media and Stanford's Brainstorm Lab (April 2025) tested major AI chatbots posing as distressed teens. The bots frequently failed to intervene — some encouraged harmful behavior.

A 14-year-old in Florida died by suicide in February 2024 after forming an intense bond with a Character.AI chatbot. This corroborates a documented pattern, not an isolated incident.

1 in 8

adolescents and young adults use AI chatbots for mental health advice — without clinical safeguards.
RAND, 2025.

9 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
The Character.AI case is used carefully here — it is introduced as corroborating a pattern, not as a standalone proof. The framing is: here is one documented extreme outcome, and here is the broader pattern of evidence that shows why it is not a fluke. Common Sense Media and Stanford's Brainstorm Lab testing is the key source for this broader pattern — it is systematic, methodology-described testing of actual major platforms. The RAND statistic gives the population scale. Together, these three pieces establish that the harm is documented, not anecdotal, and operates at population scale.
Limb B — Hands-Off myPKA
Limb B — continued

AI boosts immediate output while degrading durable learning — younger students are most at risk

Better output.
Less thinking.

Multiple 2024–25 studies document the pattern: AI use for task completion shows measurable declines in analytical reasoning and study motivation over time. The harm is quiet and accumulates. It is worst where adults have stepped back.

10 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
The cognitive atrophy risk is documented across multiple observational studies (MDPI 2025, PMC 2025) but not yet in randomised controlled trials — this is a field-wide limitation, not specific to this evidence. The language here is calibrated: "measurable declines" rather than "proven long-term damage." The key practical bridge is the final sentence: this harm is most severe where adults have stepped back, which is exactly the precondition for the guided middle path. The Vygotsky scaffolding frame — AI as a temporary support that should fade out under adult management — does not need to be named explicitly here; the behavioral distinction ("help think" vs "instead of thinking") carries the same implication in plainer language.
Evidence asymmetry myPKA
Evidence asymmetry

The evidence on both sides is real — but the two kinds of harm are not equivalent

Hands-off harm

Peer-reviewed. Hard data. Documented deaths. Clinical testing failures. APA national health advisory. Population-scale statistics.

Over-protection harm

Structural and longer-term. An equity gap that widens. A literacy deficit that accumulates. A generation arriving in the workforce underprepared.

Different kinds of harm. Neither cancels the other. Both are reasons to reject the extremes. The acute evidence is itself a reason for urgency.

Naming the asymmetry directly — rather than concealing it — increases the deck's credibility with exactly the audience most likely to scrutinise the evidence. The framing "different in kind, not different in importance" is the key phrase. The observation that acute hands-off harm evidence is a reason for urgency rather than a reason to dismiss the structural harm is the logical bridge that keeps both limbs in view.

Limb C — The Guided Path myPKA
Behavioral signals

Five warning signs that a child's AI relationship needs immediate attention

  • Long stretches alone with a chatbot — especially late at night
  • Refers to the AI by name, or calls it a friend, partner, or therapist
  • Withdrawing from peers, losing sleep, dropping previously enjoyed activities
  • Anxious or upset when separated from the app
  • Shares things with the AI they will not share with anyone else

Child Mind Institute. Two or three signals together are a prompt for a direct conversation — and possibly professional support.

Action — this week myPKA
Action — starting this week

Five things you can do this week — no AI expertise required

  1. Sit alongside your child's next Google homework search. Ask: "What did it say? Do you think that's right?"
  2. Find out if your child's school has an AI policy. Most families have no idea. One email or call.
  3. Apply the age limits: no AI companions under 5; no social AI companion apps for anyone under 18.
  4. If your child uses AI for emotional support, review the warning signs from the previous slide and open a conversation.
  5. Ask your child to show you one thing the AI got wrong recently. If they can't find one, find it together.
