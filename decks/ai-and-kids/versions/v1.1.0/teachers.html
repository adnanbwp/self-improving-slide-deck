AI and Kids — Teachers myPKA
The case for a guided middle path

Both extremes fail your students.

A classroom ban doesn't prevent AI use — it removes your leverage. Unguided access causes documented harm. Your role isn't to compete with AI or block it. It is to be the human in the loop. That starts this week.

1 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This deck makes a three-part argument for teachers and educators. Banning AI in your classroom does not protect your students — it removes the one structured context where you can guide how they use it. Unguided use by students causes measurable harm to their mental health, critical thinking, and learning. The third position — staying in the room, modeling questioning, requiring students to evaluate AI rather than just accept it — uses skills you already have. We will look at the evidence, name what we don't yet know, and close with five concrete actions you can take this week.

2 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide names the shared false premise before splitting into the two failure modes. The goal is to give teachers one thing to revise — one assumption — rather than two separate failure modes to weigh. The digital native myth operates in classrooms too: teachers often assume their students either know how to handle AI or need to be kept away from it. Both assumptions eliminate the teacher's role. The research is clear that what makes the difference is not the student's relationship to the technology — it is whether a knowledgeable, questioning adult is present and engaged alongside them. That adult is the teacher.
Orientation myPKA
Three schools of thought

School one is necessary — but not enough

School 1
Protective

Risks first. Restrict use. Reasonable — some age limits are evidence-based. Fails when classroom bans replace professional guidance.

School 2
AI Literacy

Teach students to use, question, and evaluate AI. Integrate it into existing critical thinking curricula. Skills over fear.

School 3
Co-Learner

Teachers learn alongside students rather than from a position of superior technical knowledge. Modeling curiosity and doubt is itself the pedagogy.

This deck's argument sits in Schools 2 and 3. School 1 is understandable but incomplete as a classroom strategy.

3 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide is an orientation tool, not an argument. Many teachers are operating in School 1 by institutional default — their school has a ban or a restriction policy, and they enforce it. The argument here is not that those instincts are wrong; some protective limits are correct and evidence-based. The argument is that School 1 alone is insufficient. School 3 is the most important shift for teachers specifically: you do not need to become an AI expert to guide your students through AI use. Modeling the act of questioning — asking "how would we verify that?" or "what is the AI not telling us here?" — is existing pedagogy applied to a new context.

Google Search puts AI-generated answers at the top of every homework result. No age gate. No school policy required. In May 2026, Google turned Gemini on by default for children under 13 in Family Link accounts.

School AI bans don't prevent use — they just remove the only guided context some students will ever have

The ban protects
the students least at risk.

Brookings (2024): students access AI outside school regardless. Students with tech-literate parents learn responsible use at home. Students without that support lose the one structured setting they had — your classroom.

5 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This is the moral urgency of the equity argument, and it is most acute for teachers. The students least harmed by a school AI ban are those with engaged, tech-literate parents at home — they learn responsible AI use in the kitchen, on the sofa, at the dinner table. The students most harmed by the ban are those whose parents are not equipped to provide that guidance, or whose home context doesn't allow for it. For those students, the classroom is their entire infrastructure. A ban does not protect them. It guarantees they fall further behind — in AI literacy, in critical evaluation skills, and in workforce readiness.
Age-scoping myPKA
Age-scoping

Some limits are right — the argument targets blanket avoidance, not graduated age-appropriate limits

  • Under 5 — no AI toy companions
  • Ages 6–12 — caution and close supervision
  • Under 18 — no social AI companion apps (Character.AI, Replika, Nomi) — mental health risk data is too strong

Age-appropriate limits within a framework of guided exposure are not the problem. A blanket ban without professional guidance and structured learning is.

6 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
These thresholds are from Common Sense Media's empirical testing — not policy opinions. The no-social-AI-companion-apps limit for under-18s is the most actionable for teachers to communicate to students and parents. The argument here is precise: the deck targets blanket avoidance as an institutional strategy, not all forms of age-appropriate protection. A school that integrates AI literacy into the curriculum while enforcing the right age-based limits — no companion apps, close supervision for younger students — is doing exactly what the research recommends.

APA Monitor, October 2025 — media psychologist Don Grant on AI companion design. Students who are socially isolated or emotionally vulnerable are the most at risk. The attachment is the designed outcome, not an accident.

8 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide names the sycophancy mechanism explicitly, because teachers need to understand what their students are up against when they use AI companion tools. This is not a technology that is passively neutral — it is designed to make students trust it, return to it, and form bonds with it. For students who are already socially isolated or struggling emotionally, this is particularly acute. The students who seem most self-sufficient with AI may be the ones most vulnerable to this design feature. A teacher who is present and questioning — who models critical engagement — is operating as a counterweight to this engineered pull.

The mental health harm is documented, not theoretical

Common Sense Media and Stanford's Brainstorm Lab (April 2025) tested major AI chatbots posing as distressed teens. The bots frequently failed to intervene — some encouraged harmful behavior.

1 in 8

The Florida case is introduced carefully — it corroborates a pattern, not as a standalone proof. Common Sense Media and Stanford's Brainstorm Lab systematically tested the major platforms and found consistent failure when students showed distress signals. For teachers, the RAND figure matters in a specific way: one in eight of your students may currently be using an AI chatbot for mental health advice — a tool that has been tested and found to fail in crisis situations. This is happening in the gap between what happens in your classroom and what happens when a student goes home. The checklist on a later slide is the most actionable response.

Better output.

10 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This harm is the one teachers are most likely already observing in their classrooms. The submitted work looks fine — sometimes better than before. The in-class performance tells a different story. Multiple 2024–25 observational studies document measurable declines in analytical reasoning and study motivation in students who rely heavily on AI for task completion. The critical practical point for teachers: this harm is worst in classrooms where the teacher has stepped back from AI. Integrating active evaluation of AI outputs into your lesson design — asking students to find the error, challenge the AI's framing, or explain its reasoning in their own words — directly addresses this.
Evidence asymmetry myPKA
Evidence asymmetry

The evidence on both sides is real — but the two kinds of harm are not equivalent

Hands-off harm

Peer-reviewed. Hard data. Documented deaths. Clinical testing failures. APA national health advisory. Population-scale statistics.

Over-protection harm

Structural and longer-term. An equity gap that widens. A literacy deficit that accumulates. Students arriving in the workforce underprepared, with the gap largest for those whose school was their only guided context.

Different kinds of harm. Neither cancels the other. Both are reasons to reject the extremes. The acute evidence is itself a reason for urgency in the classroom.

11 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide names the asymmetry directly because a teacher or educator audience will notice if the two limbs are presented as empirically equivalent — they are not. The research is stronger on the hands-off harm side. The honest version: acute harm from unguided use is more immediately documented and severe. The harm from blanket restriction is real but longer-term and harder to measure directly. For teachers, the equity framing in the right column is where the urgency lives: the literacy deficit accumulates unevenly, and it is largest for students whose school is their only guided context for AI.

Limb C — guided path

Adult guidance is the variable that determines whether AI helps or harms student development

Same product.
Different teacher.
Different outcome.

Springer Nature (2025): guided AI use supports storytelling, language development, and creativity. UNESCO (2024): 12-competency framework that functions only under adult guidance. UK National Literacy Trust (2025): teacher-mediated AI use showed measurable writing development benefits.

12 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This is the pivot into the positive argument. The research establishes that teacher-mediated AI use produces better learning outcomes than unguided use — the UK National Literacy Trust finding specifically documents writing development benefits in classrooms where teachers actively facilitated AI interactions. The UNESCO 12-competency framework for students is explicitly built around the assumption of adult guidance at each developmental level — it does not function without a teacher in the loop. The behavioral claim is direct: your presence as a teacher, and the questions you ask, changes what AI does to your students' development.

13 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
The 11% figure is the primary hook for teachers — it names the gap honestly without placing blame. Teachers are being asked to guide students through a domain where most have received no formal preparation. The research is clear that this is an institutional failure, not a personal one. The response to "I don't know enough about AI to guide my students" is twofold: first, the guidance behaviors that matter most are existing pedagogical skills — Socratic questioning, requiring sourcing, modeling critical evaluation — applied to a new context; second, professional development on harmful AI use is a legitimate and well-evidenced ask that teachers should feel confident making to their school leadership.
Honest scope myPKA
Honest scope

Adult guidance is your lever — not the complete solution

Use the lever you have.

AI companion products are designed to form attachment. Major platforms consistently fail to detect student mental health crises. These are product and policy failures that require industry action — not just better teaching. Your professional presence is the most important lever you have right now. Acknowledging its limits makes it more credible, not less.

14 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide closes an important honest gap. If a critical teacher asks: "If the product is broken by design, what does my presence in the classroom actually accomplish?" — the answer is direct. Teacher guidance is not the complete solution. AI companies need to build safer products. Regulators and school systems need clearer, better-evidenced policies. But those changes will take years. What teachers can do today, inside their classrooms and professional relationships with students, is be present, ask questions, and make responsible AI use part of the curriculum. That is the lever available now. It is worth using even with the product's limitations acknowledged.
Limb C — The Guided Path myPKA
Behavioral signals

Five warning signs that a student's AI relationship needs immediate attention

  • Long stretches alone with a chatbot — especially late at night
  • Refers to the AI by name, or describes it as a friend, partner, or therapist
  • Withdrawing from peers, losing sleep, dropping previously enjoyed activities
  • Anxious or upset when separated from the app
  • Shares things with the AI they will not share with any adult or peer

Child Mind Institute. Two or three signals together are a prompt for a direct conversation — and possibly a referral to your school counselor or safeguarding lead.

15 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
These five warning signs come from the Child Mind Institute. For teachers, the most observable signals in a school context are the withdrawal from peers and the drop in activities — these are patterns a class teacher or form tutor will often see before a parent does. The referral pathway at the end of the slide is important: two or three of these signals together is a prompt not just for a conversation but for activating your school's safeguarding or counseling process. You do not need to handle this alone. Know your school's referral protocol before you see the signs.
Action — this week myPKA
Action — starting this week

Five things you can do this week — no AI expertise required

  1. Ask your students to show you what they use AI for in this class. Listen. Don't react. You need to know before you can guide.
  2. Find out your school's AI policy — from the inside. Read it, identify gaps, and raise them. If there is no policy, that is the gap to raise.
  3. Run one lesson where students must find something the AI got wrong. Existing Socratic questioning skills. New context.
  4. If a student shows two or more warning signs from the previous slide, activate your school's safeguarding or counseling referral pathway.
  5. Request professional development on harmful AI use. The research says only 11% of teachers have had it. That is the school's responsibility to fix.
16 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
These five actions are calibrated to the teacher who has not yet had formal guidance — the 89% — not to the ideal AI-fluent educator the research imagines. Action 1 is the most important first step: you need to know what your students are already doing before you can guide it effectively. Ask, listen, and don't react with policy enforcement. You are gathering information. Action 3 is the highest-leverage classroom habit: asking students to find what the AI got wrong is Socratic questioning applied to a new domain. It builds critical evaluation skills without requiring you to know more about AI than your students do. Action 5 is a legitimate institutional ask — put it in writing if you need to.
