AI and Kids — Parents myPKA
The case for a guided middle path

Both extremes fail your child.

Blocking AI at home is now structurally impossible. Leaving your child to figure it out alone is documented harm. The guided middle path starts this week — and it doesn't require you to become a tech expert.

1 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This deck makes a three-part argument for parents. Trying to keep your child away from AI is both harmful in the long run and, practically speaking, no longer possible — the technology is built into the tools your child already uses for homework. Leaving your child to use AI without any adult involvement causes measurable harm. The third position — sitting alongside, asking questions, staying in the room — is something any parent can do starting this week with no special knowledge of AI. We will look at the evidence for all three, name what we don't yet know, and close with five concrete things you can do before next weekend.

2 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide names the shared false premise before splitting into the two failure modes. The goal is to give parents one thing to revise — one assumption — rather than two separate failure modes to weigh against each other. The digital native myth is well-documented: parents often assume their child is either naturally good with technology or needs to be kept away from it entirely. Both assumptions remove the adult from the picture. The research is clear that what makes the difference is not the child's relationship to the technology — it is whether a parent or trusted adult is present and engaged alongside them.
Orientation myPKA
Three schools of thought

School one is necessary — but not enough

School 1
Protective

Risks first. Delay exposure. Reasonable — some age limits are evidence-based. Fails when protection replaces preparation.

School 2
AI Literacy

Teach your child to use, question, and evaluate AI. Skills over fear. Name the risks, then build the capacity to handle them.

School 3
Co-Learner

Parents learn alongside — not from a position of superior knowledge. Being there and asking questions out loud is enough.

This deck's argument sits in Schools 2 and 3. School 1 is understandable but incomplete as a full strategy.

3 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide is an orientation tool, not an argument. Its purpose is to help parents locate their own instincts before the argument begins. Most parents have a mix of School 1 and School 3 instincts — they want to protect their child and they also want to be present and involved. The deck endorses the protective limits in School 1 but argues they are not sufficient on their own. School 3 — being there, asking questions, modeling curiosity — is the most important practical shift, and it is one any parent can make without technical expertise.

For parents, the relevance of this slide is direct: you are that engaged parent. Your child's school may or may not have a coherent AI policy. Either way, the home is where your child encounters AI most often — during homework, at the dinner table, in their room late at night. Age-scoping myPKA
Age-scoping

Some limits are right — the argument targets blanket avoidance, not graduated age-appropriate limits

  • Under 5 — no AI toy companions
  • Ages 6–12 — caution and close supervision
  • Under 18 — no social AI companion apps (Character.AI, Replika, Nomi) — mental health risk data is too strong

Age-appropriate limits within a framework of guided exposure are not the problem. Blanket avoidance without that framework is.

They are part of the guided middle path — not a contradiction of it. The argument is against walking away entirely. Having clear age-based rules while staying engaged and present is exactly what the research recommends.

Limb B — hands-off

This is not a marginal risk — it is a population-scale exposure problem

UK teens using generative AI doubled in a single year. 86% of US high school students used AI in 2024–25. One in three prefers asking AI over a human for serious conversations.

Your child is almost certainly already in the deep end. The question is whether you know it.

77%

7 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide establishes scale before naming specific harms. The adoption rate data is not itself harm evidence — it establishes that the risk is not marginal. For parents, the personal framing matters: this is not a story about other people's children. The stat that one in three teens prefers AI over humans for serious conversations is worth pausing on. If your child has something difficult to work through — a friendship falling apart, anxiety about school — there is a real chance they are talking to an AI about it before they talk to you. That is the context for everything that follows.

8 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide names the sycophancy mechanism explicitly, because parents need to understand what they are up against. This is not a technology that is passively neutral — it is designed to make your child like it, trust it, and come back to it. The product's goal is attachment. That does not make AI a villain, but it does mean that your presence as a parent is operating against active engineering in the other direction. Knowing this changes the stakes. A child who seems fine using AI alone may be forming a bond with something that was built to encourage exactly that.

The mental health harm is documented, not theoretical

Common Sense Media and Stanford's Brainstorm Lab (April 2025) tested major AI chatbots posing as distressed teens. The bots frequently failed to intervene — some encouraged harmful behavior.

A 14-year-old in Florida died by suicide in February 2024 after forming an intense bond with a Character.AI chatbot. This corroborates a documented pattern, not an isolated incident.

1 in 8

adolescents and young adults use AI chatbots for mental health advice — without clinical safeguards.
RAND, 2025.

10 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This harm is particularly hard for parents to detect because the visible output — the completed homework, the essay that got submitted — looks fine. The degradation is in the thinking that didn't happen. Multiple 2024–25 observational studies document measurable declines in analytical reasoning and study motivation in students who rely heavily on AI for task completion. The key practical point for parents: the risk is worst where adults have stepped back. Sitting alongside your child while they use AI for homework — asking "how would you check that?" or "does that seem right to you?" — changes the outcome directly.
Evidence asymmetry myPKA
Evidence asymmetry

The evidence on both sides is real — but the two kinds of harm are not equivalent

Hands-off harm

Peer-reviewed. Hard data. Documented deaths. Clinical testing failures. APA national health advisory. Population-scale statistics.

Over-protection harm

Structural and longer-term. A literacy gap that widens. A child arriving in the world underprepared. An equity gap between children whose parents are present and those who aren't.

Different kinds of harm. Neither cancels the other. Both are reasons to reject the extremes.

11 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This slide names the asymmetry directly rather than concealing it. An engaged parent will notice if the two limbs of the argument are presented as empirically equal — the research is stronger on the hands-off harm side. The honest version is this: the harm from leaving your child alone with AI is more immediately and severely documented. The harm from blocking AI entirely is real but longer-term and harder to measure. Both are reasons to take action. The fact that one is more acute is a reason for urgency, not a reason to ignore the structural harm.

Adult guidance is the variable that determines whether AI helps or harms child development

Springer Nature (2025): guided AI use supports storytelling, language development, and creativity. UNESCO (2024): 12-competency framework that only works under adult guidance. UK National Literacy Trust (2025): parent- and teacher-mediated AI use showed measurable writing development benefits.

12 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
This is the pivot into the positive argument. The research establishes that guided use produces better outcomes than unguided use — the same AI product, in the same hands, produces a different result depending on whether an adult is present and asking questions. The language here is calibrated: these studies show better outcomes, but long-term randomised trial evidence does not yet exist. The behavioral claim is simple: your presence changes what the technology does to your child's development. Sitting alongside at the dinner table while your child uses AI for a school project is not a passive act — it is the intervention.

This slide closes an important honest gap. If a critical parent asks: "If the product is broken, what does my involvement actually accomplish?" — the answer is direct. Limb C — The Guided Path myPKA
Behavioral signals

Five warning signs that your child's AI relationship needs immediate attention

  • Long stretches alone with a chatbot — especially late at night in their bedroom
  • Refers to the AI by name, or calls it a friend, partner, or therapist
  • Withdrawing from friends, losing sleep, dropping hobbies they previously enjoyed
  • Anxious or upset when separated from the app
  • Shares things with the AI they will not share with you or anyone else

Child Mind Institute. Two or three signals together are a prompt for a direct conversation — and possibly professional support.

15 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
These five warning signs come from the Child Mind Institute and are specifically oriented toward parents. None of these alone means your child is in crisis. But two or three together — particularly the late-night isolation or the refusal to share with you what they share with the AI — are a clear signal to open a direct conversation. If your child recently lost access to social media and you notice any of these patterns appearing or intensifying, that is worth particular attention. The checklist is something you can use tonight, not something to file away.
Action — this week myPKA
Action — starting this week

Five things you can do this week — no AI expertise required

  1. Sit alongside your child's next Google homework search. Ask: "What did it say? Do you think that's right?"
  2. Find out if your child's school has an AI policy. Most families have no idea. One email to the school is all it takes.
  3. Apply the age limits at home: no AI companions under 5; no social AI companion apps for anyone under 18.
  4. If your child uses AI for emotional support, review the warning signs from the previous slide and open a conversation tonight.
  5. Ask your child to show you one thing the AI got wrong recently. If they can't find one, find it together at the dinner table.
16 AI and Kids — v1.1.0
These five actions are calibrated to the parent who has never had the conversation — the 49% — not to the ideal co-pilot parent the research imagines. Action 1 is the most important: it is something you can do tonight during an ordinary homework session. No preparation required. Action 2 — finding out your child's school AI policy — is something 96% of parents have not done; one email changes that. Action 5 is the one that builds the most durable habit: asking your child to find something the AI got wrong teaches them healthy skepticism without you needing to know anything about how AI works.
