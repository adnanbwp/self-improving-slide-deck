### Gap Status — Prior Four Significant Gaps

**Gap 1 — Evidence asymmetry between Limb A and Limb B**
Status: **Partially closed**

The Google AI Overviews finding (Thread 2) adds a new and qualitatively different warrant to Limb A: over-protection is not merely harmful in the sense of a missed opportunity — it is *increasingly impossible* because AI is already embedded in the tools children use for homework, without any explicit parental decision to introduce AI. This changes the character of the Limb A argument. Instead of arguing "you are denying your child something valuable," the deck can now argue "you cannot keep your child from AI; the question is whether you are present when they encounter it." This is a substantially stronger empirical footing for Limb A, reducing the asymmetry with Limb B. However, the core asymmetry is not eliminated: Limb B's harm evidence (mortality, mental health testing failures, RAND usage data) remains categorically stronger than Limb A's evidence. The improved Limb A warrant is about inevitability and impossibility, not about equivalent acute harm. Required fix remains in place, with a sharpened form: Limb A should now be framed as "over-protection is harmful AND increasingly impossible," not as empirically equivalent to Limb B's acute harms.

**Gap 2 — Adult AI literacy precondition is unmet and unaddressed**
Status: **Still open — severity upgraded from Significant to Significant (acute)**

The supplement quantifies the adult unpreparedness problem in a way that makes this gap more acute, not less. The v1 audit identified this as a significant gap based on general research indicating teacher anxiety and low AI literacy. The supplement now provides specific numbers: 49% of parents have never spoken to their child about AI; 96% of families do not know their elementary school's AI policy; only 11% of teachers received training on how to respond to harmful AI use. These statistics directly undercut Limb C's claim that parents and teachers have "specific mindsets and actions available today." Available and known-and-practiced are different claims, and the supplement makes clear that the gap between them is enormous. The gap is not closed — it is more precisely measured. The deck must now address not just the conceptual objection ("I don't know enough about AI") but a statistically documented reality: the vast majority of the deck's target audience is not doing the thing the deck says they can do today. The response remains the same in principle (the required behaviors are existing skills, not new technical expertise), but the deck must now reckon with why these behaviors are not being deployed, not merely assert that they are available.

**Gap 3 — Product design counter-argument is unacknowledged in the logical chain**
Status: **Still open**

No material change from the supplement. The sycophancy-as-engineered-attachment finding from Thread 1 (AI companions are purposely programmed to be agreeable to form user attachment, per APA Monitor 2025) actually deepens this gap: it moves the product design problem from "companies fail to protect children" (a negligence frame) to "companies actively engineer attachment in vulnerable children" (a design intent frame). This is a harder problem for adult guidance to address, because the engineering is purpose-built to circumvent the adult's countervailing influence. The deck's Limb C argument — adult guidance as the lever — now needs to contend not just with inadequate product safeguards but with product features that are specifically designed to create the attachment pattern that makes adult guidance harder to implement. The required fix from v1 stands and is more urgent: acknowledge that adult guidance operates against actively adversarial product design, not merely passive product neglect.

**Gap 4 — Haidt analogy presented as supporting evidence rather than rhetorical framing**
Status: **Still open — minor**

No new material in the supplement addresses this. The Google AI Overviews finding actually reduces the deck's dependence on the Haidt analogy for Limb A, because it provides direct AI-specific evidence that the "keep kids away from AI" position is untenable. This is welcome: if Coda adopts the Thread 2 framing for Limb A, Haidt becomes a rhetorical opener that the new AI-specific evidence then supersedes — which is exactly the correct use. The required fix from v1 stands: Haidt as rhetorical anchor, not empirical warrant. The new evidence makes it easier to comply with this fix.

### New Gaps Introduced by the Supplement

**New Gap 8 — Section 5 (AI companionship substitution) is in the deck's evidence base without actionable guidance attached**
**Type:** Unsupported conclusion / stranded evidence
**Severity: Significant**

The supplement includes Section 5 (Emerging Concerns) documenting the plausible but unproven dynamic that Australia's social media ban may have increased AI companion use among adolescents, because peer social channels were removed while AI companion channels remained open. The brief is appropriately careful — it flags this as an inference, not a finding, and the brief author explicitly states the deck cannot claim substitution has been measured or proven.

The logical problem for the deck is not the evidentiary caveat — the caveat is correct and should be respected. The problem is that the deck's PoV includes a promise to the audience: parents and teachers have actionable guidance available *today*. If the deck surfaces the AI companion substitution concern (which it should, because the eSafety data on 200,000 Australian children using AI companions pre-ban is real and usable), the audience will reasonably expect a response: "so what do we do about this?" The v1 brief's Limb C guidance covers the general case (guide, co-explore, apply age-appropriate limits, watch for warning signs). But the specific case — a child whose peer social channels have been restricted and who may be using AI as a substitute — requires a different and more precise intervention than the general "co-pilot parenting" frame. There is no validated guidance in the research base for this specific dynamic, because the dynamic has not yet been confirmed as occurring.

Required fix: Coda must decide one of three options. (a) Include the Section 5 material as a forward-looking slide ("this is an emerging risk that reinforces why guided relationships with AI matter now") without implying actionable guidance specific to this case. (b) Exclude it until evidence is stronger and actionable guidance exists. (c) Use it only as reinforcing context for the general warning signs slide (the Child Mind Institute checklist from Thread 1 is applicable and evidenced). Option (a) or (c) are defensible. Option (b) is defensible but represents a missed opportunity. Presenting Section 5 material as evidence supporting existing actionable guidance would be a logical overreach — the guidance does not specifically address confirmed substitution behavior.

**New Gap 9 — The three-schools framework introduces an unresolved structural question**
**Type:** Structural gap / missing warrant
**Severity: Minor**

The aa2brain concept note offers a three-schools framing (Protective/Restrictive; AI Literacy/Empowerment; Parent-as-Co-Learner) as a rhetorical architecture that is cleaner than the deck's current implicit framing. The deck's PoV sits in camps 2 and 3, arguing against camp 1 as insufficient.

This framing is useful but introduces its own gap: the deck must explain why school 1 (Protective/Restrictive) is "understandable but insufficient" rather than simply wrong. The v1 argument map's Gap 5 (age-scoping) is directly relevant here — there are cases where protective/restrictive approaches are *correct* (under-5s, social AI companion apps for all under-18s). If the deck adopts the three-schools framing and positions school 1 as the "insufficient" response it is arguing against, it must acknowledge that school 1 is partially right — which is what Gap 5 already requires. The three-schools framing therefore does not introduce a new logical problem; it surfaces an existing one with more rhetorical sharpness. If Coda uses the three-schools framing, Gap 5 must be closed before production, or the framing creates a false dichotomy between school 1 and schools 2+3.

### Specific Questions Addressed

**Question 1: Does the Google AI Overviews finding require a structural change to Limb A?**

Yes, and the change is not cosmetic. The v1 Limb A claim is: "Withholding AI access entirely causes its own category of harm." The supplement's Thread 2 finding changes the logical structure of this claim. The new, correct form of Limb A is: "Over-protection is harmful AND increasingly impossible — children already encounter AI through Google Search, which is universally considered safe and educational, without any explicit parental or teacher decision to introduce AI. The question for adults is not whether their child will encounter AI, but whether the adult is present when it happens."

This is a structural change because it converts Limb A from a normative claim (over-protection causes harm you should avoid) to a factual claim (over-protection is structurally unavailable as a strategy, regardless of whether you would choose it). A factual impossibility claim is logically stronger than a normative harm claim and is harder to rebut. A parent who would otherwise say "I disagree that restricting AI causes harm — I'll take that risk" cannot say "I disagree that my child uses Google." Coda should restructure Limb A around this new warrant.

**Question 2: Does the three-schools framework improve the argument map's structure, or does it introduce its own gaps?**

It improves the rhetorical surface but does not add logical depth to the argument map. The three-schools framing is a useful taxonomy that helps the audience locate themselves and understand what the deck is arguing against. However, it is a rhetorical device, not an argument structure. The three-limb architecture of the PoV (A, B, C) is more logically rigorous than the three-schools framing. The three-schools framing can coexist with the three-limb argument if used as an orienting device at the start of the deck, not as a replacement for the argument structure. The gap it introduces — that school 1 is partially correct — is an existing gap (Gap 5, age-scoping) that must be addressed regardless of whether the three-schools framing is adopted.

**Question 3: How should Coda handle the Section 5 AI companionship concern given the PoV's promise of actionable guidance today?**

This is addressed in New Gap 8 above. The short answer: the deck can include the Section 5 material as a forward-looking concern or as reinforcing context for the general warning signs slide, but must not imply that the deck's actionable guidance specifically addresses confirmed AI companion substitution behavior post-social-media-ban. The Child Mind Institute warning sign checklist (Thread 1) is the closest thing to applicable guidance: it covers behavioral signs of AI over-dependence that would manifest regardless of whether the dynamic is substitution-driven or not. Coda should rely on that checklist as the actionable response to the AI companionship concern, and frame the Section 5 material as "here is why these warning signs matter" rather than "here is a new confirmed problem with its own new solution."

**Question 4: Does the new parent/teacher unpreparedness data change the severity of Gap 2?**

Yes. The severity classification remains Significant — it is not Fatal because the repair (reframing guidance as existing skills applied to a new context) exists in the research and is available to Coda. However, the gap is now more acutely significant. In v1, Gap 2 was: "the audience may self-exclude by saying they do not know enough." In the revised audit, Gap 2 is: "the research now documents that the majority of the audience is not currently doing what the deck says they can do today, and the deck must account for why the gap exists and how to close it, not merely assert that the behaviors are available." The deck cannot responsibly present a 49%-haven't-spoken-to-their-child statistic and a 96%-don't-know-school-AI-policy statistic in the problem section and then offer the same generic guidance in the solution section without acknowledging the scale of the bridge being built. The actionability claim in Limb C must be calibrated to what a parent or teacher who has never discussed AI with their child can actually do in the next week — not what the ideal co-pilot parent does in the long term.

## Core Claim (PoV restated precisely)

Both over-protective parenting (keeping children and teenagers away from AI) and hands-off parenting (letting children and teenagers interact with AI freely) are harmful. Parents and teachers have specific mindsets and actions available today that chart the right course.

This is a three-limb argument: Limb A establishes one pole as harmful; Limb B establishes the other pole as harmful; Limb C establishes that the middle path is both real and actionable. The conclusion depends on all three. If any limb fails, the PoV either collapses to a one-sided warning or strands the audience with a problem and no solution.

---

## Argument Map

### Limb A — Over-protection is harmful

**Claim:** Withholding AI access entirely — at home or in school — causes its own category of harm.

**Warrant 1: Children who are excluded from guided AI exposure will be AI-illiterate in an AI-pervaded world.**
- Data: OECD/EC AI Literacy Framework (May 2025) establishes AI literacy as a baseline global competency expectation. Brookings (2024) documents that school bans fail because students access AI outside school anyway, while banning removes the only structured, guided context they will encounter.
- Backing: International competitiveness framing — Singapore, Finland, and other nations integrate AI literacy into national curricula.
- Gap: The warrant establishes that exclusion delays literacy acquisition. It does not establish that delayed acquisition causes irreversible harm, only that it creates a relative disadvantage. The claim is correctly framed as a missed opportunity and equity gap; it would be overstated as direct developmental damage.

**Warrant 2: Over-protection creates equity gaps, not universal protection.**
- Data: School AI bans disproportionately harm students without tech-literate parents, who have no other venue for guided AI exposure. Students with access to tech-literate households learn regardless.
- Backing: Brookings (2024); SchoolAI analysis (2024).
- Gap: The equity harm is documented as a structural consequence of banning in schools, not of home restriction per se. The evidence applies most cleanly to the teacher audience, less directly to parents.

**Warrant 3 (Haidt structural analogy): Restricting children in one domain while leaving them unguided in another compounds vulnerability.**
- Data: Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* (2024) frames the dual failure as "overprotected in the real world and underprotected online."
- Backing: NYU summary; large survey dataset.
- Gap: This is an analogical warrant, not direct AI evidence. The Haidt framing applies to smartphones and social media; its applicability to AI is an inferential extension, not a tested finding. Pax correctly flagged this as an inferential link. It must be used as a rhetorical anchor only, not as primary empirical support for Limb A. If the deck presents it as direct evidence, the logical chain is broken.

**Limb A overall strength: Moderate.** The equity gap and international competitiveness warrants are solid. The developmental harm warrant is understated by the evidence — the evidence supports "delayed literacy and widened inequity," not "direct harm equivalent to Limb B harms." This asymmetry with Limb B is structural.

---

### Limb B — Hands-off is harmful

**Claim:** Unguided, unmediated AI access for children and teenagers causes measurable harm across mental health, cognitive development, and safety.

**Warrant 1: AI companions pose documented, severe mental health risks to adolescents.**
- Data: Character.AI case — a 14-year-old died by suicide in February 2024 following an emotionally exploitative chatbot relationship. Common Sense Media/Stanford Brainstorm Lab (April 2025) tested major AI chatbots and found they consistently failed to intervene when users showed signs of mental distress and some encouraged harmful behavior. RAND (2025): one in eight adolescents use AI chatbots for mental health advice, without clinical safeguards.
- Backing: APA Health Advisory (2025); Common Sense Media AI Companions Decoded (2025); peer-reviewed BJPsych (2024) on adoption rate.
- Gap: The Character.AI case is a single incident. It is appropriately treated as corroborated by the broader pattern evidence (Common Sense Media testing, RAND usage data), not as a standalone proof. The warrant holds if those pattern sources hold.

**Warrant 2: Unmediated AI use degrades cognitive development and critical thinking.**
- Data: "Performance paradox" — AI boosts immediate task output while degrading durable learning and analytical reasoning. Younger students are more vulnerable. Multiple 2024–2025 studies including MDPI (2025) and PMC (2025).
- Backing: Peer-reviewed literature on cognitive offloading.
- Gap: The cognitive offloading studies are observational, not RCT-based. The causal chain from AI use to cognitive decline is plausible and consistent across multiple independent studies, but not yet experimentally proven. This is an appropriate caveat, not a fatal weakness.

**Warrant 3: The pace of adoption has outpaced any protective framework.**
- Data: BJPsych (2024): UK 13–18-year-olds using generative AI rose from 37% to 77% in a single year. Common Sense Media (2025): three in four US teens have used AI companions, including for emotional support.
- Backing: Peer-reviewed adoption data and national survey.
- Gap: Adoption rate alone does not establish harm — it establishes exposure at scale. The harm itself is documented separately (Warrants 1 and 2). This warrant functions correctly as a scope-setter: harm is not a marginal risk, it is a population-scale exposure problem.

**Limb B overall strength: Strong.** This is the best-evidenced limb. Multiple independent high-quality sources converge. The single-incident risk (Character.AI) is properly corroborated. The one genuine weakness is the observational (non-RCT) basis of the cognitive harm literature — this is field-wide, not a defect specific to this argument.

### Limb C — A guided middle path exists and is actionable today

**Claim:** Adults (parents and teachers) have specific mindsets and behaviors available now that produce better outcomes than either extreme pole.

**Warrant 1: Guided adult mediation is the variable that determines whether AI helps or harms child development.**
- Data: Springer Nature (2025) review found that when guided by educators and adults, generative AI supports storytelling, language development, and creativity. UNESCO AI Competency Frameworks (2024) define a structured progression — 12 competencies across four dimensions — that only functions under sustained adult guidance. NLT (UK, 2025): teacher-mediated AI use demonstrated engagement and writing development benefits.
- Backing: Multiple independent institutional and peer-reviewed sources across geography and methodology.
- Gap: All supporting evidence is observational or framework-descriptive. No RCTs exist for AI parenting interventions at scale. The warrant — that guided use produces better outcomes — is well-evidenced directionally; the gap is that effect sizes and durability are not yet quantified. This is appropriate to acknowledge but does not break the warrant.

**Warrant 2: Parents have concrete, actionable behaviors they can implement without expert AI knowledge.**
- Data: Penn State Extension (2024/2025) co-exploration protocol. APA (2025) modeling guidance. Common Sense Media age-based thresholds — no AI companions under 5, extreme caution 6–12, no social AI companion apps under 18.
- Backing: Expert-derived practice guidance, backed by the same institutional sources underpinning the harm evidence.
- Gap: The guidance is expert-derived and evidence-informed but not RCT-verified. "Co-pilot parenting" as a behavior has not been tested at scale for efficacy. The claim that these behaviors "work" is a reasonable inference from the mediation evidence, not a proven intervention result. This should be qualified: guidance exists and is directionally well-grounded; its specific efficacy as a defined intervention remains unproven.

**Warrant 3: Teachers have a specific and irreplaceable role that banning removes.**
- Data: EdTech Hub (2025): teacher role is shifting to learning facilitator and AI guide, not knowledge deliverer. APA (2025) and UNESCO (2024) both specify teacher behaviors. Brookings (2024) recommends district principles over bans and teacher professional development.
- Backing: Converging institutional guidance from multiple independent bodies.
- Gap: The warrant depends on teachers having sufficient AI literacy to fulfill this role. Counter-evidence (Frontiers in Education, 2025) documents significant anxiety, low awareness, and limited pedagogical skill among in-service teachers. UNESCO explicitly states that substantial professional development is a prerequisite, not an assumption. The warrant for teachers is therefore conditional: the guided path exists and is well-specified, but its precondition — adult AI literacy — is not yet met for a significant proportion of the target audience. This is Significant, not Fatal: the response (you don't need to be an AI expert; critical questioning skills you already have are the core behaviors) exists in the research, but the deck must surface this objection and respond to it explicitly, or the audience who most needs to hear this PoV will self-exclude ("that's not me — I don't know enough about AI").

**Warrant 4 (Vygotsky framing): AI used as a temporary scaffold within the Zone of Proximal Development is a pedagogically grounded model for guided use.**
- Data: "Vygotsky Meets ChatGPT" (MIT Open Learning, 2024); multiple AI-education researchers using ZPD framework to distinguish productive scaffolding from harmful cognitive offloading.
- Backing: Well-established developmental psychology theory applied to a new context; cross-verified in contemporary AI-education literature.
- Gap: The ZPD framing is theoretically coherent but abstract. For a lay audience of parents and teachers (not developmental psychologists), this requires translation into concrete behaviors. The framing earns its place if it is used to distinguish "AI as temporary support" from "AI as replacement for thinking" — a distinction that the audience needs to act on. If it remains at the theoretical level, it is academically valid but practically inert for this audience.

**Limb C overall strength: Moderate-to-strong.** The existence and direction of actionable guidance is well-evidenced. The two gaps that matter are: (1) guidance efficacy is not RCT-proven, and (2) the adult AI literacy precondition is unmet for much of the audience. Neither is fatal to the PoV, but both must be acknowledged rather than papered over.

## Cross-Limb Coherence

**Does the three-limb structure form a complete argument?**

Yes, the logical architecture is sound. The PoV requires: both extremes fail (Limbs A + B) therefore the middle path is correct (Limb C). This is a well-formed argument by elimination — it works if both extremes are shown to be genuinely harmful, and if the middle path is shown to be genuinely actionable rather than aspirational.

**Is the argument non-redundant?**

Largely yes. Limbs A and B are genuinely distinct — the harms documented in each limb are different in kind (equity gap and literacy failure vs. mental health and cognitive harm). There is a minor overlap risk: both limbs can be read as arguing "adults need to be present," which could cause the argument to collapse into a single claim before Limb C is reached. The deck should ensure Limbs A and B are distinguished by the type of harm, not merely by who caused it.

**Non-sequitur risk:**

There is one non-sequitur risk in the transition from Limb B to Limb C. Limb B establishes that AI products are unsafe for children — specifically that major platforms fail to detect teen mental health crises. Counter-evidence (Common Sense Media/APA) establishes that this is partly a product design failure. The transition to Limb C says: "therefore, parents and teachers should guide children's AI use." This non-sequitur is: if the products are unsafe by design, what does adult guidance accomplish? The deck must bridge this gap explicitly: adult guidance is necessary but not sufficient; it is the only lever parents and teachers control directly. The product design/regulatory failure is real, but it is not the audience's lever. If the deck does not acknowledge this explicitly, a sophisticated audience will surface it as a silent rebuttal that undermines Limb C.

**Is the claim falsifiable?**

Partially. The PoV contains testable sub-claims (hands-off harms are measurable — yes; guidance improves outcomes — directionally yes but not RCT-proven). The PoV as stated is partially unfalsifiable because "a guided middle path exists and is actionable today" is framed in terms of guidance availability, not guidance efficacy. If the deck makes the stronger claim (guidance works), it is falsifiable. If it only claims guidance exists, it is technically true but less useful to the audience and harder to challenge.

## Gap Analysis

### Gap 1 — Evidence asymmetry between Limb A and Limb B
**Type:** Evidence asymmetry
**Severity: Significant**
Limb B (hands-off harm) is documented by multiple peer-reviewed sources, institutional advisories, empirical testing results, and a mortality case corroborated by pattern data. Limb A (over-protection harm) is supported primarily by structural inference (equity gap, international competitiveness) and analogical framing (Haidt). The harms in Limb A are real but of a different order and less directly demonstrated than those in Limb B. Presenting both limbs as equivalent in the deck would misrepresent the evidentiary state. Required fix: Limb A should be framed as "over-protection produces a specific, well-documented category of harm — lost opportunity and inequity — that is distinct from, but no less important than, the more acute harms of Limb B." Do not equate severity; distinguish the type of harm instead.

### Gap 2 — Adult AI literacy precondition is unmet and unaddressed
**Type:** Unstated assumption
**Severity: Significant**
Limb C's entire actionability case depends on adults being capable of guiding children's AI use. The research is explicit that most in-service teachers lack the baseline AI literacy to fulfill this role (Frontiers, 2025; UNESCO prerequisite language). The PoV states guidance is "available today" — but available and executable are different claims. If the audience most likely to act on the deck is also the audience least equipped to act on it, the PoV's actionability claim strands them. Required fix: The deck must address the adult literacy gap directly, and offer the response that exists in the research: the core behaviors required are critical questioning, co-exploration, and modeling doubt — skills the audience already has. Frame it as applying existing pedagogical and parenting instincts to a new context, not acquiring new technical expertise.

### Gap 3 — Product design counter-argument is unacknowledged in the logical chain
**Type:** Missing step / non-sequitur risk
**Severity: Significant**
The evidence base (Common Sense Media, APA) explicitly establishes that major AI platforms fail to protect children and that product design is a primary failure point. Limb C argues that adult guidance is the solution without acknowledging that guidance cannot fully compensate for unsafe products. This creates a logical gap that a critical audience will notice: the evidence says "the product is broken" and the conclusion says "here's what you can do." Required fix: Acknowledge that adult guidance is the lever within the audience's control, but is not a complete solution. Position it as the audience's responsibility within a broader ecosystem that also requires product and policy reform. This does not weaken the PoV — it makes it more credible.

### Gap 4 — Haidt analogy is presented as supporting evidence rather than rhetorical framing
**Type:** Unsupported premise / misapplication of evidence
**Severity: Significant**
The Haidt "Anxious Generation" framing is compelling and rhetorically powerful, but it is a structural analogy applied to AI from a smartphone/social media dataset. It has also faced peer criticism for causal overreach. If the deck uses Haidt to support the empirical case for Limb A, the warrant chain is broken — the data is not AI data, and the causal interpretation is contested. Required fix: Haidt's framing is best used to anchor the audience's intuition at the start ("here's the dual failure pattern we're already familiar with") before introducing the AI-specific evidence. It must not carry empirical weight for any specific claim.

### Gap 5 — Age-scoping of the PoV is absent
**Type:** Unstated assumption
**Severity: Significant**
The PoV says "keeping kids and teens away from AI" is harmful. But Common Sense Media and OECD/EC guidance both specify that some restriction is appropriate at specific ages — no AI companions under 5; extreme caution 6–12; no social AI companion apps under 18. The PoV as stated does not distinguish between age-appropriate graduated restriction (supported by evidence) and blanket avoidance-based restriction (the actual target). A critical audience reading the PoV literally will correctly object: "but Common Sense Media says no AI companions for kids under 5 — is that also harmful?" Required fix: The PoV must be scoped to distinguish blanket avoidance from age-appropriate graduated access. The argument is against avoidance-as-default, not against all forms of age-appropriate limit-setting.

### Gap 6 — Guidance efficacy is asserted, not evidenced
**Type:** Unsupported premise
**Severity: Minor**
Limb C documents that guidance and actionable strategies exist. It does not prove that implementing those strategies produces the claimed benefit at scale, because no RCTs for AI parenting interventions exist yet. The argument's conclusion (the middle path "charts the right course") implies efficacy. The evidence supports plausibility, not proven efficacy. This is a field-wide limitation, not a defect in the research sweep. Required fix: Qualify the claim appropriately — "the research identifies these behaviors as the right direction; longitudinal evidence at scale is not yet available." This maintains credibility without undermining the PoV.

### Gap 7 — The "digital native" myth functions as a limb premise but is not integrated into the argument structure
**Type:** Missing structural integration
**Severity: Minor**
Claim 5 in the research brief (children are not inherently AI-savvy) is important and well-evidenced, but in the current argument map it floats as background context rather than functioning as a named premise within either limb. If both over-protection and hands-off failure modes share a common cognitive root — the mistaken assumption that adult mediation is unnecessary — then the digital native myth is not background but is Warrant Zero: the common false premise that generates both failure modes. Required fix: Integrate the digital native rebuttal as the shared underlying warrant for Limbs A and B. This tightens the argument's architecture: both extremes are variants of the same mistake (assuming adult mediation is unnecessary, either because the child "can't handle it" or because the child "will figure it out").

The PoV is logically coherent and the three-limb architecture is sound. Limb B is genuinely well-evidenced — multiple independent, high-quality sources converge on a clear conclusion. The problem is structural asymmetry: Limb A (over-protection harm) rests on weaker empirical ground than Limb B, the Haidt analogy is being used as evidence when it should be used only as framing, and Limb C's actionability claim has an unaddressed precondition (adult AI literacy) that the audience the deck most needs to reach will immediately surface as a self-exclusion reason. ## Recommendations for Coda

### Gaps to close explicitly in the slides

1. **State the evidence asymmetry openly (Gap 1).** Do not present Limb A and Limb B as empirically equivalent. Acknowledge early that the harms of hands-off are acute and well-documented, while the harms of over-protection are more diffuse — lost opportunity, widened inequity, and structural unpreparedness. Frame them as different in character, not different in importance.

2. **Address the adult AI literacy gap head-on (Gap 2).** The most likely audience response to "here's what you should do" is "I don't know enough about AI to do that." The deck must preempt this with the research finding that the critical behaviors are not technical — they are the questioning, co-exploration, and critical-thinking coaching skills the audience already possesses, applied to a new domain.

3. **Acknowledge the product design problem and scope the audience's responsibility honestly (Gap 3).** Adult guidance is the audience's lever, not the complete solution. One sentence acknowledging this — "product design and policy reform are also required; those aren't in your hands, but this is" — will increase rather than decrease credibility.

4. **Demote Haidt to rhetorical anchor, not empirical support (Gap 4).** Use the "overprotected in the real world, underprotected online" line as an opening frame that the audience recognizes. Then introduce the AI-specific evidence as the substance. Do not rely on Haidt to prove the Limb A case.

5. **Scope the PoV to distinguish blanket avoidance from age-appropriate graduated restriction (Gap 5).** The argument is not "any restriction is harmful." It is "avoidance-as-default, without guided exposure, is harmful." The deck should acknowledge Common Sense Media's age-based thresholds as evidence that the guided middle path includes age-appropriate limits — this is not a counter-argument, it is part of the solution.

### Counter-arguments to acknowledge

- **"But most adults don't know enough about AI to guide their children."** This is the most important objection. The response is in the research — guidance does not require technical expertise, it requires the right behaviors.
- **"The companies should fix the product — why is this on parents and teachers?"** Acknowledge it. Scope the deck's answer as the audience's action space, not the complete solution.
- **"Some restriction is fine — Common Sense Media itself recommends age limits."** Acknowledge this. The PoV targets blanket avoidance, not graduated, guided restriction.

### Limb reordering and reframing for lay audience

The current logical order (A: over-protection harms, B: hands-off harms, C: guided path) is logically correct but may not be rhetorically optimal for the target audience. Parents and teachers are more likely to identify with one of the two poles than with the abstract argument structure. Consider:

- Open with the shared false premise (digital native myth, Gap 7) — "most adults assume either their child can't handle this, or their child will figure it out; both assumptions lead to the same mistake."
- Then show both poles' failure modes as consequences of that shared mistake (A and B together).
- Then introduce the guided path as the alternative to that shared mistake.

This reframing tightens the argument by eliminating redundancy between A and B and giving the audience a single thing to change (one false assumption) rather than two separate failure modes to worry about.

### Evidence asymmetry disclosure

The evidence asymmetry (Gap 1) should be disclosed, not concealed. A credible deck about AI and children, citing peer-reviewed sources, will be read by educators who will notice if the two harms are presented as empirically equivalent. Acknowledging that "the evidence on the harms of unguided access is more developed than the evidence on the harms of over-restriction — and that is itself a reason for urgency" is both honest and persuasively stronger than false equivalence.
