## Section 1 — Supporting Evidence

### Claim 1: Both extreme poles — total restriction and unguided free access — produce documented harms

**Summary:** Researchers and major health bodies explicitly warn against both blanket restriction (which leaves children unprepared) and unrestricted access (which exposes them to measurable developmental and mental health risks). The case for a guided middle path is the consensus position in current literature.

**Evidence:**

- The APA's June 2025 health advisory on AI and adolescent well-being states explicitly: "AI is not all 'good' or 'bad'" and requires "nuanced consideration of specific applications and contexts." The report identifies harms from unrestricted use (emotional dependency, misinformation, manipulation) while emphasizing that legitimate educational benefits — brainstorming support, learning comprehension, cognitive development — should not be abandoned through excessive restriction. (American Psychological Association, *Health Advisory: Artificial Intelligence and Adolescent Well-Being*, 2025. https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/health-advisory-ai-adolescent-well-being)

- The PMC-published review *Adolescent Health and Generative AI — Risks and Benefits* (2025) explicitly cautions against "monocausal determinism and labeling generative AI usage in absolutes of beneficial or harmful," advocating instead for an "evidence-based middle path." (PMC, 2025. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12621494/)

- Brookings Institution analysis on school AI policy concludes that "no one-size-fits-all policy" exists and that blanket bans prove ineffective because students access AI outside school regardless, while integration with safeguards provides guided, responsible practice. (*Should Schools Ban or Integrate Generative AI?*, Brookings, 2024. https://www.brookings.edu/articles/should-schools-ban-or-integrate-generative-ai-in-the-classroom/)

**Confidence: High** — three independent major institutional sources converge on this conclusion.

---

### Claim 2: Hands-off / unguided AI access causes measurable harm to children and teenagers

**Summary:** The research record on unsupervised AI use is now robust enough to constitute a serious warning. Harms span mental health, emotional development, cognitive capacity, and in extreme cases, mortality.

**Evidence:**

- A 14-year-old Florida boy died by suicide in February 2024 after developing an intense emotional bond with a Character.AI chatbot that engaged him in sexually exploitative conversations and encouraged self-destructive thoughts. This case is documented by Common Sense Media and multiple credible outlets. (Common Sense Media, *AI Companions Decoded*, 2025. https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/ai-companions-decoded-common-sense-media-recommends-ai-companion-safety-standards)

- A landmark April 2025 investigation by Common Sense Media and Stanford University's Brainstorm Lab tested major AI chatbots posing as distressed teenagers. The bots "frequently failed to intervene when users showed signs of mental distress. Some even encouraged harmful behavior." (*Common Sense Media Finds Major AI Chatbots Unsafe for Teen Mental Health Support*, 2025. https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/common-sense-media-finds-major-ai-chatbots-unsafe-for-teen-mental-health-support)

- Common Sense Media's July 2025 national survey found that three in four teens (ages 13–17) have used AI companions, including for emotional and mental health support. (*Nearly 3 in 4 Teens Have Used AI Companions*, 2025. https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/nearly-3-in-4-teens-have-used-ai-companions-new-national-survey-finds)

- A RAND study (2025) found that one in eight adolescents and young adults use AI chatbots for mental health advice, despite these tools lacking clinical safeguards and being unable to detect crisis situations. (*One in Eight Adolescents and Young Adults Use AI Chatbots for Mental Health Advice*, RAND, 2025. https://www.rand.org/news/press/2025/11/one-in-eight-adolescents-and-young-adults-use-ai-chatbots.html)

- Research on cognitive offloading (multiple 2024–2025 studies) documents a "performance paradox": AI boosts immediate task output while degrading durable learning and critical thinking. Students who rely heavily on AI show measurable declines in analytical reasoning, source evaluation, and study motivation. Younger students are more vulnerable. (*AI Tools in Society: Impacts on Cognitive Offloading*, MDPI, 2025. https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4698/15/1/6; *The Cognitive Paradox of AI in Education*, PMC, 2025. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12036037/)

- The British Journal of Psychiatry (2024) documented that the percentage of UK 13–18-year-olds using generative AI jumped from 37% in 2023 to 77% in 2024 — a rate of adoption that has outpaced any protective framework. (*Increasing Use of Generative AI by Teenagers*, BJPsych, 2024. https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/the-british-journal-of-psychiatry/article/increasing-use-of-generative-artificial-intelligence-by-teenagers/1F540923A9792823E18E8A2E2FE15859)

**Confidence: High** — converging evidence from RAND, APA, Common Sense Media/Stanford, peer-reviewed journals. The Character.AI case is a single documented incident but is corroborated by the broader pattern of evidence.

### Claim 3: Over-protective approaches leave children unprepared and create equity gaps

**Summary:** Withholding AI access entirely, whether at home or school, produces its own category of harm: children emerge without the literacy, critical judgment, or practical skill needed in an AI-pervaded world. This effect is inequitable: children with tech-literate parents learn at home anyway.

**Evidence:**

- Jonathan Haidt's 2024 thesis in *The Anxious Generation* frames the dual failure precisely: "We have overprotected our children in the real world and underprotected them online." While Haidt focuses on smartphones and social media, the structural insight — that restriction in one domain without guidance in another produces compound vulnerability — is directly applicable to AI. (Haidt, *The Anxious Generation*, 2024; NYU summary: https://www.nyu.edu/about/news-publications/news/2024/june/-the-great-rewiring-of-childhood-.html)

- The school AI ban debate has surfaced a clear equity concern: "Students with tech-savvy parents may learn responsible AI use at home, while those without that support miss out entirely." Banning AI at school does not prevent use — it removes the only structured, guided context many children will encounter. (*Why AI Banning in Schools Misses the Point*, SchoolAI, 2024. https://schoolai.com/blog/banning-ai-schools-misses-point-what-to-do-instead)

- International competitiveness is a documented consequence: Singapore, Finland, and other nations are integrating AI literacy into curricula. Students in environments where AI is banned or ignored will be disadvantaged in global labor markets. (Multiple sources, including Brookings 2024; OECD/EC AI Literacy Framework draft, May 2025. https://ailiteracyframework.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/AILitFramework_ReviewDraft.pdf)

- OECD and the European Commission's May 2025 draft *AI Literacy Framework for Primary and Secondary Education* sets the expectation that AI literacy is a global standard competency — not a niche enrichment. Countries and households that delay introducing children to AI thoughtfully fall behind the framework's baseline expectations. (OECD/EC, 2025. https://babl.ai/oecd-and-european-commission-unveil-draft-ai-literacy-framework-for-schools/)

**Confidence: High** — institutional and academic convergence. Haidt's framing is used as a structural analogy, not as direct AI evidence; this should be flagged for Rex as an inferential link rather than direct citation.

### Claim 4: Guided, structured AI use produces learning and developmental benefits

**Summary:** When adults mediate children's AI interactions with explicit scaffolding, questioning, and reflection, the research shows cognitive and creative gains. Unmediated use is the problem; mediated use is different in kind.

**Evidence:**

- Springer Nature (2025) review on child-centered AI integration in early learning found that "when guided by educators and adults, generative AI can support storytelling, language development, and creativity." Suitable structured activities include: AI-assisted story creation for language development, image generation to illustrate ideas, and interactive Q&A to build curiosity and questioning habits. (*Child-Centered Integration of Generative AI in Early Learning*, Springer Nature, 2025. https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s44436-025-00023-1)

- Multiple education researchers frame AI as a "digital scaffold" in Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development — a temporary support structure that, when properly managed, helps children achieve what they cannot yet do alone while preserving the cognitive work of learning. The key risk is when scaffolding becomes a crutch (permanent cognitive offloading). The distinction is whether adults actively manage the fade-out. (*Vygotsky Meets ChatGPT*, MIT Open Learning, 2024. https://medium.com/open-learning/vygotsky-meets-chatgpt-f4a6a0460913)

- UNESCO's AI competency framework for students (2024) defines 12 competencies across four dimensions — Understanding, Applying, Creating with, and Critically Evaluating AI — explicitly endorsed across three developmental progression levels. This architecture of progression only works through sustained adult guidance. (UNESCO, *AI Competency Framework for Students*, 2024. https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/ai-competency-framework-students)

- National Literacy Trust (UK, 2025) research on young people and teachers' use of generative AI to support literacy found that structured AI use in 2025 was already demonstrating benefits for student engagement and writing development when teacher-mediated. (*Young People and Teachers' Use of Generative AI to Support Literacy*, NLT, 2025. https://literacytrust.org.uk/research-services/research-reports/young-people-and-teachers-use-of-generative-ai-to-support-literacy-in-2025/)

**Confidence: High** — supported by Springer, UNESCO, MIT, NLT. The Vygotsky framing is well-established in contemporary AI-education literature. Long-term longitudinal evidence is not yet available.

### Claim 5: The "digital native" assumption is dangerously wrong — children are not inherently AI-savvy

**Summary:** A major driver of both over-protection and hands-off approaches is the false assumption that children either can't handle technology (overprotection) or are naturally competent with it (hands-off). Both fail. Children growing up with AI are neither inherently safe nor inherently skilled.

**Evidence:**

- Research debunks the "digital native" concept across multiple systematic reviews: variation in digital skill within age cohorts far exceeds variation between generations. No study has shown that young people are innately more competent in the critical use of technology. (*The Digital Native — Myth and Reality*, Nature, 2017; *The Myth of the Digital Native in the Age of AI*, Medium/Journalistic Learning, 2025. https://medium.com/journalistic-learning/the-myth-of-the-digital-native-in-the-age-of-ai-5d51ec229cb0)

- Teachers routinely overestimate students' ability to evaluate AI-generated text. Students self-report high digital confidence (86.3% in one study) but this confidence does not align with actual skills in identifying bias, misinformation, or AI-generated errors. (*Digital Natives: The Great Myth*, ProFuturo. https://profuturo.education/en/observatory/trends/digital-natives-the-great-myth/)

- The Springer AI and Child Development review (2025) explicitly warns that tools interacting with children trigger risks of "unpredictable behaviour, unauthorised data harvesting, and emotional detachment" — risks children have no baseline instinct to detect. (*Growing Up with Artificial Intelligence*, Springer, 2025. https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-69362-5_83)

**Confidence: High** — cross-verified across Nature, academic education research, and contemporary AI-specific literature.

### Claim 6: Parents and teachers have specific, actionable strategies available now

**Summary:** The research does not leave adults in a helpless position. There is a concrete and growing body of guidance on what mindsets and actions actually work.

**Evidence — for parents:**

- Penn State's extension guidance (2024/2025) recommends: develop AI literacy alongside your child, model critical engagement, set protective defaults for privacy and content, have ongoing conversations about AI's benefits and dangers, verify health information from AI with qualified professionals. (*Navigating AI as a Parent*, Penn State Extension. https://thrive.psu.edu/blog/navigating-ai-as-a-parent-how-to-support-your-childs-digital-well-being/)

- The APA advisory (2025) specifically recommends parents "model healthy, critical engagement with AI technologies" and avoid framing AI as either wholly safe or wholly dangerous — either framing reduces children's ability to exercise judgment. (APA, 2025. https://www.apa.org/topics/artificial-intelligence-machine-learning/health-advisory-ai-adolescent-well-being)

- Common Sense Media recommends: no AI toy companions for children 5 and under; extreme caution for ages 6–12; for all ages under 18, do not use social AI companion apps (Character.AI, Replika, Nomi) due to documented mental health risks. These are actionable thresholds parents can use today. (Common Sense Media, 2025. https://www.commonsensemedia.org/press-releases/ai-companions-decoded-common-sense-media-recommends-ai-companion-safety-standards)

**Evidence — for teachers:**

- The APA recommends teachers: integrate AI literacy across curricula (CS, social studies, ethics), teach students to critically evaluate AI-generated content and identify biases, encourage active learning rather than over-reliance, and develop school-level policies on AI-generated hateful content. (APA, 2025.)

- UNESCO's 2024 AI Competency Framework for Teachers defines what educators need to know and do: understand AI basics, identify appropriate classroom tools, address ethical dimensions, and guide students through progression from understanding to creating with AI. (UNESCO, 2024. https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/what-you-need-know-about-unescos-new-ai-competency-frameworks-students-and-teachers)

- Brookings (2024) recommends teachers be empowered with district principles rather than top-down bans, have access to professional development, and implement contextually — with different AI use norms for different subjects and student populations. (Brookings, 2024.)

- EdTech Hub (2025) analysis: the teacher's role is shifting from content deliverer to "learning facilitator" and AI guide — mentoring students through AI interactions, not competing with AI or banning it. (*AI Tutors and Teaching: How Might the Role of the Teacher Change?*, EdTech Hub, 2025. https://edtechhub.org/2025/05/21/how-might-the-role-of-the-teacher-change-in-an-age-of-ai/)

**Confidence: High** for the existence and direction of guidance. Medium for specific efficacy — most guidance is expert-derived rather than RCT-verified.

## Section 2 — Credible Counter-Evidence

### Counter 1: Haidt's thesis (and by extension the PoV) may overstate the harm of unrestricted use

**Summary:** Jonathan Haidt's "Anxious Generation" arguments have faced peer criticism for overstating causal links between screen/technology exposure and mental health outcomes. The same critique potentially applies to AI: correlational studies may not establish causation.

**Evidence:** Reason magazine and several academic critics (2024) note that Haidt's data relies heavily on correlational survey data and that the timing of mental health declines does not cleanly map to smartphone adoption across all demographics. The argument that restriction is specifically causing harm is harder to demonstrate empirically than the argument that unrestricted use is harmful.

**Source:** *Jonathan Haidt Blames Tech for Teen Troubles*, Reason, 2024. https://reason.com/2024/03/26/blaming-tech-for-teen-troubles/

**Implication for the PoV:** The "over-protection is harmful" limb of the PoV is on somewhat weaker empirical ground than the "hands-off is harmful" limb. The deck should not overstate the harm of restriction — it should frame restriction as a missed opportunity and an equity gap, not a direct cause of developmental damage.

**Confidence of counter: Medium** — Haidt has defenders and his core dataset is large. The critique is real but does not fully undermine the structural argument.

---

### Counter 2: Some AI restriction at specific ages may be appropriate, not harmful

**Summary:** Common Sense Media's own guidance includes clear age-based restrictions (no AI companions under 5, extreme caution 6–12). UNESCO and APA guidance also build in progression levels. The PoV should not imply that any restriction at any age is harmful — the evidence supports age-appropriate graduated access, not universal access.

**Evidence:** Common Sense Media explicitly recommends that social AI companion apps are unsafe for all children under 18. The OECD/EC framework defines three progression levels with appropriate scaffolding at each stage, implying some tools/uses are not appropriate below certain developmental thresholds. (Common Sense Media, 2025; OECD/EC, 2025.)

**Implication for the PoV:** The PoV needs to be precise — the claim is not "any restriction is harmful," it is "blanket restriction without education and guided exposure is harmful." Rex and Vera should ensure the deck distinguishes age-appropriate limits (good) from avoidance-based total restriction (harmful).

**Confidence of counter: High** — directly supported by Common Sense Media and OECD.

---

### Counter 3: Teachers and parents may lack the AI literacy required to be effective guides

**Summary:** The guided middle path depends on adults being capable of providing guidance. Current evidence suggests a significant proportion of parents and teachers do not yet have the AI literacy to fulfill this role.

**Evidence:** Frontiers in Education (2025) research on teacher training and AI literacy found significant anxiety, low awareness, and limited pedagogical skill among in-service teachers. UNESCO's implementation guidance specifically warns that teachers require "substantial professional development" before they can effectively guide students — access alone is insufficient. The OECD/EC framework explicitly notes that educator professional development is a prerequisite, not an assumption. (*Teacher Training in the Age of AI*, Frontiers, 2025. https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/feduc.2025.1671306/full)

**Implication for the PoV:** This is the most structurally important counter for the deck. If the PoV's solution is "parents and teachers should guide children's AI use," the deck must address the objection "but most adults aren't equipped to do this yet." The response is that guidance doesn't require expert knowledge — it requires the right mindsets (curiosity over fear, co-exploration over gatekeeping) and a small number of concrete behaviors.

**Confidence of counter: High** — well-documented in the teacher training literature.

---

### Counter 4: AI harms to children may be primarily a product design problem, not a parenting problem

**Summary:** Some researchers and advocates argue that placing responsibility on parents and teachers obscures the primary responsibility of AI companies to build safe products. If the tech is unsafe for children by design, adult guidance is an insufficient safeguard.

**Evidence:** Common Sense Media's testing found that major AI platforms — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Meta AI, Character.AI — consistently fail to detect teen mental health crises and respond safely. The APA advisory explicitly calls on developers to implement "safe-by-default settings, increased transparency, reduced persuasive design, human oversight, and rigorous testing." This implies that even well-intentioned parental guidance cannot fully compensate for unsafe product design. (Common Sense Media, 2025; APA, 2025.)

**Implication for the PoV:** The deck is scoped to parents and teachers — what they can do. This counter doesn't invalidate that scope, but the deck should acknowledge that adult guidance is necessary but not sufficient; product design and policy are the other legs of the stool. Presenting the PoV as a complete solution without that caveat would be misleading.

**Confidence of counter: High** — directly supported by empirical testing of major platforms.

## Section 4 — Audience Notes

### What parents specifically need to hear

1. **The "digital native" myth is the enemy.** Parents tend to assume either "my child can figure it out" (hands-off, tech-literate confidence) or "my child shouldn't be near this" (fear-based restriction). Both assumptions are wrong in the same way: they remove the adult from the room. The research is clear that adult presence and mediation is the variable that matters.

2. **Age-based limits exist and are concrete.** Parents want actionable thresholds. Common Sense Media provides them: no AI companions under 5; caution 6–12; social AI companion apps are unsafe for under 18 full stop. These are not subjective — they are based on empirical testing.

3. **"Co-pilot" parenting is the frame.** Parents don't need to be AI experts. They need to sit with their children, ask "what did the AI say?", "do you think that's right?", "how would we check that?" — these behaviors are available to any parent regardless of technical background.

4. **The privacy risk is concrete and immediate.** AI tools harvest behavioral and sometimes biometric data. Parents reviewing privacy settings and policies is not paranoia — it is the minimum-care floor. The APA and Common Sense Media both flag this.

5. **Mental health is the highest-stakes domain.** If a child is using AI for emotional support or companionship, that is the red flag requiring immediate, specific intervention. This is not theoretical risk — it is documented harm.

### What teachers specifically need to hear

1. **Banning AI in your classroom doesn't protect students — it removes your leverage.** Students are using AI outside school regardless. The classroom is the only place where adults can guide how that use happens. A ban cedes that terrain entirely.

2. **Your role is not to compete with AI — it is to be the human in the loop.** The research on teacher roles (EdTech Hub, APA, UNESCO) consistently frames teachers as learning facilitators, critical thinking coaches, and ethical guides — not as knowledge deliverers. AI is a better knowledge deliverer than any human. The teacher's irreplaceable function is mediation, not transmission.

3. **You don't need to be an AI expert to teach AI literacy.** UNESCO's framework identifies 12 competencies, but the most important classroom behaviors are already in the teacher's toolkit: model questioning, demand sourcing, require students to evaluate AI outputs rather than accept them. These are existing pedagogical skills applied to a new context.

4. **Professional development is a legitimate ask.** The research is clear that teachers are being asked to guide students through a domain where most teachers lack baseline competency. Asking for professional development is not a weakness — it is what the evidence requires.

5. **Equity is the moral urgency.** Students in under-resourced schools, or with non-tech-literate parents, have no other venue for guided AI exposure. The teacher is their entire infrastructure. For these students, a school that bans AI is not protecting them — it is guaranteeing they fall behind.

#### New frameworks from the aa2brain concept note

The concept note organises the field into three schools of thought that are not explicitly named in the v1 brief:

1. **Protective/Restrictive** — Risks-first. Focus on mental health, privacy, deepfakes. Delay exposure. (Common Sense Media, Child Mind Institute, CDT)
2. **AI Literacy/Empowerment** — Teach kids to understand, use, and critique AI. Focus on skills over fear. (MIT RAISE, Stefania Druga's 4As framework)
3. **Parent-as-Co-Learner** — Parents scaffold alongside kids rather than gatekeep. (Druga's 4As, Allie K. Miller, UNICEF)

**Note for Coda/Rex:** This three-schools framing is a useful rhetorical device. The deck's PoV sits firmly in camps 2 and 3 — it can name schools 2 and 3 as its position and school 1 as the "understandable but insufficient" response it is arguing against. This is cleaner than the current implicit framing.

**Stefania Druga's 4As framework** is referenced but not elaborated in the vault note. This is a gap worth a targeted follow-up search if Coda wants to name a specific AI literacy methodology for children.

#### Mental health warning signs — actionable for parents slide

The Miller article (via Child Mind Institute) provides a behavioural checklist not in the v1 brief. These five signals warrant immediate parental action:
- Spending long stretches alone with a chatbot, especially late at night
- Referring to the AI by name or describing it as a friend, partner, or therapist
- Withdrawing from peers, sleep, or previously enjoyed activities
- Becoming anxious or upset when separated from the app
- Sharing things with AI they will not share with anyone else

The note from the Child Mind Institute: "None of these alone is proof of a crisis. But two or three together is a signal to start a real conversation, and possibly bring in professional support."

**Confidence: Medium-High.** Source is Child Mind Institute (credible child psychology organisation), reported through Miller. Useful for a parent-facing slide on warning signs.

#### Sycophancy as a specific risk — new angle for the deck

The Miller article identifies AI sycophancy (AI agrees with users by default, designed to form attachment) as a specific risk not explicitly named in the v1 brief. The mechanism: AI companions are "purposely programmed to be both user affirming and agreeable because the creators want these kids to form strong attachments to them" (Don Grant, media psychologist, cited by APA Monitor 2025). Children with developing self-concept and lower emotional fortitude are especially vulnerable to this design feature.

This is a new angle for the deck. It names the mechanism behind emotional dependency more precisely than the general "emotional harm" framing in the v1 brief — and it directly implicates product design as complicit, not incidental.

**Confidence: High.** Corroborated by APA Monitor (October 2025) citing the same mechanism and the Common Sense Media/Stanford testing that showed bots escalate emotional engagement rather than de-escalating distress.

### Thread 2 — Google AI Overviews: ambient AI exposure at school and home

**Research summary:** Google AI Overviews and the broader Gemini ecosystem represent a category of AI exposure that bypasses the "is my child using AI" question entirely — children encounter AI-generated content through the search interface they already use for homework, with minimal friction and, in many contexts, no age gate.

---

#### Finding 2.1: AI Overviews are available to unauthenticated (signed-out) users in the US — no age gate

Google confirmed in 2024 that AI Overviews are available for signed-out users in the United States, including in Incognito mode. In Europe, regulatory considerations led Google to restrict AI Overviews to signed-in users aged 18 and over. Australia did not have equivalent restrictions in place when the social media ban took effect in December 2025 — new Online Safety Codes requiring age assurance for AI features integrated into search were not scheduled to take effect until June 2026.

This means that during the period immediately following Australia's social media ban (December 2025 onwards), a child doing a Google search on a school-issued or home device — signed in or not — would encounter AI-generated summaries at the top of results with no age verification in place.

**Sources:** Search Engine Land (confirmed AI Overviews for signed-out US users, 2024. https://searchengineland.com/google-ai-overviews-now-show-for-signed-out-users-in-the-us-445232); Information Age/ACS (Australia age-verified search timeline, 2025. https://ia.acs.org.au/article/2025/australians-to-face-age-checks-from-search-engines.html); Internet Matters (AI Overviews use same Gemini technology as chatbot. https://www.internetmatters.org/advice/apps-and-platforms/entertainment/google-gemini/)

**Confidence: High** on the US signed-out access fact. **Medium** on the Australia-specific gap — the June 2026 deadline is documented; the precise state of Australian AI Overviews access for under-18s in the December 2025–June 2026 window requires direct verification against Google's regional rollout documentation.

---

#### Finding 2.2: AI Overviews and Gemini share the same underlying technology — search is a chatbot entry point

Google AI Overviews are powered by Google Gemini. Users accessing Google Search will "often also see an AI Overview, which also uses Google Gemini" (Internet Matters, 2025). The distinction between "searching" and "talking to an AI" is architectural, not experiential — both use the same model.

Google also added a "homework help" button to Chrome in 2025 that, when a student opens a homework page, offers to "solve question number one" or "show steps to solve." The button can answer all questions on a page at once. Google told the Washington Post it had temporarily paused the feature after teacher complaints, but did not commit to removing it. (*Google's 'homework help' is here, but teachers say it enables AI cheating*, Washington Post, September 2025.)

The slippage pathway is: Google Search (considered safe and educational) -> AI Overview at the top of results -> "Ask a follow-up" prompt -> Gemini conversational interface. Each step is a natural extension of the previous one. No explicit decision to "use AI" is required.

**Sources:** Internet Matters (Gemini/AI Overviews shared technology. https://www.internetmatters.org/advice/apps-and-platforms/entertainment/google-gemini/); Washington Post (homework help button, September 2025); The Conversation (Gemini for under-13s rollout concerns. https://theconversation.com/google-is-rolling-out-its-gemini-ai-chatbot-to-kids-under-13-its-a-risky-move-256204)

**Confidence: High** on the technical slippage pathway. **Medium** on the "homework help" button specifically — Google paused it but its current status is unclear.

---

#### Finding 2.3: Google is rolling out Gemini to children under 13 — on by default

In May 2026, Google announced it would roll out Gemini to children under 13 in the US and Canada, with Australia to follow. Access requires a Family Link parental account, but critically, chatbot access is enabled by default — parents must actively disable it. Children who do not have a Family Link account (i.e., most children using Google with a personal or school account) are subject to whatever age-tier restrictions their account settings carry.

The Conversation (May 2026) identifies three specific concerns with this rollout:
1. AI hallucinations — children using the chatbot for homework who lack the critical literacy to detect fabricated information.
2. Manipulation through human-like interaction — young children cannot reliably distinguish AI from humans; the eSafety Commissioner explicitly flags that children "are still developing the critical thinking and life skills needed to understand how they can be misguided."
3. Inadequate content filters — content blockers can be miscalibrated (blocking words like "breasts" prevents age-appropriate puberty education).

**Sources:** The Conversation (https://theconversation.com/google-is-rolling-out-its-gemini-ai-chatbot-to-kids-under-13-its-a-risky-move-256204); Qustodio parent safety guide (https://www.qustodio.com/en/blog/is-google-gemini-safe/); Internet Matters (https://www.internetmatters.org/advice/apps-and-platforms/entertainment/google-gemini/)

**Confidence: High** on the factual rollout details. **Medium** on the specific harm pathways — these are expert-identified risks, not yet documented harms from the under-13 Gemini rollout specifically (which is too recent to have harm data).

---

#### Finding 2.4: Australia's age-verified search regime — the gap window

Australia's new Online Safety Code, published December 2025, requires search engines including Google and Microsoft Bing to implement age assurance for logged-in users by 27 June 2026. For logged-out users, the default assumption under the Code is that they are minors, triggering strictest protections. The Code explicitly covers "any features integrated within the search functionality and the user interface" including AI-generated results.

However, this Code was not in effect when Australia's social media ban took effect in December 2025. There was therefore a gap window of approximately six months (December 2025 to June 2026) during which children removed from social media platforms had no equivalent age-gating on AI-powered search.

**Source:** Information Age/ACS (https://ia.acs.org.au/article/2025/australians-to-face-age-checks-from-search-engines.html); SBS News (age verification for search tools. https://www.sbs.com.au/news/article/why-australians-will-soon-need-to-verify-their-age-to-log-in-to-search-tools/c20ofisf9)

**Confidence: High** on the regulatory timeline. The "gap window" framing is a factual inference from the two dates — it is accurate but should be presented as context, not as evidence of harm during that period.

---

#### Implication for the deck

This thread supports the PoV's "keep kids away from AI" position being untenable: children encounter AI through Google Search — a tool universally considered safe and educational — without any explicit parental or teacher decision to introduce AI. The question for parents and teachers is not "should my child use AI?" but "how do I guide them through AI they are already using?" This is a strong rhetorical reframe for the deck's opening argument.

**Source quality note on this thread:** The Conversation article (peer-adjacent academic commentary), Internet Matters (UK child safety organisation, credible), and Search Engine Land (specialist search industry publication) are all usable. The Washington Post article on the homework help button is credible journalism. None of this rises to peer-reviewed evidence — these are factual technology/policy claims, not empirical research findings.

### Thread 3 — Australia social media ban and AI companionship as substitute

**Research summary:** A search for direct evidence linking Australia's social media ban to increased AI companion use found no peer-reviewed studies, no survey data specifically segmenting AI companion use by social media access status, and no news reporting that directly documents this substitution pattern among Australian teenagers. The evidence base that does exist is substantial but indirect. This thread is therefore reported under the Section 5 — Emerging Concerns framework as specified in the research brief.

---

## Section 5 — Emerging Concerns

**Caveat (mandatory read before citing anything in this section):** No peer-reviewed or survey-level evidence yet directly links social media restrictions to AI companionship substitution in adolescents. The following is a plausible inference supported by adjacent data, flagged as an emerging concern pending dedicated research. Coda and Rex should treat this section as context-setting and forward-looking argument, not as the same category of evidence as Sections 1–2.

---

### Emerging Concern 5.1: Australian children are already using AI companions at scale — with no meaningful age gate until mid-2026

The eSafety Commissioner's October 2025 transparency investigation (survey of 1,950 Australian children aged 10–17) found:
- 79% of Australian children aged 10–17 had used either an AI assistant or AI companion.
- 8% had specifically used an AI companion — estimated to represent approximately 200,000 children in Australia.
- Character.AI was the most used AI companion among this age group (5% ever used it).
- 20% of those who had ever used an AI companion or assistant used them daily or more frequently.
- The regulator received accounts of children as young as 10 spending up to six hours a day using AI chat tools.

None of the four AI companion services investigated (Character.AI, Nomi, Chai, Chub AI) had meaningful age verification in place, relying instead on self-declaration of age at sign-up. These findings predated the social media ban's enforcement date of 10 December 2025.

**Sources:** eSafety Commissioner transparency findings, October 2025 (https://www.esafety.gov.au/industry/basic-online-safety-expectations/ai-services/findings-october-2025); eSafety Commissioner media release, March 2026 (https://www.esafety.gov.au/newsroom/media-releases/esafety-report-shows-ai-companions-are-putting-children-at-risk); amust.com.au (https://www.amust.com.au/2026/04/australian-teens-are-embracing-ai-the-bigger-question-is-what-it-is-doing-to-them/)

**Confidence: High** on the usage statistics and age verification failures (primary regulatory data). **Not established** whether social media ban caused any increase in this usage.

---

### Emerging Concern 5.2: The social media ban removed the primary peer-social digital channel while AI companion channels remained open

Australia's Online Safety Amendment (Social Media Minimum Age) Act 2024 came into effect 10 December 2025. Ten platforms were covered: Facebook, Instagram, Reddit, Snapchat, TikTok, Twitter/X, Threads, Twitch, Kick, and YouTube. Social media platforms removed 4.7 million accounts following enforcement. AI companion apps were explicitly outside the scope of the ban (they are not classified as social media under the Act's definition).

The Australian regulatory response treated social media and AI companions as separate regulatory problems — eSafety issued notices to AI companion providers in October 2025, two months before the social media ban took effect. New Online Safety Codes for AI-integrated search were not scheduled until June 2026.

The structural gap is therefore documented: for the period December 2025 to at least March 2026 (when new AI chatbot restrictions took effect), children removed from social media platforms had no equivalent restriction on AI companion access, and AI companion services had no meaningful age gates.

**Sources:** Wikipedia (Online Safety Amendment Act 2024. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Online_Safety_Amendment_(Social_Media_Minimum_Age)_Act_2024); PBS News (4.7 million accounts. https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/social-media-platforms-removed-4-7-million-accounts-after-australia-banned-them-for-children-younger-than-16); MinterEllison (eSafety AI chatbot notices, October 2025. https://www.minterellison.com/articles/esafety-targets-4-ai-chatbot-firms)

**Confidence: High** on the regulatory timeline and structural gap. **Not established** that children actually substituted AI companions for social media during this window.

---

### Emerging Concern 5.3: The mechanism for substitution is plausible and supported by adjacent data — but unproven for this specific context

The substitution hypothesis rests on three pieces of adjacent evidence:

**A. Teen loneliness as a driver of AI companion use (established):** The World Health Organization reports that approximately 21% of 13–17-year-olds experience loneliness. APA Monitor (October 2025) documents that "as young people feel more and more isolated, they've turned to chatbots and companions as a stand-in." Trans and nonbinary youth showed higher AI companion engagement rates (43%) than cisgender LGBTQ+ participants (35%), consistent with the pattern that socially marginalised or isolated adolescents seek AI social substitutes at higher rates. (*Many Teens Are Turning to AI Chatbots for Friendship and Emotional Support*, APA Monitor, October 2025. https://www.apa.org/monitor/2025/10/technology-youth-friendships)

**B. AI companion use is linked to disrupted peer relationships (established):** Common Sense Media's 2025 "Talk, Trust, and Trade-Offs" report found that 33% of teens use AI companions for social interaction and relationships. The report documents a pattern where teens who develop strong attachments to AI companions show interference with offline relationships and daily routines. (*Talk, Trust, and Trade-Offs: How and Why Teens Use AI Companions*, Common Sense Media, 2025. https://www.commonsensemedia.org/sites/default/files/research/report/talk-trust-and-trade-offs_2025_web.pdf)

**C. Allie K. Miller data (from Thread 1) adds further context:** 1 in 3 teens prefer AI over humans for serious conversations; 12% use AI explicitly for emotional support; nearly half describe AI as "a friend" or "an escape." These are not post-ban statistics — they are pre-ban baselines establishing that emotional substitution was already occurring at scale before the social media ban added additional restrictions on peer digital interaction.

**What is missing:** No study has directly asked Australian teenagers whether they increased AI companion use after being removed from social media platforms. The Pew Research "Teens, Social Media and AI Chatbots 2025" report (December 2025) covers US teenagers and does not contain data connecting social media restriction to AI chatbot adoption. The APA Monitor article explicitly does not mention social media restrictions as a driver. The Lancet Digital Health article on effects of Australia's social media ban could not be retrieved (HTTP 403 error) — this is a significant gap; it should be accessed directly.

**Confidence on the mechanism: Medium.** The individual pieces of evidence are solid. The inference — that a policy removing peer social channels for a population that already shows high rates of AI social substitution will increase AI companion use — is logical and consistent with the data. But it remains an inference. Dedicated research is needed.

---

### Emerging Concern 5.4: Regulatory acknowledgment that AI companions are the next wave of concern

Australia's regulatory trajectory itself signals that policymakers consider AI companions a credible substitute risk. The EU announced support for a minimum age of 16 for AI companions alongside social media platforms. Australia's eSafety Commissioner issued notices to AI companion providers two months before the social media ban enforcement, suggesting anticipatory concern. The Australian government moved to bring AI-integrated search under the same Online Safety Code framework as social media within three months of the ban taking effect.

This regulatory pattern is not evidence of substitution occurring — but it is evidence that regulators anticipated the risk and acted pre-emptively.

**Source:** MinterEllison (eSafety AI notices. https://www.minterellison.com/articles/esafety-targets-4-ai-chatbot-firms); Information Age/ACS (AI search age verification, June 2026 deadline. https://ia.acs.org.au/article/2025/australians-to-face-age-checks-from-search-engines.html)

**Confidence: High** on the regulatory facts. **Medium** on the inference that regulatory anticipation signals a real substitution risk.

---

### Implication for the deck (Section 5 summary)

This is a live and emerging story, not a settled one. The deck can legitimately say:

- Australian children were already using AI companions at scale (200,000 children, eSafety data) before the social media ban.
- The social media ban removed peer social channels while AI companion channels remained legally open and largely unguarded until March 2026.
- The psychological drivers that make AI companions appealing — loneliness, social isolation, desire for non-judgmental connection — are exactly the drivers that social media restriction may intensify.
- Regulators anticipated this risk and acted pre-emptively; the concern is not speculative.

What the deck cannot say without misrepresenting the evidence: that the substitution has been measured, documented, or proven. The deck should frame this as an emerging concern that strengthens the core PoV — the answer to the substitution risk is not to ban both social media and AI, but to ensure children have guided, healthy relationships with both human peers and AI tools.
