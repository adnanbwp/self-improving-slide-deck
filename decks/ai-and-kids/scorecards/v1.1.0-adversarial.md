## Summary

v1.1.0 is primarily a design pass, and its adversarial posture has improved in one specific place: the logical bridge connecting Limb A's inevitability argument to the necessity of adult presence has been moved into the slide body on Slide 4. That resolves the most acute form of CA-1. The other four counter-arguments from v1.0.0 carry over substantially unchanged. The score holds at 6/10 because the improvements are real but narrow. The sycophancy structural insufficiency objection (CA-2) is untouched, the equity objection (CA-3) is untouched, the regulatory scope objection (CA-5) has received partial mitigation via Slide 14's new body text but no explicit justification of the scope choice, and the statistics vulnerability (CA-4) has worsened rather than improved: the confidence caveat that appeared in footnote italic text in v1.0.0 has been removed entirely from the slide body in v1.1.0, with sourcing now appearing as "Common Sense Media, 2024" without qualification. The Lancet Digital Health article (HTTP 403 flagged by Pax) remains unresolved and continues to represent the most significant gap in the social-media-ban-to-AI-companion-substitution evidence chain. One new low-severity issue is introduced: Slide 4's Google/Gemini factual claim is time-sensitive and has an unknown verification window.

The deck remains adversarially resilient against casual challenge. It remains exposed to a well-prepared hostile questioner who has read the primary sources and is looking for three things: the evidence gap on sycophancy-vs-guidance sufficiency, the confidence basis for the 49%/96%/11% statistics, and the absence of a scope justification on the regulatory question.

## Steelmanned PoV

The deck's strongest possible version is unchanged from v1.0.0 and runs as follows. AI has already entered children's lives through tools their families and schools have approved — Google Search, classroom software, family devices — without any explicit parental or institutional decision to introduce it. The May 2026 Google Gemini rollout to under-13s via Family Link accounts makes this concrete and current: the parent who has not made a decision about AI has still made a decision, because Google has made it for them. Given that children are encountering AI regardless, the only variable remaining within adult control is whether an adult is present and engaged when that encounter happens. Research across multiple independent sources consistently identifies adult mediation as the variable separating developmental benefit from documented harm — mental health crises, cognitive atrophy, and emotional exploitation by adversarially designed products. The behaviors required for that mediation are not technical. They are the critical questioning, co-exploration, and modeling of doubt that competent parents and teachers already practice in other domains. The deck asks adults to apply those existing skills to a new context, starting this week. That is a limited and achievable ask. It is the only lever adults have within the regulatory timeline.

## Motivated Opponent Profile

Unchanged from v1.0.0. The most capable hostile audience member is a well-read parent or educator who accepts the deck's harm evidence, agrees that restriction is probably ineffective, and still rejects the conclusion — because they believe adult guidance is structurally too weak to matter against adversarially designed products, or because they are one of the parents the deck identifies as the solution but who occupy the structural problem category instead.

A secondary hostile reader remains the policy advocate who will use the product-design acknowledgment in Slide 14 to argue that the deck's entire framing is a distraction from the correct regulatory target.

A third profile is now more salient in v1.1.0 than it was in v1.0.0: the research-literate audience member (academic, journalist, competing advocacy org) who will note that the 49% statistic on Slide 13 now carries more specific attribution ("Common Sense Media, 2024," "September 2024" in the notes) without any qualification caveat. This reader knows the Common Sense Media 2024 report library and will check. If the figure does not appear in primary form, the increased specificity of the attribution makes the evidentiary failure more damaging, not less.

## Counter-Arguments Mounted

### CA-1 — "If children encounter AI anyway, why does parental guidance matter?" (Impossibility collapses Limb C)
**Severity: Significant** (downgraded from Critical)

**Change from v1.0.0:** The logical bridge has been moved into the slide body. Slide 4 now reads: "The question has already shifted: not *should my child use AI* — but *was I there when they first did?*" This is the bridge sentence the v1.0.0 scorecard identified as missing from the visible deck. It converts the inevitability argument into a direct call to presence in a single readable line.

**Residual vulnerability:** The bridge sentence is strong, but it is placed at the end of the left column of a two-column layout on Slide 4, before the harm documentation in Limbs B (Slides 7–10) and before the mediation evidence in Limb C (Slide 12). The transition to Slide 12 does not re-anchor to this formulation. A reader encountering Slide 12's "Same product. Different adult. Different outcome." without having retained Slide 4's bridge will still have to supply the logical connection themselves.

More critically: the bridge question ("was I there when they first did?") frames presence in terms of the initial encounter. A hostile reader will note that a child's ongoing daily use of AI — not just the first encounter — is where the attachment formation, sycophantic reinforcement, and cognitive atrophy evidence applies. Presence at first encounter is a lower bar than sustained adult engagement through habitual use, and the deck's evidence is about the latter, not the former.

**Deck's response:** Partial. The previously missing bridge is now visible. The downstream reconnection and the first-encounter framing limitation remain unresolved.

**Required resolution:** Slide 12 (or the transition between Slides 11 and 12) should re-anchor the mediation claim to the inevitability established in Slide 4. The bridge question on Slide 4 should be broadened or supplemented to cover ongoing use, not only first encounter.

### CA-2 — "If the product is designed to form attachment, adult guidance is structurally insufficient" (Sycophancy absorbs the solution)
**Severity: Critical** (unchanged)

**Change from v1.0.0:** None in substance. Slide 14 in v1.1.0 carries new body text: "AI companion products are designed to form attachment. Major platforms consistently fail to detect teen mental health crises. These are product and policy failures that require industry action — not just better parenting. Adult guidance is the most important lever you have. Acknowledging its limits makes it more credible, not less." This is the same logical structure as the v1.0.0 speaker notes — it names the adversarial engineering and pivots to guidance as "the most important lever" — without demonstrating that the lever is sufficient against the specific mechanism it has named.

The attack is unchanged and unresolved. The deck has named the sycophancy mechanism with precision on Slide 8 and has named the population most at risk: the child with a developing self-concept, who prefers AI over humans for serious conversations, and who uses the AI specifically to avoid adult mediation. That child — the child who most needs the deck's guidance — is precisely the child for whom "sit alongside your child" (Action 1, Slide 16) is structurally unavailable, because the designed product has already outcompeted the adult relationship.

The Slide 14 formulation "acknowledging its limits makes it more credible, not less" is a rhetorical claim that the act of acknowledgment is itself a substantive response. Credibility and sufficiency are different properties. The deck is more credible in v1.1.0; it is not more sufficient against the adversarially engineered attachment mechanism for the highest-risk population.

**Deck's response:** Partial on credibility, absent on sufficiency. The acknowledgment is now in visible body text but adds no mechanism by which adult guidance operates against adversarially engineered attachment for the at-risk population.

**Required resolution:** Unchanged from v1.0.0. The deck must either (a) produce evidence that adult-mediated relationships are resilient against AI-engineered attachment in high-risk cases, or (b) explicitly scope the guidance case to children who are not already in acute at-risk territory, acknowledging that the at-risk population requires more than individual guidance. The "any parent or teacher starting this week" framing cannot be maintained against the sycophancy finding without one of these two resolutions.

### CA-3 — "The deck's solution requires the parents who are the problem" (Equity undercuts Limb C)
**Severity: Significant** (unchanged)

**Change from v1.0.0:** None. Slide 5's equity argument against school AI bans is unchanged. Slide 13's framing of the 49% gap as addressable by information provision is unchanged. The reframe of guidance as "existing skills applied to a new context" is unchanged.

The attack is unchanged and unresolved. The deck runs the equity argument in Slide 5 against school bans only. A hostile reader runs it against Limb C with equal force: if the solution is guided parental engagement, and if the children most at risk are those with the least engaged parents, the guidance is most available where it is least needed. Slide 13's "this deck is for that 49%" overstates the deck's reach by implying that exposure to the deck resolves the gap. For a significant portion of the 49% — those in lower-income, lower-bandwidth households where other stressors crowd out engaged parental presence — the gap is not information absence. It is capacity and circumstance that a presentation deck cannot address.

**Deck's response:** Unchanged and weak.

**Required resolution:** Unchanged from v1.0.0. Either scope the audience explicitly to parents and teachers with baseline engagement capacity — acknowledging that the structural problem for lower-engagement families requires institutional rather than individual responses — or acknowledge the distribution of the 49% gap and offer a differentiated response that names what this deck cannot do.

### CA-4 — "These statistics are unverified" (Evidentiary foundations are partly unsound)
**Severity: Significant** (unchanged in severity; worsened in vulnerability)

**Change from v1.0.0:** The confidence caveat that appeared in small italic footnote text in v1.0.0 has been removed from the slide body entirely. The stat callout now reads "49% — of parents have never spoken to their child about generative AI. Common Sense Media, 2024." The caveat about confidence level and verification status is now only in speaker notes.

This is a regression from v1.0.0. The attribution is now more specific (September 2024, a real-looking month-and-year anchor) while the caveat has been removed. A hostile audience member who has read the Common Sense Media 2024 research output and cannot locate the 49% figure in primary form now faces a slide that asserts the figure with a precise attribution and no visible qualification. The specific attribution makes the evidentiary failure more visible if the figure cannot be verified — not less.

The Lancet Digital Health article remains unresolved (HTTP 403 in Pax's sweep). It has been an open item since the decisions log entry of 2026-05-25 and is not resolved in v1.1.0.

**Deck's response:** Worsened. The precision of attribution without accompanying qualification increases the evidentiary exposure.

**Required resolution:** Unchanged from v1.0.0, now more urgent. Verify the 49%/96%/11% statistics against primary sources before any live presentation. If verification confirms the figures, the attribution stands and no caveat is needed. If verification fails, rebuild Slide 13 around verifiable figures. A precise-looking secondary attribution that cannot be confirmed under questioning is the worst possible evidentiary position — worse than an acknowledged lower-confidence figure, because it asserts a certainty it cannot deliver. Retrieve the Lancet Digital Health article via institutional access and resolve its relevance to the Australia-substitution claim.

### CA-5 — "The deck is a false trilemma — the fourth option is regulatory intervention, not individual guidance"
**Severity: Significant** (unchanged in severity; marginally improved)

**Change from v1.0.0:** Slide 14 now carries the "Use the lever you have" display phrase in body text and the support line explicitly names the regulatory dimension: "These are product and policy failures that require industry action — not just better parenting." The regulatory dimension is now visible to an audience receiving the slides without a presenter. This is partial improvement.

**Residual vulnerability:** The scope justification is still absent. The deck names the regulatory lever, acknowledges it is necessary, and then sets it aside with "Adult guidance is the most important lever you have" — without explaining why this deck addresses the individual lever rather than the regulatory one. A policy advocate in the room will note that naming industry failure and then directing the audience to five individual behaviors is structurally incoherent — or, more precisely, that it is doing the industry a structural favor by channeling parental concern toward individual adaptation rather than collective demand for product reform. This objection is marginally sharper in v1.1.0 than v1.0.0 because the product-failure acknowledgment is now in body text and the non-sequitur to individual action is correspondingly more visible.

**Deck's response:** Improved on visibility, unimproved on substance. The acknowledgment is now in the body; the justification for the scope choice is not.

**Required resolution:** Unchanged from v1.0.0. One sentence in Slide 14 explaining the scope choice: this deck addresses parents rather than regulators because the regulatory timeline is measured in years and the child's AI encounter is happening this week. That sentence accepts the regulatory argument on its merits and explains the urgency of the individual-level case without dismissing the structural one.

### CA-7 — "The Google/Gemini claim is time-sensitive and may already be inaccurate" (New in v1.1.0)
**Severity: Low**

**New vulnerability introduced in v1.1.0.** Slide 4 now includes a specific dateable claim in the slide body: "In May 2026, Google turned Gemini on by default for children under 13 in Family Link accounts." This is a precise, checkable product-configuration assertion tied to a named company and a specific month.

Two vulnerabilities follow. First, the claim has a known date and may have been reversed, modified, or superseded by the time the deck is presented. Google product rollouts at this scale are frequently modified in response to regulatory pressure or parental backlash. A deck used in June 2026 or later that asserts a specific May 2026 product configuration as a present-tense fact is making a claim that could be falsified by any audience member who checked their Family Link settings recently.

Second, "on by default" is the strongest possible reading of the rollout. If the rollout was opt-in, or if the default was on-but-with-parental-notification, the sentence is factually overstated in a way that could be checked by an audience member live from their phone.

**Deck's response:** The claim carries no as-of qualifier and no staleness caveat in speaker notes.

**Required resolution:** Add an as-of date qualifier ("as of May 2026") and a speaker-notes flag that the specific configuration should be confirmed current before live presentation. Alternatively, replace the Google default claim with the structurally more durable AI Overviews claim (available to unauthenticated users with no age gate), which is less likely to be reversed by a product update.

## Exploitable Vulnerabilities (Ranked)

1. The 49% stat is more specifically attributed in v1.1.0 and now less defensible if it cannot be verified. Removing the confidence caveat while adding precise attribution ("September 2024") means any audience member who knows the Common Sense Media 2024 research output can challenge the figure with more precision than before. This is the single most dangerous vulnerability in a live presentation.

2. The sycophancy finding is more prominent in v1.1.0 (Slide 8 display phrase is a named mechanism in body text) and the gap between that finding and the guidance solution is correspondingly more visible. The deck has raised the salience of the attack while leaving the solution unchanged. A hostile reader who worked through v1.0.0 would have had to search for the sycophancy argument; in v1.1.0 it is a display-phrase-level claim in the body before the guidance recommendation appears.

3. The regulatory scope choice is now named in body text on Slide 14 but still not justified. The sequence — name product failure, name need for industry action, pivot to individual guidance — is clearer in v1.1.0, which means the non-sequitur is also clearer. A policy-oriented hostile reader will find the gap faster.

4. The Slide 4 bridge question frames presence as first-encounter, not ongoing engagement. "Was I there when they first did?" is a narrower framing than the evidence requires. The cognitive atrophy and attachment formation evidence is about habitual use over time, not first exposure. This creates a gap between the rhetorical framing and the evidentiary base.

5. The equity objection cuts both ways and the deck still only runs it one direction. Slide 5 uses equity to attack school bans; the same argument applies with equal force to Limb C's individual guidance solution for lower-engagement families. The deck does not acknowledge this symmetry.

6. The Google/Gemini claim is dateable and verifiable and may already be outdated. Any audience member who has recently checked their Family Link settings has real-time evidence against a specific factual claim in the deck's body text.

## Recommendations (Priority Order)

1. Verify the 49%/96%/11% statistics against primary sources before any live presentation. This is prerequisite, not improvement. Removing the confidence caveat while adding precise attribution has worsened the evidentiary exposure relative to v1.0.0. If the figures verify, the attribution stands and no caveat is needed. If they do not verify, rebuild Slide 13 around verifiable statistics. A specific-looking attribution that cannot be confirmed under questioning is the worst possible evidentiary position.

2. Address the sycophancy-guidance gap at either the evidence or scope level. The deck must either (a) add evidence that adult-mediated relationships are resilient against AI-engineered attachment for the high-risk population, or (b) explicitly scope the guidance case away from the at-risk population and acknowledge that those children require more than individual guidance. The current "acknowledge the limit, then assert the lever" structure is a rhetorical pivot, not a resolution.

3. Add one sentence to Slide 14 justifying the scope choice. "The regulatory timeline is measured in years; your child's AI encounter is happening this week" converts what currently reads as an evasion into an honest argument for urgency. This is a low-effort change with material impact on adversarial resilience against the policy-advocate hostile reader.

4. Broaden or supplement the Slide 4 bridge question to cover ongoing use, not only first encounter. "Was I there when they first did?" is narrower than the cognitive atrophy and attachment evidence requires. A formulation covering habitual engagement — presence as an ongoing practice, not a one-time event — would align the rhetorical framing with the evidentiary base.

5. Acknowledge the distribution of the 49% gap in Slide 13. The deck implies the gap is uniformly an information deficit. For a significant portion of that 49%, it is a capacity and engagement deficit. Naming this honestly increases credibility with the sophisticated audience member already thinking it and removes the overstatement in "this deck is for that 49%."

6. Add an as-of date qualifier to the Google/Gemini claim on Slide 4 and flag the staleness risk in speaker notes. The May 2026 product configuration claim is the deck's most time-sensitive factual assertion. A configuration caveat closes a real vulnerability for any presentation delivered more than a few weeks after the claim was verified.

7. Retrieve the Lancet Digital Health article via institutional access. This has been an open item since Pax's sweep and remains the most significant unresolved gap in the social-media-ban-to-AI-companion-substitution evidence chain. Resolution is required before the substitution-adjacent content in Slide 15 can be treated as confirmed rather than inferred.
