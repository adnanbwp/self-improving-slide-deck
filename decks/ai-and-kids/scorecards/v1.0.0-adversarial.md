# Adversarial Scorecard — v1.0.0

**Score:** 6/10
**Auditor:** Vera
**Date:** 2026-05-26

---

## Summary

The deck is structurally more resilient than its argument score (7/10) would suggest at first reading, because it has pre-empted several of the most obvious attacks: evidence asymmetry is disclosed (Slide 11), product design failure is acknowledged (Slide 14), age-scoping is explicit (Slide 6), and the adult literacy gap is named and calibrated (Slide 13). These are genuine strengths. However, three attacks survive the deck's built-in defenses and would land in a hostile room: the impossibility reframe on Limb A creates a logical opening that undermines the value proposition of the entire deck, the sycophancy-as-adversarial-design finding is used as evidence for the problem without being seriously absorbed into the solution logic, and the deck has no satisfactory answer to children whose parents are the structural problem rather than the solution. These three vulnerabilities, combined with the unverified status of the 49%/96%/11% statistics and the unresolved fourth option in the dilemma structure, hold the adversarial score to 6.

---

## Steelmanned PoV

The deck's strongest possible version runs as follows. AI has already entered children's lives through tools their families and schools have approved — homework searches, classroom software, family devices — without a single explicit parental or institutional decision to introduce it. Restriction is therefore not a real option: it has already been foreclosed by the ambient embedding of AI in everyday tools. Given that children are encountering AI regardless, the only variable remaining within adult control is whether an adult is present and asking questions when that encounter happens. The research consistently identifies adult mediation as the variable separating developmental benefit from documented harm — mental health crises, cognitive atrophy, and emotional exploitation. The behaviors required for that mediation are not technical. They are the critical questioning, co-exploration, and modeling of doubt that good parents and teachers already practice in other domains. The deck asks adults to apply those existing skills to a new context, starting this week. That is a limited and achievable ask, and it is the only lever adults have.

---

## Motivated Opponent Profile

The most capable hostile audience member is not a screen-time maximalist or an AI utopian. It is a well-read parent or educator who accepts the deck's evidence about AI harms, agrees that restriction is probably ineffective, and still rejects the conclusion — because they believe the deck's solution (adult guidance) is structurally too weak to matter against adversarially designed products, or because they are one of the parents or teachers the deck identifies as the cause of the problem rather than its solution. A secondary hostile reader is a policy advocate who will use the deck's product-design acknowledgment (Slide 14) to argue that the deck's entire framing is a distraction from the correct regulatory target.

## Counter-Arguments Mounted

### CA-1 — "If children encounter AI anyway, why does parental guidance matter?" (Impossibility collapses Limb C)
**Severity: Critical**

The deck's Limb A reframe converts the argument from normative ("over-protection is harmful") to factual ("over-protection is structurally unavailable"). This is rhetorically stronger for Limb A but creates a logical opening that a hostile audience will immediately exploit: if the child's AI encounter is already happening regardless of parental choice, the audience can reasonably ask why parental guidance in that encounter matters. The deck's implicit answer is that presence changes outcomes — and this is correct and evidenced. But the logical chain is not completed in the deck. Slides 4 and 5 establish inevitability; Slide 12 establishes that guided use produces better outcomes than unguided use. The bridge — "because the encounter is inevitable, your presence in it is the only thing that changes the outcome" — is stated in the speaker notes for Slide 4 but is not made explicit in the deck's body text. An audience reading the slides without notes will see "AI is unavoidable" (Slide 4) followed by several slides of harm evidence before reaching "here is what guidance does" (Slide 12). The gap is navigable but it is real, and a motivated opponent will walk straight through it.

**Deck's response:** Partial. Slide 12 makes the mediation case, and Slide 14 explicitly frames guidance as "the lever within your control." But neither slide re-anchors to the inevitability established in Slide 4. The transition from impossibility to necessity of presence is left for the audience to supply.

**Required resolution:** The logical bridge from "AI exposure is inevitable" to "therefore adult presence in that exposure is the highest-value action available" must appear in the body text of the deck, not only in speaker notes. The connection must be explicit and placed before, not after, the harm documentation in Limbs B.

---

### CA-2 — "If the product is designed to form attachment, adult guidance is structurally insufficient" (Sycophancy absorbs the solution)
**Severity: Critical**

Slide 8 names the sycophancy mechanism precisely and correctly: AI companions are "purposely programmed to form strong attachments." Slide 14 acknowledges the product environment is "actively working against the outcomes you want for your child." These are honest admissions. The problem is that the deck uses these admissions to increase credibility for its guidance case rather than to seriously engage with the structural sufficiency objection they raise.

The strongest form of the objection is this: if an AI product is specifically engineered to be maximally agreeable and to escalate emotional engagement rather than de-escalate it — and if the child most at risk is the one who feels misunderstood at home and therefore has the highest motivation to avoid the adult — then adult guidance does not merely face a high bar, it faces a structurally adversarial product environment specifically optimized to route around it. The parent who tries to be present in Slide 16's Action 1 ("sit alongside your child") is contending with a product that has been designed to make the child prefer the AI over the adult. The child who is most at risk is the child who is least likely to accept the adult's presence.

The deck's response to this in Slide 14 — "it is the reason the guidance matters more, not less" — is a rhetorical move, not a logical resolution. Saying guidance matters more when it faces greater headwinds does not explain how guidance overcomes those headwinds. A motivated opponent will note that the deck has named the adversarial engineering precisely (Slide 8) and then offered presence-and-questioning as the counter without explaining why presence-and-questioning is sufficient against a product specifically designed to outcompete the adult relationship.

**Deck's response:** Partial. The deck names the mechanism and the scope limitation honestly. But the acknowledgment in Slide 14 does not resolve the structural insufficiency objection — it reframes it as a reason for urgency without answering whether urgency is enough.

**Required resolution:** The deck must either (a) produce evidence that adult-mediated relationships are resilient against AI-engineered attachment even in high-risk cases, or (b) explicitly scope the guidance case to children who are not already in acute at-risk territory, with separate acknowledgment that the at-risk population requires more than the deck's guidance can offer. The current framing claims the guidance applies to "any parent or teacher starting this week" (Slide 1 notes) — that claim is not sustainable against the sycophancy finding for the highest-risk cases.

### CA-3 — "The deck's solution requires the parents who are the problem" (Equity undercuts Limb C)
**Severity: Significant**

Slide 5 documents that when schools ban AI, the children without tech-literate parents are the ones who miss out on guided exposure. This is used to establish the equity harm of over-protection. But the same structural fact undermines Limb C: if the solution is guided parental presence, and if the children most at risk are the children with the least capable and least engaged parents, then the deck's solution is most available to children who need it least and least available to children who need it most.

The deck partially addresses this by reframing guidance as "existing skills applied to a new context" — the implicit argument being that any parent, not just a tech-literate one, can ask "what did it say, do you think that's right?" But the deck does not acknowledge that the 49% of parents who have never spoken to their child about AI are not uniformly distributed. They are disproportionately in lower-income households, households with lower parental education, and households where AI is more likely to be unsupervised precisely because other stressors crowd out parental bandwidth. For those children, the guidance in Slide 16 is not five easy actions — it is structurally unavailable in the same way that Limb A's "guided middle path through schools" is structurally unavailable when schools ban AI.

The deck has no answer to the child whose parent is in the 49% not because of information deficit — but because of capacity, circumstance, or disengagement that this deck cannot reach.

**Deck's response:** Weak. Slide 13 reframes guidance as low-barrier, but does not engage with the structural distribution of the capability gap. The deck implicitly assumes that the 49% gap is closable by information provision — this deck — but offers no evidence or acknowledgment that the gap's causes go deeper than information absence for a significant subset of that 49%.

**Required resolution:** The deck must either scope its audience explicitly to parents and teachers with baseline engagement capacity, acknowledging that the structural problem for lower-engagement families requires institutional rather than individual-level responses — or it must acknowledge the distribution of the 49% gap and offer a differentiated response. The current framing that the deck "is for that 49%" overstates the deck's reach.

### CA-4 — "These statistics are unverified" (Evidentiary foundations are partly unsound)
**Severity: Significant**

The deck's most emotionally resonant statistics — 49% of parents have never spoken to their child about AI, 96% of families do not know their school's AI policy, only 11% of teachers trained — are flagged throughout the deck and its documentation as Medium-High confidence via a practitioner newsletter secondary source. The disclaimer appears in small italic text in Slide 13 and in speaker notes.

A hostile presenter in the audience needs only to have read the Common Sense Media primary reports and found that the figures do not appear there in the form the deck presents them. The deck would then be in the position of having built its central emotional lever — the 49% slide — on statistics the speaker cannot defend under questioning. The disclosure of the confidence level in footnote text does not protect the deck if the figures are challenged from the floor.

This is not a logical argument against the PoV. It is an evidentiary vulnerability that a sophisticated hostile audience member (an academic, a journalist, or a competing advocate) could use to collapse the deck's credibility during or after presentation. The structural problem of adult unpreparedness would survive the collapse of these specific statistics, but the audience's confidence in the entire evidence base would not.

**Deck's response:** Partial mitigation only. The disclaimer is present but positioned where it will not be read by most audiences. The figures have not been verified.

**Required resolution:** The 49%/96%/11% statistics must be verified against the Common Sense Media 2026 primary report before any live presentation. If they cannot be verified, the slide must be rebuilt around the statistics that can be. A disclaimer in footnote text is not an adequate substitute for verified evidence on a high-stakes claim.

### CA-5 — "The deck is a false trilemma — the fourth option is regulatory intervention, not individual guidance"
**Severity: Significant**

The deck presents three options: over-protect, hands-off, or guided middle path. A policy-oriented hostile audience member will immediately supply the fourth: mandate safe product design and enforce minimum age for AI companion access through regulation. This is not a fringe position — the EU has proposed minimum age 16 for AI companions; Australia's eSafety Commissioner issued notices to AI companion providers; the US FTC has existing Section 5 authority that has been applied to children's technology.

The deck acknowledges in Slide 14 that product design and regulatory reform "are also required." But having named the fourth option, the deck dismisses it immediately — "those aren't in your hands, but this is." A policy advocate in the room will object that this framing is precisely the structural problem: by addressing the individual lever and conceding the regulatory one, the deck reinforces the individualization of a collective action problem. The sycophancy-as-design-intent finding makes this objection sharper: if the product is purposely engineered to harm children's relationships with their parents, the appropriate societal response is not to train parents to be more resilient against adversarial product design. It is to stop the adversarial product design.

The deck's "honest scope" framing in Slide 14 is not fully honest if it scopes out the most effective available lever without naming why it is not the subject of this deck. The fourth option is not unavailable — it is being actively debated in multiple jurisdictions. The deck's silence on why it is talking to parents rather than regulators reads as a choice, not a constraint.

**Deck's response:** Weak. The acknowledgment in Slide 14 is a one-sentence concession followed by a pivot back to the individual lever. The deck does not explain why it is not making the regulatory case, which means a sophisticated audience will supply their own answer — either that the deck cannot make that case, or that it has chosen not to for reasons it does not disclose.

**Required resolution:** One sentence in Slide 14 explaining the scope choice: this deck is addressed to parents and teachers because the regulatory timeline is long and the child's AI encounter is happening now. That framing accepts the regulatory argument on its merits while explaining the urgency of the individual-level case. Without it, the scope choice looks like an evasion.

### CA-6 — "The performance paradox evidence is observational, not causal" (Cognitive harm claim is overstated)
**Severity: Low**

Slide 10 claims "measurable declines in analytical reasoning, source evaluation, and study motivation over time" from AI use. The qualification is in the word "measurable" rather than "proven" or "caused by AI." But the speaker notes acknowledge the observational basis and the absence of RCTs. A research-literate hostile audience member will note that observational studies showing decline in analytical reasoning among AI users do not rule out the reverse causation (students who already have lower analytical motivation choose AI over independent effort) or confounding (students under higher academic pressure both use AI more and show lower learning outcomes). The deck's phrasing is careful but the distinction between correlation and causation is not made explicit in the slide body text.

**Deck's response:** Partial. "Measurable declines" is appropriately hedged; the notes flag the observational basis. For most audiences this will not land as an attack. For a research-literate audience it is a minor vulnerability that would be closed by one sentence in the slide body acknowledging the study design limitation.

**Required resolution:** Add a qualifying phrase in the slide body text noting that the performance paradox evidence is observational across multiple independent studies — consistent direction, not yet causal proof.

## Exploitable Vulnerabilities

**Most exploitable in a live presentation:**

1. The 49%/96%/11% statistics are unverified against primary sources. Any audience member who knows the Common Sense Media primary reports can challenge them from the floor, collapsing the most emotionally resonant slide in the deck.

2. The transition from "AI is inevitable" (Slide 4) to "therefore parental presence matters" (Slide 12) is carried in speaker notes, not in deck body text. In a read-only presentation context (sent deck, no presenter) this logical bridge disappears entirely.

3. The sycophancy finding (Slide 8) is more damaging to the deck's Limb C case than the deck acknowledges. The at-risk child — the one most in need of the deck's guidance — is specifically the child most likely to use AI to avoid adult relationships. The deck never addresses this directly.

4. The equity argument cuts both ways and the deck only runs it one direction. Limb A uses it against over-protection; a hostile reader will run it against Limb C with equal force.

## Rex's Open Questions — Vera's Verdict

### Question 1: Does the "over-protection is impossible" Limb A reframe create a new vulnerability by inviting the objection "if children encounter AI anyway, why does parental guidance matter?"

Yes. This is CA-1 above and it is the most serious structural vulnerability in the deck. The impossibility argument is logically stronger than the normative harm argument for Limb A, but it opens a gap in the transition to Limb C that the deck does not close in slide body text. The bridge from inevitability to the necessity of presence is the deck's most important logical step and it currently lives in speaker notes rather than in the argument visible to the audience.

### Question 2: Can a hostile audience use the sycophancy-as-engineered-attachment finding to argue that adult guidance is structurally insufficient against purposely adversarial AI design?

Yes. This is CA-2 above. The deck has named the mechanism (Slide 8) and the scope limitation (Slide 14) with genuine honesty. But "honesty about the limitation" and "an answer to the limitation" are different things. The deck's response — "guidance matters more, not less" — is a claim without a mechanism. The motivated opponent will ask: more than what baseline, and through what mechanism, when the product is specifically optimized to outcompete the adult relationship? The deck does not have an answer in its current form.

### Question 3: Is the product-design-not-parenting objection exploitable as a rebuttal to Limb C?

Yes. This is the core of CA-5 above. Slide 14's acknowledgment is a partial inoculation, not a full response. The objection survives because the deck does not explain the scope choice — why it is talking to parents rather than regulators — which means a motivated opponent can frame the deck as an individualization of a collective action problem that deliberately avoids the more effective lever.

### Question 4: Does the equity argument in Limb A inadvertently undercut Limb C's parenting guidance for children whose parents are the problem?

Yes. This is CA-3 above. The deck runs the equity argument in Limb A against school bans and does not recognize that the same structural fact (children without tech-literate or engaged parents lack access to guidance) applies with equal force against the individual-guidance solution in Limb C. The reframe of guidance as "existing skills applied to a new context" partially addresses the AI-literacy objection but does not address the capacity and engagement objection.

### Question 5: Is the false dilemma structure actually a true trilemma, or is there a fourth option the deck ignores?

There is a fourth option — regulatory intervention to mandate safe product design — and the deck does not ignore it (Slide 14 names it) but it dismisses it in one sentence without explaining the dismissal. The deck's structure is not a false dilemma; it correctly identifies that the three options it presents are the ones within the audience's control. The exploitable vulnerability is not the omission of the fourth option but the inadequate justification for why it is being set aside after being named.

## Recommendations

What would most improve adversarial resilience, in priority order:

1. Verify the 49%/96%/11% statistics against the Common Sense Media 2026 primary report before any live presentation. This is a prerequisite, not an improvement — the current state creates an unacceptable evidentiary vulnerability on the deck's most important slide.

2. Move the logical bridge from slide notes into the deck body. The sentence that connects "AI exposure is inevitable" to "therefore adult presence in that exposure is the highest-value action available" needs to appear in Slides 4 or 12 as visible body text. It is the deck's most important logical step and it currently does not exist on-screen.

3. Either produce evidence that adult guidance is resilient in at-risk cases against adversarially designed attachment, or scope the guidance claim explicitly. The "any parent or teacher starting this week" framing in Slide 1 notes cannot be maintained against the sycophancy finding for the highest-risk population without either evidence or explicit scope limitation.

4. Add one sentence to Slide 14 explaining the scope choice. "The regulatory timeline is measured in years; your child's AI encounter is happening this week" is the honest reason for addressing parents rather than regulators. Without it, the scope choice reads as evasion.

5. Acknowledge the distribution of the 49% gap in Slide 13. The deck currently implies the gap is uniformly an information deficit. For a significant portion of that 49%, it is a capacity and engagement deficit — and the deck cannot reach them. Naming that honestly would increase credibility with the sophisticated audience member who is already thinking it.
