# Persuasion Scorecard — v1.0.0 (Teachers)

**Score:** 7.5/10
**Auditor:** Aria
**Audience:** Teachers
**Date:** 2026-05-26
**CTA:** Assign one task this week where students find something the AI got wrong about your subject area, then discuss it as a class.

---

## Audience Model

**Cognitive priors.** Teachers enter this deck with a professional identity built around being the knowledge authority and the trusted adult in the room. AI challenges both. Most are aware students are using AI but lack clear institutional guidance on how to respond. The majority have encountered school-level ban policies with little accompanying professional development. They have absorbed the "AI cheating" discourse heavily and may be primed to read pro-AI arguments as naive or dismissive of academic integrity.

**Values.** Student wellbeing and genuine learning over credentialing. Professional autonomy — they resent being told what to do by administrators who also have not figured this out. Fairness across socioeconomic lines is a live value for many, particularly in schools serving lower-income communities. Competence: they do not want to be put in front of a class unable to answer basic questions about the tools students are using.

**Anxieties.** Three cluster distinctly: (1) being replaced — AI as the better teacher anxiety is real and unspoken; (2) professional inadequacy — being asked to guide something they were never trained in; (3) liability — if a student is harmed by AI interaction, who is responsible? The deck addresses the first two but does not touch the third, which is live for teachers in safeguarding-aware systems.

**JTBD.** Teachers need a defensible position they can explain to students, parents, and administrators simultaneously. They need a concrete classroom practice they can implement without a new curriculum or a training course. They need permission to act — specifically permission to engage with AI rather than ban it, from a frame grounded in evidence they can cite.

**Current belief state.** Distributed across a meaningful range. Some teachers in this deck's likely audience have already moved toward integration and need validation and tools. A significant group is in reluctant compliance with ban policies and looking for permission to do what they already suspect is better. A smaller group is deeply skeptical of AI in any classroom context and will read Slide 7 ("AI is a better knowledge deliverer than any human teacher") as a threat before a reframe. The deck must earn slide 7, and it mostly does — but see friction audit below.

---

## Activation Gap

Moderate. Teachers are closer to acceptance than parents (who are dealing with unfamiliar territory at home), but they face a structural constraint parents do not: institutional policy. A teacher who is persuaded by this deck may still be unable to act without administrator buy-in or a district AI policy. Slide 11 Action 3 acknowledges this, but the deck does not devote sufficient weight to navigating the institutional constraint — the biggest practical gap between "I agree" and "I act" for this audience.

---

## Summary

The teachers variant is a well-constructed audience adaptation that correctly identifies the professional identity reframe — from knowledge competitor to AI guide — as the load-bearing persuasive move for this segment. The statistical foundation is strong, the equity argument lands with moral force, and Slide 8 (existing skills apply) is the deck's most persuasive slide for teachers. The deck earns a meaningful score here. What keeps it below 8.5 is a combination of a friction-generating moment in Slide 7 that is not adequately prepared, an underdeveloped path through the institutional constraint, and a CTA that is appropriately specific but does not fully close the loop on the school policy dimension it raises.

---

## Emotional Resonance

**Score: 7/10**

**What works.** The 11% teacher training statistic, immediately reframed as systemic failure rather than individual failure, is the single strongest emotional move in the deck. Teachers carrying quiet shame about their AI literacy gap will exhale at that sentence. The equity argument in Slide 3 — that under-resourced students have no other guided context — activates the professional moral sense of purpose that most teachers enter the profession carrying. Slide 8's list of existing pedagogical skills (Socratic questioning, source evaluation, collaborative sense-making) reads as validating rather than instructional, which is the right register for a professional audience.

**What falls flat.** Slide 7 opens with "AI is a better knowledge deliverer than any human teacher." This claim is accurate and the reframe that follows is sound, but for a teacher who has not yet been given permission to relax their threat response, this is an identity hit before a lifeline. The deck reaches this slide having built credibility with Slides 2–6, so most of the audience will follow the argument through — but a meaningful fraction will have an emotional shutdown at the first sentence that the rest of the slide has to work to undo. A one-sentence primer before that claim — something that names the distinction between knowledge delivery and teaching — would reduce the friction without softening the argument.

The 58% callout in Slide 6 (students prefer asking a chatbot to asking their teacher) lands as threat rather than motivation for some teachers. It is placed as a margin statistic without a sentence that reframes it as a signal of how much teachers are needed, not how redundant they are. This is a missed activation opportunity.

---

## Narrative Clarity

**Score: 8/10**

The three-limb structure (ban fails, hands-off harms, guided path works) is clean and accessible for educators. The argument progression from Slides 2 through 9 is well-sequenced: reality before argument, harm before recommendation, scope acknowledgment before CTA. The Google Chromebook material in Slide 4 is particularly well-chosen for a teacher audience — it names a tool already in their classroom, which converts the abstract "AI is everywhere" premise into immediate recognition.

The honest scope slide (Slide 9) does meaningful work. Naming the professional development gap as a structural failure and explicitly inviting teachers to ask for training rather than struggle in silence is both practically useful and rhetorically smart — it demonstrates the deck is not asking teachers to solve a problem alone that is not theirs to solve alone.

Minor clarity issues: The "Limb A", "Limb B", "Limb C" internal labels appear in the slide header labels (slide-header spans). Teachers will not know what these mean and while they are secondary UI, they are visible in a deck presentation context and introduce unnecessary jargon. This is a presentation hygiene issue, not a structural problem.

## CTA Assessment

**Score: 7/10**

**Primary CTA identified:** Assign a task where students find one thing the AI got wrong about your subject area, discuss it as a class — this week, no new curriculum required.

**What works.** The CTA is teacher-native — it maps directly to existing lesson planning behaviors. "Find something the AI got wrong" is a concrete, bounded task, not an attitude shift. The "this week" time anchoring is well-calibrated. The secondary actions (raise AI policy with leadership, use AI output as discussion starting point rather than submission endpoint) are appropriate escalations that do not require the primary action to be completed first.

**What limits CTA strength.** Action 3 — finding out whether a school AI policy exists and raising it with leadership — is presented as a co-equal item in the numbered list rather than as the institutional unlock that makes all other actions sustainable. A teacher who does Action 1 alone is doing one lesson. A teacher who also does Action 3 is doing policy advocacy for structural change. These have different weights and the list formatting flattens them.

The framing box on Slide 11 ("you do not need to be an AI expert — you need to be the adult in the room who asks the questions") is the strongest single sentence in the deck for a teacher audience. It functions as the CTA's emotional headline, but it is in a secondary layout element on the right column rather than in a position of visual prominence. If this deck were redesigned, that sentence would be the closing line before the references, not a box in a grid.

There is no explicit ask to share, discuss with a colleague, or bring to a departmental meeting — a natural viral loop for a teacher audience that the deck leaves inactive. Teachers are more likely to act when they can discuss with a peer, and a "share with one colleague" secondary CTA costs nothing.

## Friction Audit

**Friction point 1 — "AI is a better knowledge deliverer than any human teacher" (Slide 7).** This triggers the professional threat response before the reframe. The reframe is good but the cold open is a stumble for the portion of the audience still in threat mode. Mitigation: one sentence before the claim that signals the argument structure — something that names what the teacher's role is *not* about before the claim lands.

**Friction point 2 — Institutional authority displacement.** The deck repeatedly advises teachers to "find out if your school has a policy" and "raise it with leadership." For teachers in schools with active ban policies, this reads as instruction to challenge their administration. The deck does not acknowledge that a teacher in a ban-policy school may not feel they have permission to deviate from that policy, even if persuaded. This is the gap between intellectual agreement and actual behavior change.

**Friction point 3 — Source chain transparency.** The footnote on Slide 12 acknowledges that the CDT and Common Sense Media statistics are "Medium-High confidence via practitioner newsletter — verify before live presentation." Teachers who notice this footnote (and credentialed teachers often do) may soften their confidence in the deck's quantitative anchors. The deck would be stronger if the core statistics (86%, 58%, 11%) had been verified against primary reports before v1.0.0 was produced. This is a flagged open item from the decisions log, and it remains a persuasion risk in this version.

**Friction point 4 — Professional development ask framed as individual action.** Slide 9 says teachers should ask for training. But teachers who have been asking for professional development for years without institutional response may read this as naive optimism. The deck does not acknowledge that asking has limits.

---

## Recommendations

1. **Resequence Slide 7's opening.** Replace the cold claim ("AI is a better knowledge deliverer than any human teacher") with a one-sentence frame that names the distinction first — something like "Teaching and knowledge delivery are not the same thing" — before making the comparative claim. This lets the argument land as insight rather than threat for the segment still in defensive mode.

2. **Elevate the framing box to the CTA's closing headline.** Move "You do not need to be an AI expert. You need to be the adult in the room who asks the questions..." out of the secondary column layout and into slide-closing prominence on Slide 11. This is the emotional landing the deck is working toward — it should be the last thing teachers read before the references.

3. **Separate Action 3 from the action list and give it distinct weight.** The policy advocacy action (raise AI policy with leadership) is structurally different from the classroom practice actions. Presenting it at equal weight in a numbered list understates its significance and may cause teachers to treat it as optional. A brief call-out that frames it as the institutional complement to classroom action — distinct from but connected to the in-class steps — would serve this CTA better.
