# Narrative Scorecard — ai-and-kids v2.0.0 (canonical)

## Mode: Critique

## Objective
Convince parents and teachers that both over-protection (banning AI) and hands-off (unguided access) harm children, that adult presence is the deciding variable, and get them to take specific actions this week.

## Inferred arc & purpose
**Man in a Hole** — comfort (parents assume they're handling it) → trigger (both your instincts harm the child) → treasure in the dark (adult presence is the variable) → recovery (the guided path) → better place (five things this week). Purpose: **Convince + Motivate**. The two-sided harm structure (over-protection section, then hands-off section) is reunified by the evidence-asymmetry seam (slide 13).

## Checks

### Hook — PASS
- Evidence: Opens strong. The title "Your Child Is Already Using AI" (01) is a relatable/unexpected hook, and slide 02 delivers the spiky reversal immediately: "Most parents are handling AI wrong — and it is hurting their children either way." The "either way you're wrong" framing is a genuine information-gap hook, not a cold start.
- Fix: n/a — protect it. Optional: the presenter can pause on the "either way" beat before advancing, since that reversal is the whole hook.

### One arc — PASS
- Evidence: A Man-in-a-Hole line holds start to finish: false premise (04) → the two failure modes (06–12) → the deciding variable (14) → guided path + actions (15–18). The two-column harm structure risks reading as two mini-decks, but slide 13 (evidence asymmetry: "neither cancels the other") is the deliberate seam that reunifies them into one argument.
- Fix: n/a. Watch-item: keep slide 13 doing its reunifying job in any future edit — it's load-bearing for the single-arc read.

### Movie moment — PASS
- Evidence: Multiple concrete, visualisable moments: the 14-year-old in Florida who died after bonding with a Character.AI chatbot (11), a child's homework search with Gemini answering by default (06), the billboard rule (18: "Don't type anything you wouldn't put on a public billboard").
- Fix: n/a — the Character.AI case is the deck's most powerful beat; hold it exactly where it is (the harm section, not the open).

### Rule of three — PASS
- Evidence: The spine is a clean three-pattern — two extremes (over-protect / hands-off) plus the guided middle. Reinforced by the three schools (05) and three age bands (08). The two checklists (five warning signs 17, five actions 18) exceed three but are scannable action lists, not argument points.
- Fix: n/a.

### Leave it out — PASS
- Evidence: Disciplined at 20 slides with no obvious filler; each harm slide makes a distinct point (scale 09, sycophancy 10, documented harm 11, cognitive atrophy 12). For a skeptical-parent audience the evidence density is load-bearing, not padding.
- Fix: n/a, but one watch-item: slides 09–12 are four consecutive harm slides and are the one place that risks fatigue. If a future cut is needed, cognitive atrophy (12) is the softest of the four (observational, not RCT per its own note) and is the first candidate to compress or move to a reference slide.

### Concreteness — PASS
- Evidence: Strong exemplary details that stand for the whole: "49% of parents have never spoken to their child about AI" (15), "Gemini on by default for under-13s" (06), the billboard rule (18).
- Fix: n/a.

### Payoff — FAIL
- Evidence: The deck's most quotable, couldn't-say-it-until-now line — "Same product. Different adult. Different outcome." — lands at slide 14 of 20. Nothing after it tops it: the deck continues into preparedness (15), honest scope (16), warning signs (17), and closes the argument on the five-action checklist (18) before Sources/Team. The payoff is delivered mid-deck and then diffused; the ending trails into a list rather than re-landing the moral.
- Fix: Re-land the payoff as the closing beat. After the five actions (18), add one short closing slide that crystallises "you are the variable" — e.g., a single line that sends them out on the insight, not the fifth bullet. The deck currently has no CTA/close slide the way hybrid-delivery does; that's the gap.

### Trust — FAIL
- Evidence: The objective is a high-trust, act-on-my-advice parenting ask, and the trust slot is empty. Slide 03 (About the Presenter) is a stub: title "Parent", bio "Ask me about me", with a speaker note to "Update bio before presenting." The presenter's own parent-credibility — the exact "Trust Me, I'm an Expert" values-in-action move this topic needs — is a placeholder.
- Fix: Fill slide 03 with the presenter's real, concrete parent moment — sitting with their own child during an AI homework search, the conversation that followed, what it changed. Values shown in action, not asserted as a job title. This is the single highest-leverage fix.

## Ranked issues
1. **Trust** — the About-me slide (03) is a blank stub; for a parenting-advice deck the presenter's own parent-credibility is the load-bearing trust move and it is currently empty. Highest leverage.
2. **Payoff** — the killer line ("Same product. Different adult. Different outcome.") lands at slide 14; the deck then trails into a checklist and back-matter instead of re-landing "you are the variable" as the closing beat. Add a crystallising close after slide 18.

## Verdict
Holds as one arc — **needs light work**. This is a strong deck: a spiky "either way you're wrong" hook, a devastating movie moment, concrete anchors, balanced evidence, and a clean two-extremes-plus-middle structure. Two fixes stand between it and ready: fill the empty trust slot (03) with the presenter's real parent story, and re-land the payoff as a closing beat after the five actions. Both are additive and touch no claim or evidence — the same shape of fix that moved hybrid-delivery from 4 FAIL to 0.
