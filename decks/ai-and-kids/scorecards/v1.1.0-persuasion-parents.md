# Persuasion Scorecard — v1.1.0 (Parents)

**Score:** 8/10
**Auditor:** Aria
**Audience:** Parents
**Date:** 2026-05-26
**CTA:** Tonight, when your child next uses Google for homework, sit alongside them and ask: "What did it say? Do you think that's right?" This week, ask your child to show you one thing the AI got wrong recently — if they can't find one, find it together at the dinner table.

---

## Summary

The v1.1.0 parents variant is a clear improvement over v1.0.0 in three material respects: the personalised Slide 2 display phrase creates an immediate cognitive hook that v1.0.0 lacked; the addition of a specific, repeatable action in Action 5 addresses the single most critical CTA gap from the prior scorecard; and the Slide 14 bridge phrase "Use the lever you have" reduces the motivational dip that previously fell immediately before the action slide. The design pass — stat callouts, display phrases, col-layouts, and the two images — meaningfully increases emotional activation without distorting the argument. The deck now earns 8/10 overall.

Two weaknesses remain. The Slide 5 sequencing gap identified in v1.0.0 was not addressed, and the consequences are still present: prescriptive age thresholds land before the emotional case that earns them. The 44% figure on Slide 13 carries a confidence caveat that has been moved to speaker notes only — this is a defensible call given the parent audience, but it creates a small credibility risk if a sceptical parent asks for the source and finds the provenance trail through a practitioner newsletter rather than a primary report. Neither issue is fatal at 8/10, but both are actionable for v1.2.0.

## Audience Model

**Priors.** Most parents in this room believe either that their child's school is managing AI exposure, or that their child is more technically capable than they are and needs less guidance, not more. The 49% who have never spoken to their child about AI are not negligent — they are in a default posture of deferral that has served them adequately on other technology questions. They come in expecting to be told they are doing something wrong. The deck's job is to reframe deferral as a structural mistake, not a moral failure.

**Values.** Protection is the dominant value, but parental agency is close behind it. Parents want to be effective, not just well-intentioned. Self-efficacy is load-bearing here: a parent who believes they lack the technical knowledge to act will not act, regardless of how alarmed the deck makes them feel. Relatedness — being a good parent who stays present — is the motivational engine the deck can and should pull on.

**Anxieties.** The primary anxiety is "I don't know enough about AI to help my child" — named explicitly in the 44% figure. The secondary anxiety is "am I already too late?" — addressed but not fully resolved by the deck. A third anxiety, less visible but significant, is "if I bring this up, will it make things worse or invite a conflict I'm not equipped to handle?" The deck does not address this third anxiety directly, which is a minor friction point.

**Jobs to be Done (JTBD).** Give me a specific thing I can do tonight that does not require me to understand how AI works. Tell me what warning signs to watch for. Help me have the conversation with my child without making it adversarial. Reassure me that being present and curious is enough — I do not need to become a technology expert.

**Current belief state.** Low-to-moderate alarm, high inadequacy, passive deferral. AI is on their radar as a concern but has not yet competed successfully with the volume of other parenting demands. Screen time fatigue is real — many parents have already been through one cycle of moral panic about social media and are calibrating their response to AI against that experience. Some will arrive sceptical of urgency claims; others will arrive anxious and open.

**Activation gap.** Moderate. The deck needs to do three things in sequence: establish that the hands-off posture is not safe neutrality (it is active exposure without mediation), show that parent guidance requires no technical knowledge, and land a specific same-day action before the audience leaves. The gap between current passivity and the guided co-learner posture is achievable in a single session if the emotional case is made before the prescriptive guidance arrives.

## Emotional Resonance

**Score: 8/10**

The v1.1.0 design pass materially increases emotional activation compared to v1.0.0. The stat callouts on Slides 7, 9, and 13 — 77%, 1 in 8, 49% — function as emotional anchors that break the text rhythm and create a moment of pause. For a parent audience that processes risk through personal relevance rather than population statistics, the rendering of these numbers as large typographic statements is more persuasive than the same numbers embedded in body copy. This is a meaningful improvement.

The Slide 2 display phrase "Remove the parent. Remove the risk. Wrong." is the single most effective addition in the parents variant. It names the shared false premise before the audience has constructed a defence against it, it uses "parent" rather than "adult" (which is the canonical variant's word), and the single-word refutation "Wrong." delivers the deck's entire thesis in three lines. A parent who was intending to be passive is directly confronted on Slide 2, before they have heard the argument — this is structurally correct because it sets up the emotional question the rest of the deck answers.

Slide 7's personalised line — "Your child is almost certainly already in the deep end. The question is whether you know it." — is well-calibrated. It creates urgency through an unresolved personal question rather than through fear framing, which is the right approach for a parent audience that will resist being alarmed but will respond to curiosity about what is happening in their own home. The contrast between the population-scale stat (77%) and the personal framing (your child) on the same slide is the correct rhetorical structure for this audience.

Slide 12's display phrase — "Same product. Different parent. Different outcome." — is the emotional payload of Limb C. It makes the parent the variable rather than the technology, which is the message that converts anxiety into agency. This is the pivot the deck needs to earn the action slide, and it lands cleanly.

The motivational dip from v1.0.0 (scope acknowledgment arriving immediately before the CTA) is partially addressed. The "Use the lever you have" display phrase on Slide 14 heads the scope acknowledgment with an agentic statement rather than a disclaimer, and the closing line — "Your involvement is the most important lever you have right now. Acknowledging its limits makes it more credible, not less." — is a meaningful improvement over the v1.0.0 equivalent. However, a parent who processes the acknowledgment that AI platforms are "designed to form attachment" and "consistently fail to detect teen mental health crises" may still exit Slide 14 feeling outgunned rather than empowered. The slide does more work than its v1.0.0 predecessor but does not fully close the dip.

One flat spot not previously flagged: Slide 5's display phrase — "The ban protects the students least at risk." — is the strongest line on that slide, but for a parent whose child attends a school without an AI ban, the slide's framing around school policy can feel at one remove. The speaker notes do the work of translating this to the home context, but a parent reading the slide without the notes may not make that connection independently.

## Narrative Clarity

**Score: 7/10**

The argument sequence is clear and accessible to a non-expert parent. There is no unexplained jargon. The three-schools orientation on Slide 3 is an effective device for helping parents locate their own instincts before the argument begins — most parents will recognise themselves as a mix of School 1 and School 3, which sets up the deck's position in Schools 2 and 3 as an achievable reframe rather than a contradiction of their values.

The Slide 5 sequencing problem from v1.0.0 was not addressed and the friction remains. A parent receiving the age-based thresholds — "no AI companions under 5," "no social AI companion apps for anyone under 18" — on Slide 5 has not yet heard the documented harms that make these limits urgent (Slides 7–10). For a parent who is not yet alarmed, the thresholds arrive as instructions without a felt reason to follow them. For a parent who is skeptical, they arrive as prescriptive guidance from a source that has not yet earned the authority to prescribe. The natural reading sequence for this audience is: establish what is already happening (Slide 4), show what it produces when unguided (Slides 7, 8, 9, 10), then present the specific evidence-based limits that follow from that harm evidence (Slide 5 content). Moving the age-scoping slide to follow Slide 10 would allow the emotional case to earn the prescriptive guidance.

The evidence asymmetry disclosure on Slide 11 is a clarity asset. It names the asymmetry directly — hands-off harm is more acutely documented than over-protection harm — rather than implying false equivalence. For a parent who has been tracking the argument, this is the kind of honesty that increases credibility rather than undermining the case. The contrast-pair layout makes the comparison visually immediate.

Slide 13's body copy contains the most load-bearing claim for this audience: "You do not need to become an AI expert. Asking 'what did it say?', modeling doubt, sitting alongside your child — you already do all of this. Point it at AI." This is the self-efficacy anchor the deck needs, and it is well-placed. However, it arrives alongside three statistics (49%, 96%, 44%) in a relatively text-dense slide. The stat callout foregrounds the 49% figure, which is the right choice — the 96% and 44% figures are supporting data, not headline claims. The density of three statistics on one slide may cause a live audience to anchor on the numbers rather than processing the self-efficacy message, which is the more important payload.

The warning signs on Slide 15 remain a clarity asset: five specific behavioral signals from a named clinical source, with a clear threshold for action. This is the format a parent can use tonight.

## CTA Assessment

**Score: 9/10**

The Action 5 replacement — "Ask your child to show you one thing the AI got wrong recently. If they can't find one, find it together at the dinner table." — is the single most important improvement in the v1.1.0 parents variant and directly addresses the most critical recommendation from v1.0.0. It converts a disposition ("stay curious") into a specific, schedulable act that requires zero AI knowledge, can be anchored to an existing routine (dinner), and models the exact behaviour the deck argues matters: sitting alongside the child and asking critical questions rather than accepting AI outputs at face value. The act is also intrinsically repeatable — "recently" implies it can become a weekly habit without needing to be restated as such.

Action 1 — "Sit alongside your child's next Google homework search. Ask: 'What did it say? Do you think that's right?'" — is same-session, zero-barrier, and directly connected to the deck's core thesis that parental presence during AI use is the variable that changes outcomes. The questions are specific enough to be rehearsable and do not require the parent to know anything about how the AI works.

Action 2 (school AI policy email) remains a strong CTA move: the 96% statistic in the body copy creates urgency, the action is specific and takes less than five minutes, and it addresses the institutional knowledge gap that many parents in this room will have.

Action 3 (apply age limits) is directly actionable tonight for parents of younger children. The limits are stated on Slide 6 earlier in the deck, so this action recaps and converts prior information into a specific check the parent can do on their child's devices.

Action 4 — reviewing warning signs and opening a conversation — is directionally correct but remains the weakest of the five. The suggested entry point ("if your child uses AI for emotional support") is narrower than the parent may need: many parents will not know whether their child uses AI for emotional support, which is precisely the gap the warning signs slide addresses. A cleaner Action 4 entry would remove the conditional and replace it with "Review the five warning signs. If two or more apply to your child, open a conversation tonight." This does not require the parent to have a diagnosis before taking the action.

The five-action sequence as a whole is now strong. Every action is specific, every action is same-day or same-week, and no AI knowledge is required for any of them. The score is 9/10 rather than 10/10 because Action 4 still has a conditional that creates a decision point the parent may not be equipped to navigate at the moment of decision.

## Friction Audit

**Friction 1 — "This sounds like another thing I'm failing at."**
Parents arrive with an already-full anxiety load. The deck is well-calibrated to avoid shaming (the 49% anchor is non-punitive; the self-efficacy message on Slide 13 is clear), but the accumulation of documented harms across Slides 7–10 creates a compounding sense of risk that may tip into overwhelm for a parent who is already stretched. The risk is not that any single slide is too alarming — it is that four consecutive harm slides in sequence (scale of exposure, sycophancy mechanism, documented mental health harm, cognitive atrophy) produce a cumulative weight that requires active decompression before the action slide. The Slide 11 evidence asymmetry disclosure partially does this work, but it is calibrated for a sceptical or analytical parent rather than an overwhelmed one. A brief acknowledgment within the Slide 12 pivot — something like "Everything we have just described is the picture when the adult is absent. Here is what changes when you are present." — would help a parent who is processing the harms emotionally rather than analytically.

**Friction 2 — "My child won't engage with this."**
The deck does not address the conversational dynamics between parent and child. A parent who wants to act but fears that initiating the conversation will be received as surveillance or accusation may stall at the CTA stage even though the actions are specific. The Action 1 framing — sitting alongside during homework, asking a factual question about what the AI said — is the right approach because it positions the parent as curious rather than policing. But a parent who does not already have this conversational posture with their teenager may not see how to execute it without it feeling forced. This friction is not addressable at the slide level — it belongs in a follow-up resource or speaker notes — but presenters should be aware it is present.

**Friction 3 — "If the platforms are broken, what does my involvement actually do?"**
Slide 14 addresses this directly, and the "Use the lever you have" framing is better than v1.0.0's handling. But for a parent who is already sceptical of technology companies — and a significant portion of this audience will be — the acknowledgment that platforms "consistently fail to detect teen mental health crises" may land as "this is worse than I thought and also out of my control" rather than as a scoped, honest acknowledgment that increases credibility. The tension between the acknowledgment and the self-efficacy message is not fully resolved by the current framing. A single line that makes the sequencing of responsibility explicit — "the product problem is real and will take years; your involvement is what is available now" — would close this more cleanly.

**Friction 4 — "Is the 44% figure actually reliable?"**
The confidence caveat on the 44% figure has been moved to speaker notes only. For a mixed audience, this is defensible — the 44% figure is directionally plausible and consistent with the broader evidence pattern, and footnoting every confidence level in a live presentation disrupts persuasive momentum. However, a parent with a data background or a professional in the audience may ask where this number comes from. The speaker notes carry the caveat, which means a presenter who is prepared will handle the question; a presenter who is reading the notes for the first time may not. This is a presenter training issue as much as a deck issue, but it is worth flagging.

**Friction 5 — Screen time fatigue and moral panic calibration.**
Some parents in this room have been through the social media moral panic cycle and have calibrated their AI response against it. They will notice if the alarm level on this deck is higher than that cycle warranted. The deck's honest scope acknowledgment (Slide 14) and evidence asymmetry disclosure (Slide 11) are the correct defences against this friction — they signal that the deck is not making maximalist claims. The Florida case study is introduced carefully, framed as corroborating a pattern. These choices are correct for this audience and should be preserved.

## Recommendations

**1. Move Slide 5 (age thresholds) to follow Slide 10 (cognitive atrophy).**
This recommendation was made in v1.0.0 and was not implemented. The friction is still present and still reduces narrative clarity. Slides 7–10 build the emotional and evidentiary case for why age-appropriate limits matter; placing the limits at Slide 5 — before any of this material — means the thresholds arrive without warrant. The fix is the same as v1.0.0: move Slide 5 content to the position currently occupied by Slide 11 (or before it), after the harm evidence has done its work. This is the single highest-impact structural change available to v1.2.0.

**2. Add one line to the Slide 12 pivot that explicitly releases the accumulated harm weight.**
Four consecutive harm slides (7–10) create a cumulative emotional load that requires active decompression before the self-efficacy message of Slide 12 can land. The current Slide 12 display phrase — "Same product. Different parent. Different outcome." — is the right message, but it arrives without a reset. Adding a single transition line at the top of Slide 12 that explicitly marks the shift — something like "Everything above is the picture when the adult is absent. Here is what the same research shows when you are present." — would allow a parent who has been processing harm information emotionally to re-engage with agency before the positive case begins.

**3. Remove the conditional from Action 4 on Slide 16.**
Action 4 currently reads: "If your child uses AI for emotional support, review the warning signs from the previous slide and open a conversation tonight." Many parents will not know whether their child uses AI for emotional support — this is precisely the information gap the warning signs slide addresses. The conditional creates a decision point before the action: "Do I know if my child uses AI emotionally? I'm not sure. I'll skip this one." Replace with: "Review the five warning signs from the previous slide. If two or more apply to your child, open a conversation tonight." This removes the pre-condition and makes the action executable for the full audience.

**4. Add a provenance note to the 44% figure in a way accessible to the presenter.**
The confidence caveat for the 44% figure is in speaker notes only, which is the right call for slide real estate. However, the speaker notes should explicitly state the provenance chain (reported via practitioner newsletter, sourced to Common Sense Media) and give the presenter a one-sentence response for a direct question from the audience. Currently the speaker notes do not include this. This is a low-effort fix that reduces presenter exposure in a mixed-knowledge audience.

**5. Consider a one-line acknowledgment of the conversational challenge within or adjacent to Action 4.**
The deck does not address the dynamics of initiating the conversation with a teenager who may resist. Action 1 handles this well by framing the entry as curiosity rather than policing (sitting alongside during homework). A single line at the end of Action 4 — or as a sixth action — that names the conversational posture explicitly would be useful: "Approach it as a question, not a conversation you need to win. 'I saw something about AI and kids — can you show me how you use it?' is enough to start." This is optional but would address Friction 2 directly at the moment the parent needs it most.
