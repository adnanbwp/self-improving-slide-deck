# Persuasion Scorecard — v1.1.0 (Teachers)

**Score:** 8.2/10
**Auditor:** Aria
**Audience:** Teachers
**Date:** 2026-05-26
**CTA:** This week, ask your students to show you what they use AI for in your class — listen without reacting, then run one lesson where they must find something the AI got wrong about your subject area.

---

## Audience Model

**Cognitive priors.** Teachers enter this deck carrying a professional identity built around being the knowledge authority and the responsible adult in the room. AI challenges both simultaneously. Most teachers in this deck's likely audience are aware that students use AI and have absorbed the "AI cheating" discourse — some have been given ban policies to enforce with no accompanying guidance. A meaningful portion are operating in institutional compliance mode: they enforce a rule they are not sure is right, and they are looking for a defensible alternative framed in evidence. A smaller group is already integrating AI into their classrooms and needs validation. A smaller group still is deeply skeptical of AI in any educational context and will read every data point as ammunition for restriction rather than engagement.

**Values.** Student wellbeing and genuine learning over credentialing and output metrics. Professional autonomy — particularly resentment of top-down mandates from administrators who are equally unprepared. Equity: in schools serving lower-income communities, this is a live, morally weighted concern, not an abstract policy consideration. Competence: teachers do not want to be put in front of a class unable to field basic questions about the tools their students are already using.

**Anxieties.** Three clusters are distinct and worth mapping separately. (1) Replacement anxiety: AI-as-better-teacher is an unspoken but real fear, especially among teachers who identify strongly with knowledge authority. (2) Inadequacy anxiety: being asked to guide students through a domain they were never trained in. The 11% training figure names this anxiety with precision and then immediately reframes it as a systemic failure — this is the deck's most psychologically sophisticated move. (3) Liability and safeguarding: if a student is harmed through AI use the teacher was aware of and did not intervene, who bears responsibility? The warning signs slide (Slide 15) addresses the observable behavioral layer; the liability question itself is not answered in this deck and remains a live anxiety in safeguarding-conscious school systems.

**JTBD.** Teachers need: a defensible position they can explain to students, parents, and administrators simultaneously; at least one concrete classroom action they can implement this week without new curriculum or training; and permission to engage with AI rather than ban it, sourced in evidence they can cite to peers and leadership.

**Current belief state.** The distribution is wide. Some teachers reading this deck have already moved toward guided integration and primarily need validation and tools. A significant group is in reluctant compliance with ban policies — they suspect the ban is wrong but have not been given evidence or institutional cover to do otherwise. A smaller group enters in defensive mode: they read "AI is everywhere" as a threat argument rather than a practical premise. The v1.1.0 design changes — particularly the Slide 2 display phrase and the restructured Slide 7 — make a meaningful difference to how this last group navigates the opening third of the deck.

## Activation Gap

Moderate, narrowed from v1.0.0. Teachers are closer to acceptance than parents, but they carry a structural constraint parents do not: institutional policy. A teacher persuaded by this deck may still be unable to act unilaterally if a district ban policy is in place. v1.1.0 addresses this more directly than v1.0.0: Action 2 on Slide 16 ("Find out your school's AI policy — from the inside. Read it, identify gaps, and raise them. If there is no policy, that is the gap to raise.") and Action 5 ("Request professional development on harmful AI use") both give teachers specific language for navigating the institutional constraint. The gap between "I agree" and "I act" remains real for teachers in restrictive schools, but the deck now gives them two levers inside that constraint rather than one. The gap is partially closed; it is not eliminated.

## Summary

The v1.1.0 teachers deck is a materially stronger version than v1.0.0, and the score reflects that. The two changes with the most persuasive impact are the restructuring of Slide 7 — which removes the friction-generating identity threat that was Friction Point 1 in the prior scorecard — and the reorientation of the Slide 16 CTA, which is now more sequenced, more teachable, and more actionable. The display phrases in Slides 2, 5, 8, 10, 12, and 14 lift the emotional resonance of the middle section of the deck. The stat-callout components on Slides 7, 9, and 13 give quantitative anchors visual prominence without requiring text-heavy explanation. The score does not reach 8.5 because two significant friction points remain from v1.0.0 — the Limb A/B/C header labels and the unresolved source confidence on key statistics — and one new friction point has been introduced by the v1.1.0 Slide 7 redesign (explained in the friction audit below). The CTA is the strongest version this deck has produced for teachers.

## Emotional Resonance

**Score: 8/10**

**What works.** The Slide 2 display phrase — "Remove the teacher. Remove the risk. Wrong." — is the single most effective opening move the teachers variant has made across either version. It does three things simultaneously: it names the false assumption without requiring teachers to admit they hold it, it positions teachers as the critical variable before any evidence has been presented, and it does so with a rhetorical punch that signals the deck will not waste their time. Teachers who have felt sidelined by the AI debate — treated as obstacles rather than professional guides — will feel seen by this slide before Slide 3 begins.

The Slide 13 restructuring is the second-strongest emotional move. Putting 11% as the visual centrepiece — with the headline "Only 11% of teachers received training on how to respond to harmful AI use — this deck is for the other 89%" — is a direct acknowledgment that the deck's audience is operating under conditions of institutional neglect. The reframe from "you don't know enough" to "you were never given the tools, and that is the system's failure, not yours" is the same move v1.0.0 made, but the visual prominence now matches the rhetorical weight. Teachers carrying quiet shame about their AI literacy gap get both the reassurance and the evidence for it in the same visual moment.

Slide 12's display phrase — "Same product. Different teacher. Different outcome." — is the deck's most compressed statement of the positive argument. It places teachers as the decisive variable in the outcome equation, which is motivating rather than burdensome when it follows the evidence on Slides 8–11. Slide 5's "The ban protects the students least at risk" carries genuine moral force for teachers in high-equity-consciousness schools.

The Slide 14 display phrase "Use the lever you have" works for a different emotional register: it gives teachers permission to act within limits, rather than asking them to solve a problem that is not theirs to solve alone. This is particularly resonant for the teacher who has been told AI harms are real but has been given no route to act.

**What falls short.** Slide 10's "Better output. Less thinking." is the weakest display phrase in the deck. It is accurate, and the support line explains it competently, but as a visual centrepiece it reads as a general AI-critique headline rather than a teacher-specific argument. Teachers already know students are using AI to produce better-looking work — the emotional activation opportunity here is not in naming the pattern but in naming what it does to the teacher's ability to assess real learning. A slight reframe toward the teacher's experience ("You can see it in the room. The work looks fine. The thinking isn't there.") would have more activation energy for this audience.

Slide 9's case example — the 14-year-old in Florida — lands with appropriate gravity, but the body text surrounding it reads slightly procedural ("corroborates a documented pattern, not an isolated incident"). For a teacher audience that may include safeguarding leads, the clinical framing is arguably correct. For a general teacher audience, however, the emotional register is cooler than the subject warrants. The stat-callout "1 in 8" works harder here than the body text.

## Narrative Clarity

**Score: 8/10**

The three-limb structure remains clean and accessible. The argument progression — reality before argument, harm before recommendation, scope acknowledgment before CTA — is sound and well-executed. The Slide 7 restructuring is a genuine improvement in narrative sequencing: the prior version asked teachers to absorb a professional identity threat midway through Limb B before they had been offered any positive argument. The v1.1.0 version leads with population scale ("58% of students would rather ask a chatbot than their teacher") and frames it as a description of current reality rather than a comparative judgment on teachers. The closing line on Slide 7 — "Your students are already in the deep end. The question is whether you are in the room when it matters." — converts the statistic from threat to urgency, which is the right move for this audience.

Slide 4's reframe is also effective in narrative terms. "The classroom is the only place where a professional can be in the room when it happens" converts the impossibility argument from "you cannot win, so stop trying" into "here is the unique value of your professional presence." The logical form of the argument is the same; the activation it generates is different.

The Slide 11 evidence asymmetry slide continues to do important work. Naming honestly that the two kinds of harm are not empirically equivalent prevents the deck from appearing to oversimplify, and for a teacher audience that may include educators with research backgrounds, this credibility-building slide pays dividends in the CTA section.

Minor clarity issue from v1.0.0 that remains open: the "Limb A", "Limb B", "Limb C" internal labels in the slide-header spans are still present in v1.1.0. Labels such as "Limb A — Over-Protection", "Limb B — Hands-Off", and "Limb C — The Guided Path" appear in the deck's slide headers. Teachers will encounter these without context for what "Limb" means as a structural term. In a live presentation context where these header spans appear at the top of each slide, this remains a presentation hygiene issue. It is not a structural problem — the argument is clear — but it introduces unnecessary jargon that could prompt a question or break concentration at the wrong moment.

## CTA Assessment

**Score: 8.5/10**

**Primary CTA identified:** Ask your students to show you what they use AI for in your class — listen without reacting, then run one lesson where they must find something the AI got wrong about your subject area.

**What v1.1.0 gets right that v1.0.0 did not.** The sequencing of actions on Slide 16 is now more deliberate and more teachable. Action 1 — "Ask your students to show you what they use AI for in this class. Listen. Don't react. You need to know before you can guide." — is the correct first action for a teacher who has not yet engaged with AI in their classroom. It asks nothing technically demanding. It requires no AI expertise. It uses an existing teacher skill (listening and observational assessment) in a new context. And it is explicitly framed as an information-gathering step rather than a policy enforcement step, which reduces the risk of defensive student responses.

Action 3 — "Run one lesson where students must find something the AI got wrong. Existing Socratic questioning skills. New context." — is a compressed but effective description of what is arguably the highest-leverage classroom practice available to teachers right now. The parenthetical framing ("existing skills, new context") directly addresses the professional inadequacy anxiety without dwelling on it.

Action 5 — "Request professional development on harmful AI use. The research says only 11% of teachers have had it. That is the school's responsibility to fix." — closes the institutional loop. A teacher who reaches Action 5 having internalized the 11% figure from Slide 13 is now being told they have both the evidence base and the moral standing to make that ask. This is a meaningful improvement on v1.0.0's equivalent action.

**What still limits CTA strength.** The five-action list remains a flat-priority numbered list. Actions 1, 2, and 3 are classroom-level behaviors; Action 4 is a safeguarding escalation behavior; Action 5 is an institutional advocacy behavior. These have different cadences, different audiences, and different risk profiles. A teacher reading the list quickly — which is how most teachers consume slide content during a live presentation — may not distinguish between "do this in your next lesson" and "submit a written PD request to HR." The list treats them as structurally equivalent, which understates both the simplicity of Actions 1 and 3 and the significance of Action 5.

Recommendation 2 from v1.0.0 — elevating the framing sentence about not needing to be an AI expert to CTA closing prominence — has not been implemented in v1.1.0. The equivalent sentence has migrated into the body text of Slide 13. It is doing more work there than in the secondary layout element from v1.0.0, but it is still not functioning as the emotional landing before the references. The deck's strongest sentence for teachers is still buried in slide body text rather than used as a closing headline.

There is still no "share with one colleague" or "bring this to your department" secondary CTA — the natural viral loop for a teacher audience remains inactive.

## Friction Audit

**Friction point 1 — Slide 7: 58% statistic as a residual threat signal.**
In v1.0.0, the opening line ("AI is a better knowledge deliverer than any human teacher") was Friction Point 1. That line is gone, and the restructuring is a genuine improvement. However, the 58% statistic now placed in the Slide 7 body text — "58% of students would rather ask a chatbot than their teacher when confused in class" — creates a new, lower-level friction for the same segment of teachers who were threatened by the prior framing. The reframe that follows ("Your students are already in the deep end. The question is whether you are in the room when it matters.") does good work. But the 58% figure is absorbed before the reframe sentence arrives. In v1.0.0, this statistic sat in the margin notes of Slide 6. Its promotion into Slide 7 body text is an overall improvement — it is no longer buried — but for teachers whose identity is anchored in being their students' first resource, this friction is reduced, not eliminated.

**Friction point 2 — Institutional authority displacement (unchanged from v1.0.0).**
Actions 2 and 5 on Slide 16 instruct teachers to surface gaps in institutional policy and request professional development. For teachers in schools with active ban policies, or in systems with slow or closed PD processes, these actions read as instruction to challenge their administration. The deck gives them stronger evidence for making the ask (the 11% figure is now prominent), but it does not acknowledge that a teacher in a restrictive institutional environment may not feel they have the standing or the safety to push back, and that asking has limits. This is the gap between intellectual agreement and behavioral change for a meaningful portion of the teacher audience.

**Friction point 3 — Source confidence (partially unresolved from v1.0.0).**
The decisions log records that the 11%, 49%, and 96% figures were Medium-High confidence statistics sourced through a practitioner newsletter, pending verification against primary reports. The 11% figure (CDT, 2025) is now the visual centrepiece of Slide 13 and the anchor for Action 5. The references slide cites "CDT, 2025" as the source, which is a credible primary source, and this is recoverable if a teacher checks it. But the decisions log open item — verify before live presentation — has not been resolved or noted as resolved in v1.1.0. The persuasion risk is unchanged from v1.0.0: credentialed teachers who notice this gap may soften their confidence in the deck's quantitative anchors.

**Friction point 4 — Limb A/B/C header labels (unchanged from v1.0.0).**
The structural labels remain visible in slide headers. In a live presentation, a teacher who is distracted by "Limb B — Hands-Off" appearing above a slide about documented mental health harm will lose the thread of the argument. This is minor but preventable.

**Friction point 5 — Safeguarding referral pathway named but not defined.**
Slide 15 instructs teachers to "activate your school's safeguarding or counseling referral pathway" if two or more warning signs appear. The speaker notes elaborate helpfully. But for a teacher in an audience without access to the notes — or in a school system where the referral pathway is unclear, unavailable, or slow — this instruction creates an anxiety it does not resolve. "Know your school's referral protocol before you see the signs" is in the speaker notes only. This is a friction point for teachers in under-resourced or under-organized schools, and it costs one sentence on the visible slide to resolve.

## Recommendations

**1. Strip the Limb A/B/C labels from slide headers.**
Replace with the descriptive section labels that already exist as `.section-label` spans in the deck — "Over-protection", "Hands-off", "The guided path" are already there. The "Limb" terminology can be retired from the visible presentation layer. This is a two-minute edit with no argument impact and directly resolves Friction Point 4.

**2. Differentiate CTA action weight visually on Slide 16.**
Actions 1, 2, and 3 are this-week classroom behaviors; Action 4 is a safeguarding escalation; Action 5 is an institutional advocacy action with a different timeline and addressee. Group or visually distinguish these tiers — a horizontal divider between Action 3 and Action 4 would signal the shift in kind without restructuring the content. This is the single most critical framing adjustment needed in v1.1.0.

**3. Elevate the "adult in the room" framing to a closing headline.**
The sentence "You do not need to become an AI expert. Asking questions, modeling doubt, requiring students to evaluate outputs — you already do all of this. Point it at AI." is the deck's strongest statement of the teacher's role. It currently lives in Slide 13 body text. It should be the last text a teacher reads before the references — either as a closing display phrase on Slide 14 or as a stand-alone beat between Slide 16 and Slide 17. This is Rec 2 from v1.0.0, still not implemented, and still the highest-value single framing adjustment the deck has not yet made.

**4. Move the safeguarding bridge sentence from speaker notes to the visible Slide 15.**
"Know your school's referral protocol before you see the signs" is currently in the notes only. One support line beneath the warning-signs list that says this directly — and names that this is a pre-action, not a crisis response — converts Friction Point 5 from anxiety-generating to action-generating.

**5. Add a secondary peer-sharing CTA to Slide 16.**
A single sentence at the bottom of Slide 16 — "Share this with one colleague who teaches the same year group" — activates the professional viral loop teachers use for curriculum material. It costs nothing and has meaningful reach implications.

**6. Confirm and flag source verification status in the references slide.**
Once the 11%, 49%, and 96% figures have been verified against CDT and Common Sense Media primary reports, note this in the references slide. The open item in the decisions log should be closed before the deck is used in a live presentation context.
