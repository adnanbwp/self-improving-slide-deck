# Persuasion Scorecard — v1.0.0 (Parents)

**Score:** 7/10
**Auditor:** Aria
**Audience:** Parents
**Date:** 2026-05-26
**CTA:** Tonight, during homework, sit alongside your child when they use Google or any AI tool and ask three questions out loud: "What did it say? Do you think that's right? How would we check?" Then this week, find out whether your child's school has an AI policy, and apply the age thresholds — no AI companion apps for under 18.

---

## Summary

The parents variant is the strongest audience-specific version of this deck produced in v1.0.0. The emotional framing is well-calibrated — it does not shame parents, it names the gap they are actually in, and it gives them a self-efficacy path that requires no technical background. The CTA on slide 11 is specific and same-day, which is rare for this genre. The deck loses points primarily because one structural sequencing choice (guidance before emotional case) and one motivational dip (slide 10 immediately before the action slide) reduce the persuasive momentum a parent needs to act tonight rather than "at some point."

---

## Emotional Resonance

**Score: 8/10**

The deck's emotional architecture is its strongest asset. The non-shaming 49% anchor on slide 2 is well-placed — it normalises the parent's current position before asking them to change it, which removes defensiveness. Slide 7 ("am I the relationship in my child's life that still matters more?") is the emotional peak of the deck and will land hard for a parent who feels their child is increasingly oriented toward their phone. The Character.AI case on slide 6 is deployed carefully — framed as corroborating a pattern, not as typical — which is the right calibration for a parent audience that will otherwise dismiss it as an edge case.

One flat spot reduces the score. Slide 10 (honest scope) correctly acknowledges that parental guidance is not a complete solution and that platform and policy failures are real. This is intellectually honest and argumentatively necessary. But it arrives immediately before the CTA slide and creates a motivational dip at exactly the moment when emotional momentum is most needed. The slide's own framing ("knowing this is not discouraging") signals awareness of this risk, but naming the problem is not the same as solving it. A parent who internalises "major AI platforms are not built with child safety as the primary design constraint" at slide 10 may feel outgunned rather than empowered walking into slide 11.

The "co-pilot not gatekeeper" reframe on slide 9 resolves the most common parental anxiety about this topic — "I am not technical enough to help" — and does so before the action slide. This is structurally correct and emotionally well-timed.

## Narrative Clarity

**Score: 7/10**

The argument is accessible to a non-expert parent. There is no unexplained jargon. The slide-by-slide sequence — problem exists now, both wrong postures make the same mistake, here is what is already in your home, here are the documented risks, here are warning signs, here is the right posture, here is what to do — is followable without any AI background. The "gatekeeper vs. co-pilot" contrast on slide 9 is the kind of plain-language reframe that this audience needs and will remember.

One sequencing issue introduces friction. Slide 5 (age-appropriate thresholds) appears before slide 6 (documented harms). A parent receives the specific guidance — "no AI companion apps under 18," "close supervision ages 6 to 12" — before the deck has made the full emotional case for why these limits matter. For a parent who is skeptical or disengaged at this stage, the guidance may feel prescriptive rather than earned. The natural reading order for this audience is: here is what is already happening (slides 3, 4), here is why it matters (slide 6), here is what the product is designed to do (slide 7), here is what to watch for (slide 8), here are the specific limits that are evidence-based (slide 5), here is the right posture (slide 9). Moving slide 5 to follow slide 8 would allow the emotional case to do its work before the prescriptive guidance arrives.

The warning signs slide (slide 8) is a clarity asset. Five behavioral signals from a named clinical source, with a threshold for action (two or three together), is exactly the format a parent can use immediately. The addition about social media restrictions and potential AI substitution is well-caveated and adds specificity without overstating the evidence.

## CTA Assessment

**Score: 7/10**

The CTA on slide 11 is above average for this genre. "Tonight, during homework" is a same-day, zero-barrier action. The three questions ("What did it say? Do you think that's right? How would we check?") are specific enough to be rehearsable and require no AI knowledge to deploy. Action 3 ("check what apps your child has on their phone or tablet") is concrete and immediately doable. The framing box — "the goal is not to become an AI expert, the goal is to be the relationship in your child's life that matters more than the one they have with their phone" — lands the emotional close after the action list, which is the correct order.

Two weaknesses prevent a higher score. First, Action 4 ("start a direct conversation... what do you like about it?") is directionally useful but the suggested script is open-ended enough that a parent who is anxious about the topic may not know how to navigate the conversation after the opening line. A single follow-up anchor would sharpen this. Second, Action 5 ("make AI a topic in your house... ask regularly, stay curious") is a disposition rather than an act. "Stay curious" is not a CTA. A specific replacement — for example, "put a monthly 10-minute AI check-in on your calendar" or "ask one AI question at dinner every Friday" — would convert this from a platitude into an anchor.

The 96% school-policy statistic on Action 2 is well-deployed as motivation for a specific action (call the school), which is a textbook CTA move: give the parent a number that creates urgency, then give them the exact step to take.

## Recommendations

**1. Move slide 5 (age thresholds) to follow slide 8 (warning signs).**
The current sequence puts prescriptive guidance before the emotional case that earns it. A parent who is in a skeptical or hands-off posture at slide 5 will receive the age thresholds without the motivational groundwork to act on them. Placing slide 5 after slide 8 allows the documented harms (slide 6), the engineered attachment mechanism (slide 7), and the warning signs (slide 8) to build the case first. The thresholds then arrive as a natural response to a concern the parent already feels, rather than as instructions they are not yet ready to follow.

**2. Resequence slides 10 and 11 or add a bridging line at the end of slide 10.**
The honest scope acknowledgment on slide 10 is intellectually necessary but creates a motivational dip immediately before the CTA. Two options: (a) move the scope acknowledgment to a brief addendum or footnote within slide 11 itself, framed as "this is what you control, within a larger problem that needs industry and policy action too," or (b) end slide 10 with a single energising line that pivots toward the parent's agency — for example: "Your guidance is the most important lever you personally control. Here is how to use it." The current closing line ("knowing this is not discouraging") is doing this work but not landing it.

**3. Replace Action 5 on slide 11 with a specific, scheduled act.**
"Stay curious" is not a CTA. Replace with something a parent can calendar or anchor to an existing routine — for example: "Once a week, ask your child one question about something they asked AI that week. It takes two minutes and it keeps the conversation open." The test for an action item on the CTA slide is: can a parent close this deck and do it? "Stay curious" fails that test. A specific weekly prompt passes it.
