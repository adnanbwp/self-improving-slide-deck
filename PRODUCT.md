# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

The presenter is an independent researcher and thinker projecting personal intellectual credibility — no institution to borrow authority from. Audiences vary by deck (parents, teachers, professionals), but the design system must serve the presenter's authority across all of them. The audience sees one slide at a time in a darkened room; the presenter's voice carries the depth.

## Product Purpose

A system for producing argument-led slide decks that earn trust without institutional backing. The slide's job is to hold the room on the presenter — not to substitute for them. Success: the audience trusts the argument before the presenter has finished the sentence on screen.

The register defaults to brand but can be overridden per deck in `pov.md`. Individual decks are marketing artefacts; this system is their production infrastructure.

## Brand Personality

Credible, provocative, measured.

Voice: states the position no one else is willing to say plainly, then earns it with evidence. Does not overstate. Trusts the audience to handle a hard claim without scaffolding. Measured ≠ timid.

## Anti-references

- **Silicon Valley startup pitch**: dark navy, big funding stats, "10x" rhetoric, logo rows, Pitch.com aesthetic
- **Corporate PowerPoint**: blue gradient headers, bullet trees, clip art, Microsoft template feel
- **Academic lecture slides**: dense text, Times New Roman, no visual hierarchy, pre-reading density
- **Canva / feel-good social content**: pastel, stock photos of hands and coffee, optimism without substance

The design must not be verbose. Slides are performance artefacts for a live presenter — not documents sent for pre-reading. Every slide is an engaging anchor that focuses the room on what the presenter is saying.

## Design Principles

1. **One thing per slide.** A single dominant focal point — a stat, a claim, an image. The rest is the presenter's voice.
2. **Silence is a visual choice.** Whitespace earns attention; crowding loses it. Generous space signals confidence.
3. **Typography IS the visual.** When there is no chart or image, the words themselves must command the room at display size. Type is not decoration — it is the argument.
4. **Commit to position.** The design takes a stance. Measured does not mean safe or neutral. A beige slide is a coward's slide.
5. **The presenter is the product.** The slide is backdrop, not document. Design serves the speaker's credibility, not the reader's comprehension.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. High contrast text required on all backgrounds. Reveal.js transitions should be subtle or disabled — no motion that distracts from the presenter. No autoplay, no timed transitions.
