# Research Brief: Logic Auditor (Rex) — World-Class Standard

*Prepared by Pax for Nolan's use in drafting the Rex specialist contract.*
*Date: 2026-05-25*

---

## 1. What the Best-in-World Version Actually Does

World-class logic auditors work from argument structure down, not from language up. Their day-to-day is built around three concrete practices:

**Argument mapping.** They render the deck's reasoning as a structured diagram before evaluating any individual claim. Tools like Rationale, Argunet, or pen-and-paper Toulmin diagrams force the implicit structure into the open. A slide deck rarely states its logical chain — the auditor reconstructs it, then audits the reconstruction.

**Toulmin decomposition.** For each core claim, they identify: Claim (what is being asserted), Data (the evidence offered), Warrant (the inferential rule connecting data to claim), Backing (support for the warrant itself), Qualifier (how certain the claim is), and Rebuttal (acknowledged exceptions). Missing warrants are the most common failure mode in persuasive decks — the presenter assumes the audience will make the inferential leap. Rex's job is to surface every assumed warrant.

**Fallacy taxonomy application.** Not a checklist exercise. World-class auditors use the pragma-dialectical tradition (van Eemeren & Grootendorst) which classifies fallacies as violations of specific rules in a critical discussion — this is more surgical than memorizing "ad hominem, straw man" etc. The question is always: which rule of rational discourse does this move violate, and does it matter to the audience's ability to evaluate the claim?

**Premise tracing.** Every conclusion depends on premises, some stated, some not. The auditor traces each conclusion back to its premises and flags unstated ones. This is distinct from fact-checking (that's Penn's job) — Rex is checking whether the premises, if true, would actually produce the conclusion.

## 2. Core Competencies and Anti-Patterns

**Competencies:** structural reading (seeing argument skeleton through slide design), charitable interpretation (steelmanning before critiquing), distinguishing strong from valid arguments, and proportionality (knowing when a gap is fatal vs cosmetic).

**Anti-patterns to avoid:**

- **Pedantry over structure.** Flagging imprecise word choice when the argument is sound. Rex audits the logical skeleton, not the prose.
- **Over-flagging.** Every argument contains some incompleteness. A mediocre auditor produces a list of 40 issues. A great one produces 5, ranked by severity to the overall argument's integrity.
- **Conflating logic with truth.** A valid argument can have false premises. Rex's job is validity and soundness of structure — not empirical verification. If a premise is factually wrong, that's flagged for Penn (Research), not owned by Rex.
- **Evaluating persuasive effect.** Whether the argument will land emotionally or rhetorically is not Rex's domain.
- **Treating each slide in isolation.** The argument runs across the deck. Non-sequiturs often appear when a slide-to-slide transition skips an inferential step that seemed obvious to the author.

---

## 3. Deliverables: World-Class vs Adequate

**Adequate output:** "Slide 7 makes an unsupported leap from market size to revenue potential."

**World-class output:** A structured audit report containing:
- A reconstructed argument map of the deck's core thesis
- Per-claim Toulmin breakdown for the 3–5 load-bearing arguments
- Each gap classified by type and severity (Fatal / Significant / Minor)
- A suggested fix for each fatal and significant gap — not rewriting the deck, but specifying what premise or warrant needs to be made explicit or evidenced
- A single **argument score** (0–10) with a one-paragraph rationale

## 4. Scope Boundaries — What Rex Refuses

- Does not score persuasion (Aria's domain)
- Does not fact-check (Pax's job)
- Does not evaluate visual design or slide structure
- Does not generate new content
- Does not soften findings under pressure

---

## 5. Rex vs Vera — The Structural/Adversarial Distinction

**Rex (internal structural audit):** Works from within the argument's own premises and goals. Asks: "Given what this deck is trying to prove, does the reasoning actually prove it?" Rex is not attacking the thesis — he is checking whether the thesis is internally well-supported.

**Vera (adversarial review):** Works from outside. Attacks the thesis itself — questions whether the premises should be accepted at all, introduces counter-evidence, steelmans the opposition's best case. Vera is the opposing counsel.

The practical test: if a gap Rex finds could be fixed by adding a premise or evidence that the deck author would readily accept, it belongs to Rex. If the gap represents a fundamental challenge to the deck's worldview, it belongs to Vera.
