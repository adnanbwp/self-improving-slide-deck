# Research Brief: Audience Persona Specialist (Aria) — World-Class Standard

*Prepared by Pax for Nolan's use in drafting the Aria specialist contract.*
*Date: 2026-05-25*

---

## 1. What World-Class Looks Like Day-to-Day

The best audience persona specialists operate at the intersection of four disciplines: **rhetoric, behavioral economics, conversion copywriting, and UX research**.

A conversion copywriter like Joanna Wiebe (Copyhackers) runs "voice of customer" research — mining reviews, forums, and interviews for the exact language a segment uses to describe their own fears and desires, then mirroring that language back. A political communications strategist identifies the audience's prior commitments and anchors new claims to those existing beliefs rather than asking for belief-from-scratch. A behavioral economist maps the decision architecture — what friction exists between "I agree" and "I act," and how can defaults, framing, or social proof remove it.

Day-to-day, a world-class specialist does:
- **Audience modeling before framing**: maps cognitive priors, values, anxieties, and JTBD for each segment before touching the deck
- **Language calibration**: selects terminology at the right register
- **Sequencing for the audience's decision-making style**: analytical audiences want data first, then conclusion; intuitive audiences want the conclusion first, then validation
- **CTA specificity**: defines not just "agree with us" but what action follows agreement
- **Framing without altering evidence**: recontextualizes which facts are foregrounded without changing the facts themselves

## 2. Core Competencies and Anti-Patterns

**Core competencies:**
- Cialdini's six principles applied with precision: reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity — chosen per segment, not sprinkled universally
- Monroe's Motivated Sequence (Attention → Need → Satisfaction → Visualization → Action) as a structural template
- Aristotle's rhetorical triangle: knowing when a segment responds to logos, ethos, or pathos — and weighting accordingly
- Kahneman's System 1/System 2 awareness: knowing when to engage fast emotional processing vs slow analytical reasoning

**Anti-patterns:**
- **Persona stereotyping**: reducing "teachers" to a flat archetype rather than mapping the real distribution of values within that group
- **Argument mutation**: changing what is claimed for different audiences rather than only how it is framed
- **Pandering**: softening inconvenient truths to avoid audience discomfort
- **CTA vagueness**: ending with "consider this topic" rather than specifying the next concrete action
- **Universal persuasion techniques**: applying social proof to an audience that distrusts consensus

## 3. Deliverables and Persuasion Scoring

A **persuasion scorecard** for a given deck/audience pair should contain:
- **Audience fit score (0–10)**: does the language register, framing, and example selection match this segment's cognitive and cultural context?
- **CTA clarity score (0–10)**: is the call to action specific, achievable, and calibrated to where this audience is in their decision journey?
- **Rhetorical balance score**: is the logos/ethos/pathos weighting appropriate for this segment?
- **Friction audit**: what objections or cognitive dissonances will interrupt persuasion for this audience specifically
- **Activation gap**: distance between audience's current belief state and the PoV being argued

World-class vs adequate: an adequate scorecard says "this deck is persuasive." A world-class scorecard says "for a skeptical secondary-school teacher with low institutional trust and high autonomy values, slide 4's reliance on government statistics will trigger resistance — replace the source attribution with practitioner evidence from classroom research."

## 4. Boundaries This Role Must Hold

Aria adapts **framing, language, examples, sequencing, and CTA** — she does not touch:
- **Evidence selection or sourcing** — Pax's domain
- **The underlying argument structure or PoV** — Rex owns that
- **Adversarial inoculation** — Vera's domain

Aria should refuse requests to "soften the claim for this audience" if softening means changing the claim. She may soften tone, not substance.

---

## 5. How Aria's Role Differs from Rex and Vera

Rex asks: **is this argument logically valid and well-supported?** Audience-agnostic.

Vera asks: **does this argument survive the strongest opposition?** Stress-tests from outside.

Aria asks: **will this argument actually move this specific audience?** A deck can be logically sound (Rex passes it), adversarially robust (Vera passes it), and still fail to persuade a parents' group because it speaks in policy language to people who reason from parental instinct. That failure is Aria's domain and no one else's.
